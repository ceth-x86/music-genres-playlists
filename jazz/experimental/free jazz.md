# Free Jazz

## 1960–1966: Foundations & first wave

**Ornette Coleman — Free Jazz (Free Jazz: A Collective Improvisation, 1961)**
The title piece that effectively gave the movement its name: an extended, collective improvisation that breaks with fixed chorus forms while keeping jazz's conversational push-and-pull. It's the blueprint for "group mind" free jazz, where texture and spontaneous structure replace chord changes as the main organizing force.

**Jimmy Giuffre 3 — The Five Ways (Free Fall, 1963)**
On "The Five Ways", Jimmy Giuffre 3 offers a early-'60s language-break in a small-group setting, leaning into harmonic looseness that privileges timbre and gesture. It's a reference point for how free jazz builds form in real time.

**Prince Lasha Quintet (feat. Sonny Simmons) — Congo Call (The Cry!, 1963)**
On "Congo Call", Prince Lasha Quintet (feat. Sonny Simmons) offers a foundational rupture in a small-group setting, leaning into rhythmic freedom that keeps the music in constant motion. It remains a touchstone for later improvisers working in open form.

**New York Art Quartet — Sweet (New York Art Quartet, 1965)**
On "Sweet", New York Art Quartet offers a early-'60s language-break in a leader-led setting, leaning into extended techniques and register-stretching phrasing. It distills the style's priorities—sound, momentum, and interaction—over changes-and-choruses.

**Giuseppi Logan Quartet — Dance of Satan (The Giuseppi Logan Quartet, 1965)**
On "Dance of Satan", New York Giuseppi Logan Quartet offers a first-wave statement in a small-group setting, leaning into collective improvisation with elastic time. It captures the genre's 'textbook' balance of freedom and collective coherence.

**Albert Ayler Trio — Ghosts: First Variation (Spiritual Unity, 1965)**
Ayler's singable theme becomes a launching pad for raw, ecstatic improvisation—wide vibrato, cries, and multiphonics that embody free jazz's emotional directness. It's a defining statement of the "folk melody + freedom" approach that shaped countless later players.

**François Tusques — La Tour Saint-Jacques (Free Jazz, 1965)**

**Don Cherry — Complete Communion (Complete Communion, 1966)**
Cherry's suite-like writing blurs head/solo boundaries, using recurring themes as signposts inside free-flowing improvisation. It shows how free jazz could be structurally coherent without returning to standard changes or fixed meters.

**John Coltrane — Ascension (Ascension, 1966)**
A monumental large-ensemble performance that turns the jazz soloist's "feature" into a communal ritual of sound—dense, eruptive, and structurally liberated. Its intensity and scale made it a touchstone for the mid-'60s avant-garde and the later "energy music" tradition.

**Cecil Taylor — Steps (Unit Structures, 1966)**
Taylor treats the piano as a full percussion orchestra, building architecture from clustered attacks, shifting densities, and elastic time. It's a textbook example of free jazz's break from song-form into long-form, kinetic improvisational design.

## 1967–1969: Fire Music & international breakout

**Bill Dixon — Metamorphosis 1962–1966 (Intents and Purposes, 1967)**
Dixon's sound-world favors contour and color over harmonic direction, making space and pacing as important as notes. The piece exemplifies free jazz as modernist orchestration—improvisation shaped by timbral strategy rather than chord progressions.

**Gato Barbieri — In Search of the Mystery (In Search of the Mystery, 1967)**
