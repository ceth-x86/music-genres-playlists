# Noise Electronic

## Japanoise & East Asian core (1980s–2000s)

**Hijokaidan — Bad Character, But Great Sounds (Viva Angel, 1984)**
A landmark example of Osaka's early noise scene, capturing the raw, performance-derived chaos that became central to Japanese harsh noise. The track's dense feedback and uncontrolled dynamics exemplify noise as the primary musical material.

**Solmania — Flood (Metamorphor Chorus, 1991)**
Solmania's early-90s recordings embody the tactile, analog brutality of Japanese harsh noise. 'Flood' emphasizes thick saturation and unstable feedback layers over rhythm or harmony.

**Aube — Flash-Point A (Flash-Point, 1993)**
Aube's concept-driven approach turns a single sound-source idea into a focused noise architecture. The piece shows how Noise Electronic can be both extreme and methodical in its use of texture and dynamics.

**M.S.B.R. — Structured Suicide (Structured Suicide, 1993)**
MSBR's material is a touchstone for 'pure' harsh noise: loud, continuous, and physically oppressive without relying on industrial rhythm. The title piece became emblematic of early-90s Japanese noise extremity.

**Incapacitants — Apoptosis (As Loud As Possible, 1995)**
A canonical Incapacitants cut: maximal volume, continuous feedback, and relentless density with minimal 'composition' in the conventional sense. It demonstrates the genre's core principle—noise as total immersion.

**Pain Jerk — Vividsector (Trashware, 1995)**
Pain Jerk is pivotal for bridging analog harshness and early computer-era grit. This track's jagged edits and abrasive timbral shifts anticipate later digital noise practices while staying firmly within harsh noise.

**C.C.C.C. — No Escape (The Japanese / American Noise Treaty, 1995)**
A representative cut from the Japanese/American exchange moment when harsh noise circulated globally on compilations. It showcases C.C.C.C.'s dense, high-pressure wall of distortion without leaning on industrial rhythm.

**Merzbow — Woodpecker No.1 (Pulse Demon, 1996)**
Often cited as the signature Merzbow track, it distills harsh noise into a brutal but strangely structured blast of distortion. Its hyper-saturated texture helped define the 1990s 'Japanoise' aesthetic for international listeners.

**Masonna — Part I (Spectrum Ripper, 1997)**
Masonna's rapid-cut 'parts' translate live noise violence into a studio format—short, surgical bursts of overload. This opening segment introduces the project's trademark speed, abrasion, and shock editing.

**Kazumoto Endo — While You Were Out (While You Were Out, 1999)**
Endo's harsher-than-harsh approach pushes density and distortion into a near-solid block. It's a late-90s statement of the genre's 'no compromise' pursuit of total sonic abrasion.

**K.K. Null — Helium Flash Phase 0.3 (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
KK Null's approach often bridges guitar-derived noise and electronics; here the emphasis is on abrasive timbre and controlled overload. The track is significant for connecting Japanese noise lineage with broader experimental electronics.

**Government Alpha — Quicksand (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
Government Alpha's sound is emblematic of post-90s Japanese harsh noise: thick, corrosive layers that feel almost 'granular' in their density. 'Quicksand' showcases that suffocating, sinking-wall sensation central to the style.

**Mo-Te — 0.9MG (Japanoise, 2003)**
Mo-Te's cut represents the turn-of-the-millennium Japanese harsh noise sound captured in compilation form. It foregrounds corrosive texture and physical volume while avoiding industrial rhythm, keeping 'noise' fully dominant.

**Torturing Nurse — double-sided (Double Sided, 2020)**
Torturing Nurse represents the Chinese harsh noise lineage, combining sustained harshness with a ritualistic sense of escalation. The track foregrounds texture and pressure over any conventional groove.

## Compilation-era underground networks (mid-1990s–early 2000s)

**Princess Dragon-Mom — Hnia-Gone (The Japanese / American Noise Treaty, 1995)**
This track illustrates the early-90s trans-Pacific circulation of harsh noise through compilations. Its raw, high-energy abrasion is a snapshot of underground noise as a scene of small-run releases and extreme sound.

**Pica — Tightening The Pilliwink (The Japanese / American Noise Treaty, 1995)**
Pica's piece emphasizes harsh noise as unstable motion—shifting feedback layers rather than a static wall. It represents the compilation's role in documenting stylistic diversity within Noise Electronic.

**The White Rose — Carnival Of Voles (The Japanese / American Noise Treaty, 1995)**
A distinctive example of harsh noise with a collage-like sense of pacing, demonstrating that 'noise' can still imply form through density changes and textural contrast. Its inclusion on this compilation marks the genre's 90s global networking.

**The Haters — Distance (The Japanese / American Noise Treaty, 1995)**
The Haters are foundational to noise as material practice, and this track foregrounds abrasive texture as sculptural 'object.' It's historically important for linking performance-oriented noise art to recorded harsh noise circulation.

**K2 — Desmoplakin For Crash (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
K2's contribution captures the American harsh noise taste for abrasive, 'junk-sculpted' timbre and relentless saturation. It's a straight-ahead demonstration of Noise Electronic's physicality and endurance aesthetics.

**Smell And Quim — Pervertual Reality (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
This track exemplifies mid-90s harsh noise as an assault on clarity: smeared frequencies, feedback bloom, and deliberately 'unmusical' gesture. It's influential for its uncompromising commitment to texture over structure.

**Bacillus — Progressive Areas Of Necrosis (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
A classic of lo-fi harshness where tape grit and distortion become the main narrative. The piece is important for showing how Noise Electronic can be built from crude means yet still achieve overwhelming intensity.

**OVMN — Storm Leather Kaori Candyland (She Crushes My Mind) (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
OVMN represents the genre's more grotesque, collage-like harsh noise strand—rapid timbral shifts and jagged edits rather than steady drones. The track's fractured density is a textbook 'noise as montage' approach.

**Evil Moisture — Compressed Air Hostess, Scud Squad, Ear Bomb (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
Evil Moisture's cut emphasizes hyperactive, cut-up abrasion—noise as a stream of unstable fragments. It's significant for anticipating later 'digital' noise attitudes while staying grounded in harsh-noise overload.

**Third Organ — Yagamania Part 7 (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
Third Organ's piece shows the genre's fixation on bodily impact via sustained, midrange-heavy distortion. It's a strong example of harsh noise that feels sculpted rather than purely chaotic.

**Hyper Ventilation — Catalepsy (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
A compact demonstration of harsh noise as 'stasis under pressure'—thick bands of distortion held at punishing intensity. It's emblematic of the mid-90s compilation-driven harsh noise circulation.

**Iugula Thor — Physical Abuse (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
The track leans into abrasive dynamics and abrupt shifts, spotlighting the genre's confrontational performance ethos. Its rawness underscores how Noise Electronic often privileges immediacy over polish.

**Hanged Mans Orgasm — Strangulation Fetish (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
This cut embodies harsh noise's transgressive edge through sound alone—harsh, claustrophobic, and intentionally uncomfortable. It's important as a snapshot of the genre's mid-90s extreme underground.

**Noiseuse — Untitled (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
Noiseuse represents the early-2000s 'pure harsh' aesthetic: relentless, texture-first noise without rhythmic anchoring. The track's anonymity (even in naming) reinforces the genre's focus on sound as material.

**Guilty Connector — Genocide Manimal (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
A sharply cut, high-pressure harsh noise piece that shows how early-2000s noise could be both brutal and concise. It's a strong example of the genre's move toward tightly edited impact.

**Defektro — Euthanasia (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
Defektro's cut is a model of harsh noise as pressure-cooker texture—tight bands of distortion with little relief. It's valuable for representing lesser-known but committed practitioners within the scene.

**Wage-Slave Exchange — HONDA!! Nice! You Too. (Japanoise Of Death, 2003)**
A concise blast of harsh noise that highlights the genre's taste for abrupt, violent timbral events. Its cult status stems from capturing the compilation-era 'noise hit' format in under five minutes.

**Yellow Swans — Foiled (Going Places, 2010)**
Yellow Swans' long-form noise builds a hypnotic, immersive field where subtle shifts matter as much as sheer volume. 'Foiled' is influential for demonstrating how Noise Electronic can sustain duration with evolving texture.

## North American scene-definers (1990s–2010s)

**Daniel Menche — Furnace 'Part One' (Furnace, 1994)**
An early Menche statement where harshness is sustained long enough to reveal internal spectral movement. The track is historically significant for helping establish Pacific Northwest noise as a serious, enduring practice.

**Richard Ramirez — Killing Capacity (Deconstructed Hand Device, 1995)**
A classic of mid-90s harsh noise where texture is relentlessly foregrounded and structure is reduced to intensity management. The track helped define a strain of American noise that feels physically 'compressive' and unforgiving.

**Macro Beaulieau — That Didn't Hurt One Little Bit (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
Emil Beaulieau's work is central to North American harsh noise, and this track distills his taste for overwhelming density and texture. It stands out for balancing monolithic saturation with subtle internal motion.

**Killer Bug — Brain Police Besieged Parkside Corp. (Music Should Hurt, 1996)**
A key example of cassette-era harsh noise where tape compression and distortion form the 'instrument.' Its frantic, overloaded surface reflects the DIY distribution networks that fueled the genre's growth.

**Macronympha — Fucking My Nurse In The Dark (After She Gives Me The Morphine Shot) (Intensive Care, 1998)**
Macronympha are central to US harsh noise's cassette/CD era, and this track showcases their taste for thick, obscene saturation and sustained pressure. It's historically important as part of the scene's late-90s consolidation.

**Kevin Drumm — Inferno (Sheer Hellish Miasma, 2002)**
A cornerstone of early-2000s American noise: three long-form slabs where micro-variation inside saturation becomes the drama. 'Inferno' demonstrates how Noise Electronic can be both monolithic and deeply detailed.

**Sickness — I Have Become the Disease that Made Me (I Have Become the Disease that Made Me, 2002)**
Sickness represents the darker, more oppressive side of harsh noise without relying on industrial beats. The title track is a potent example of dense, choking distortion as an aesthetic end in itself.

**Wolf Eyes — Stabbed in the Face (Burned Mind, 2004)**
Wolf Eyes helped bring American noise to wider underground attention, and this track captures their claustrophobic, blown-out electronics. It's influential for its mix of harsh texture with an almost 'song-like' trajectory without becoming rock.

**Hair Police — Obedience Cuts (Obedience Cuts, 2004)**
Hair Police's early work is key to the 2000s 'American harsh noise' boom: crude electronics, maximal distortion, and confrontational pacing. The title track crystallizes the era's DIY extremity.

**John Wiese — Sad Psychics (Soft Punk, 2007)**
Wiese's work is pivotal in the American noise network (label work, curation, and releases), and this track highlights his precise, aggressive editing. It's a model of 'composed' harsh noise—dense but intentionally shaped.

**Aaron Dilloway — Tremors (Modern Jester, 2012)**
Dilloway's tape-based noise aesthetic turns degradation—hiss, warp, overload—into expressive structure. 'Tremors' is important for the post-2000 shift toward 'materialist' noise composition and label/DIY culture.

**Bastard Noise — Obligatory Mortal Obsolescence (The Skull Scriptures, 2021)**
Bastard Noise connect harsh noise with grindcore's extremity while keeping noise as the dominant material. This track shows a modern, high-definition harshness that still carries the genre's DIY ferocity.

## Harsh Noise Wall & contemporary global extremes (2000s–2020s)

**Skin Crime — First Shock (Tsunami, 2000)**
Skin Crime's work is a touchstone for harsher-than-harsh American noise, and 'First Shock' balances brutal saturation with a controlled, almost ritual pacing. It's emblematic of the genre's late-90s/early-2000s evolution.

**Werewolf Jerusalem — Untitled 1 (Werewolf Jerusalem, 2001)**
A cult Harsh Noise Wall document: minimal change, maximal pressure, and an 'endurance-as-form' mindset. Its importance is tied to the underground codification of HNW aesthetics in the early 2000s.

**Pedestrian Deposit — Mascara (Fatale, 2004)**
Pedestrian Deposit bring a precise, almost architectural sense of layering to harsh noise. 'Mascara' is a standout for its controlled density and the way it suggests structure through timbral strata rather than rhythm.

**The Cherry Point — Untitled/ 1 (Night Of The Bloody Tapes, 2005)**
The Cherry Point's tape-sourced harsh noise is influential for its grainy, 'analog horror' pressure and minimal variation. This opener exemplifies the genre's fascination with lo-fi texture as atmosphere.

**The Rita — Thousands of Dead Gods (Thousands Of Dead Gods, 2006)**
A defining Harsh Noise Wall statement: near-static immersion that turns endurance into form. It's significant for codifying the 'wall' as a distinct noise-electronic listening practice.

**Sewer Election — III (Sex / Death, 2007)**
Sewer Election represents the Scandinavian strand of harsh, textural noise that favors severe minimalism over collage. 'III' is significant for its sustained pressure and subtle internal modulation.

**Mania — Armed To The Teeth (Armed To The Teeth, 2008)**
Mania's single-track barrage is a strong example of late-2000s harsh noise focused on uninterrupted intensity. It illustrates how the genre's 'one long take' approach can still feel carefully calibrated.

**Vomir — Proanomie (Proanomie, 2009)**
Vomir is among the central architects of Harsh Noise Wall, and 'Proanomie' demonstrates the style's principle of unchanging, suffocating texture. Its importance lies in establishing HNW as a coherent, international micro-genre.
