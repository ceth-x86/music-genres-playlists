# Future Bass

## Proto & Pioneers (2011–2014)

**Cashmere Cat — Mirror Maru (Mirror Maru (EP), 2012)**
A touchstone for the early 'purple' strain of future bass, built on elastic low‑end and glossy, detuned chord colors. It helped define the genre's preference for sensuous harmony and heavily processed vocal/lead motifs.

**Hudson Mohawke — Chimes (Chimes (EP), 2013)**
A proto‑future‑bass blueprint: pitched synth stabs, swung percussion, and an exaggerated 'pump' that later producers formalized as the style's signature drop feel. Its maximal sound design sits right at the genre's point of origin.

**Rustie — Slasherr (Triadzz / Slasherr (single), 2013)**
Rustie's bright, overdriven synth stacks and hyper‑compressed groove became a foundational reference for future bass timbre. The track's kaleidoscopic chord work anticipates the later 'festival' version of the sound.

**Ryan Hemsworth — Snow in Newark (Guilt Trips, 2013)**
A crucial early link between beat-scene aesthetics and what would be codified as future bass: woozy harmony, vocal fragments, and bass-weighted swing. Its dreamy chord language and producer-led focus helped lay groundwork for the genre's later melodic emphasis.

**What So Not — High You Are (High You Are (single), 2013)**
A seminal early single that merges half‑time swing with lush chord payloads without tipping into trap or dubstep formulas. Its 'roomy' drop design became a template for mid‑2010s melodic bass producers.

**Basenji — Dawn (Dawn (single), 2013)**
A clean, melodic Australian take on the emerging style: bright chord voicings, rubbery sub, and pop‑leaning hookcraft. It shows how quickly future bass could become song‑forward while keeping bass‑music punch.

**Lindsay Lowend — GT40 (Windfish (EP), 2013)**
An early showcase of the genre's 'cute' synth palette and cartoon‑bright harmonic language—precursors to later kawaii‑leaning future bass. The track's playful sound design broadens the genre beyond purely 'purple' grit.

**Wave Racer — Streamers (Streamers (single), 2014)**
A defining example of the sunlit, bouncy end of early future bass, with chiptune‑adjacent leads and clean, chord‑heavy drops. Its upbeat aesthetic opened the door to a more pop‑accessible strain of the style.

**Lido — Money (feat. Andreas Moe) (I Love You (EP), 2014)**
Lido's meticulous drum programming and glossy chord modulation helped codify future bass as a producers' genre with clear technical signatures. The track also foregrounds 'soulful' songwriting inside the drop framework.

**ODESZA — Say My Name (feat. Zyra) (In Return, 2014)**
A crossover‑friendly future‑bass-adjacent anthem that popularized wide, emotional chord swells and vocal‑sample hooks in a clean, cinematic mix. It demonstrates the genre's bridge between bass music and indie‑electronic songcraft.

**Giraffage — Tell Me (No Reason (EP), 2014)**
A sleek, minimal future‑bass approach that emphasizes negative space and melody over maximal aggression. Its soft‑edged drop vocabulary influenced the more intimate, headphone‑oriented side of the genre.

**Vanic — Skinny Love (Vanic Remix) (Skinny Love (Vanic Remix) (single), 2014)**
An early example of future bass remix culture: recognizable topline, transformed into a chord‑swell drop and punchy bass. It helped normalize the genre's 'emotional' pop reframing within a bass‑music structure.

## Breakout & Codification (2015–2016)

**Jai Wolf — Indian Summer (Indian Summer (single), 2015)**
A core SoundCloud‑era statement: euphoric chords, vocal‑sample hooks, and a drop that's melodic without drifting into house. It became a reference point for the 'summery' side of future bass and its festival‑ready emotional arc.

**Louis the Child — It's Strange (feat. K.Flay) (It's Strange (single), 2015)**
Louis the Child helped popularize future bass as hook‑centric songwriting for the streaming era. The track's playful vocal chopping and bright chord-stack drop became a widely imitated template.

**Kiiara — Gold (Gold (single), 2015)**
A pop vocal performance framed by a distinctly future‑bass drop language—swelling chords, tight sidechain, and glossy synth coloration. It signaled how the genre's production grammar could carry mainstream toplines without losing its identity.

**KRANE — About You (feat. Lemay) (About You (single), 2015)**
A clean, melodic future bass single that balances sub weight with luminous harmony and precise drum programming. Its restrained drop shows the genre's capacity for elegance and space rather than maximal aggression.

**Prince Fox — Ode to a Conversation Stuck in Your Throat (Ode to a Conversation Stuck in Your Throat (single), 2015)**
A key 'emotional future bass' track: nostalgic chord movement, intimate vocal framing, and a drop that feels like a chorus. It helped define the genre's bittersweet, diary‑like affect in the mid‑2010s.

**Ekali — Braids (Braids (single), 2015)**
Ekali's early work highlights the genre's percussive snap and bass‑music discipline while keeping the drop firmly chord‑led. It's a strong example of future bass where groove and harmony are equally foregrounded.

**Alison Wonderland — U Don't Know (feat. Wayne Coyne) (Run, 2015)**
A high‑profile alternative‑leaning future bass cut that brought the style into a rock‑adjacent songwriting space without turning into trap. Its dramatic chord swells and vocal treatment helped expand the genre's emotional range.

**Snail's House — Kirara (Kirara (EP), 2015)**
A defining kawaii-side future bass release, built from bright, playful leads and tightly pumped chord drops. It demonstrates how the genre's harmonic-drop logic can support highly 'cute' timbres while still reading as bass music at its core.

**Sweater Beats — Magic (Magic (single), 2015)**
A bridge between beat‑scene sensibilities and the future‑bass drop format: smooth harmony, vocal sampling, and bass‑music swing. It exemplifies the genre's roots in producer culture rather than band-centric writing.

**Branchez — Dreams (Dreams (single), 2015)**
A staple of the emotional SoundCloud wave, with swelling chords and a drop that functions like a melodic chorus. It shows how future bass absorbed indie‑pop sentiment while staying rhythmically bass‑music-forward.

**Mura Masa — Love$ick (feat. A$AP Rocky) (Someday Somewhere (EP), 2016)**
A hybrid that still reads as future bass because the harmonic payload and drop contour dominate the record's identity. Its crisp, minimal drop sound influenced a wave of producers seeking 'less is more' future bass.

**Marshmello — Alone (Alone (single), 2016)**
A simple but influential festival‑ready future bass single with an ultra‑clean, punchy drop. It exemplifies the genre's ability to turn minimal melodic material into huge crowd impact through mix and dynamics.

**Martin Garrix — In the Name of Love (feat. Bebe Rexha) (In the Name of Love (single), 2016)**
A high‑visibility bridge between big-room EDM and future bass: pop vocal, then a chord‑swell drop that keeps the bass‑music pulse. It helped cement future bass as a 'default' drop style for mid‑2010s mainstage writing.

**OOKAY — Thief (Thief (single), 2016)**
A bright, high‑energy future bass hit whose drop melody is carried by chopped vocal timbres and compact chord stabs. The record's clarity and immediacy made it a staple reference for producer technique.

**Chet Porter — Stay (Stay (single), 2016)**
A quintessential 'soft' future bass record: airy chords, delicate vocal sampling, and a drop built around harmonic motion rather than brute force. It illustrates the genre's intimate, bedroom‑electronic side.

**Kasbo — Again (Again (single), 2016)**
Kasbo's melodic sensibility shows how future bass can feel cinematic without becoming downtempo or house. The track's warm chord voicings and bright melodic drops helped define the Scandinavian wing of the sound.

**DROELOE — Bon Voyage (Bon Voyage (single), 2016)**
A sharp, playful articulation of future bass with syncopated leads and crisp, modern drum design. It's a landmark for the genre's 'cartoon‑bright' sound design and bouncy groove.

**WRLD — By Design (By Design (single), 2016)**
A Monstercat-era future bass cut that pairs big chord drops with tight, radio‑friendly structure. It's representative of how the genre standardized a polished, festival‑scale mix aesthetic.

**Slushii — To Say Goodbye (To Say Goodbye (single), 2016)**
Slushii's approach spotlights the genre's maximal chord stacks and playful melodic motifs, tuned for big drops. It's a clear example of future bass as high‑contrast 'cute vs. heavy' sound design.

**Manila Killa — All I Need (All I Need (single), 2016)**
A glossy, R&B‑adjacent future bass track where the drop remains chord‑centric and emotionally direct. It helped shape the West Coast 'late‑night' interpretation of the genre.

**Robotaki — Ghostboy (Ghostboy (single), 2016)**
A sleek future bass single that favors clean harmonic motion and tasteful restraint over maximalism. It's a strong reference for the genre's pop‑leaning, song‑first direction.

**Vincent — Her (Her (single), 2016)**
Vincent's melodic bass writing demonstrates future bass as tight hook design plus heavy low‑end control. The track's drop is built from luminous chord voicings and sharp rhythmic punctuation—hallmarks of the style.

**Bearson — Pink Medicine (Pink Medicine (single), 2016)**
A luminous, melodic future bass record that emphasizes songwriting and chord color as much as low‑end impact. It's representative of the genre's shift toward vocal‑centric, headphone‑friendly production.

**Sia — Helium (This Is Acting, 2016)**
A pop record whose drop is unmistakably future bass in articulation—chopped vocal textures, wide chord swells, and pumped dynamics. It's a clear marker of the style's assimilation into mainstream pop production.

**The Chainsmokers — Don't Let Me Down (feat. Daya) (Collage (EP), 2016)**
A mainstream breakthrough that made future‑bass drop grammar legible to pop radio: vocal‑chop lead, side‑chained supersaws, and clean half‑time swing. Its success accelerated the genre's mid‑2010s dominance.

**Flume — Never Be Like You (feat. Kai) (Skin, 2016)**
A textbook codification of future bass as pop structure plus advanced sound design—detuned chords, elastic bass, and vocal‑sample melodic glue. It also demonstrates Flume's role in pushing the genre's timbral sophistication.

**San Holo — Light (Light (single), 2016)**
One of the defining future bass anthems: guitar‑tinged writing, vocal‑chop hook, and an instantly recognizable chord swell drop. It set a benchmark for 'human' warmth in a genre often associated with synthetic maximalism.

**YUC'e — Future Candy (Future Candy (EP), 2016)**
A foundational kawaii-leaning future bass statement: bright, sugary timbres and tightly side‑chained chord drops, without shifting into hardcore or pop EDM tropes. It shows how the genre's harmonic-drop logic could be re-skinned with playful, 'anime' synth color while remaining unmistakably future bass.

## Golden Age (2017–2018)

**Illenium — Fractures (feat. Nevve) (Awake, 2017)**
A defining 'melodic' future bass record where the drop's chord motion functions like a chorus, not a gimmick. It set the emotional and mix‑polish standard for the genre's late‑2010s stadium scale.

**Gryffin — Heading Home (Heading Home (single), 2017)**
A pristine, pop‑ready future bass single that turns the build–drop cycle into full-on song form. Its clean chord swells and melodic lift exemplify the genre's radio‑friendly peak period.

**Dabin — Alive (Alive (single), 2017)**
A guitarist-producer translation of future bass: organic lead writing over bass‑music dynamics and chord‑drop structure. It helped normalize live‑instrument sensibility inside a very 'produced' genre.

**Taska Black — In Your Eyes (In Your Eyes (single), 2017)**
A clean, crystalline bitbird-era future bass single with tightly articulated chord stabs and vocal‑chop phrasing. It's emblematic of the genre's shift toward precision, clarity, and melodic memorability.

**Conro — Close (Close (single), 2017)**
A compact, songwriting‑first future bass record that keeps the drop melodic and hook-like rather than purely textural. It represents the 'pop structure, future bass sonics' formula at its most efficient.

**k?d — Lose Myself (Lose Myself (single), 2017)**

**Electric Mantis — Daydreamer (Daydreamer (single), 2017)**
