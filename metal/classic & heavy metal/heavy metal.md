# Heavy Metal

## Foundations & First-Wave (1970–1979)

**Black Sabbath — War Pigs (Paranoid, 1970)**
A foundational heavy metal epic: monolithic riffs, dark harmonic language, and a lurching groove that turned blues-rock heaviness into something unmistakably metallic. Its riff construction and guitar tone became a blueprint for traditional metal songwriting for decades.

**Sir Lord Baltimore — Kingdom Come (Kingdom Come, 1970)**
Often cited in the genealogy of heavy metal for its sheer volume, speed, and riff-centric writing at a time when rock was still largely blues-based. The track's aggressive drumming and thick guitar attack anticipate the 'metal' feel that would harden in the mid‑70s.

**Budgie — Breadfan (Never Turn Your Back on a Friend, 1973)**
An early metal-leaning riff monster: chunky low-end power-chords, sudden tempo turns, and a sense of menace that goes beyond typical early-70s rock. It became a cult touchstone for later traditional metal players hunting for 'pre-NWOBHM' DNA.

**Judas Priest — Victim of Changes (Sad Wings of Destiny, 1976)**
One of the key moments where heavy metal sheds rock's looseness for precision: twin-guitar drama, sharp dynamic shifts, and operatic vocal intensity. The track's structure and soloing vocabulary fed directly into the later NWOBHM and 'classic metal' canon.

**Rainbow — Stargazer (Rising, 1976)**
A grand, riff-driven proto-epic that helped define heavy metal's taste for mythic scale without leaving the core guitar/bass/drums punch. The mix of neoclassical guitar phrasing, pounding rhythm, and towering vocals points forward to traditional metal's 'heroic' mode.

**Scorpions — Sails of Charon (Taken by Force, 1977)**
A crucial bridge between hard rock and fully-formed heavy metal, built around razor-edged riffing and flashy, modal lead work. Its taut arrangement and guitar heroics prefigure the early-80s European approach to classic heavy metal.

**Riot — Warrior (Rock City, 1977)**
A foundational US entry that translates the emerging metal aesthetic into street-level, anthem-ready songwriting. Its tight riffing and chorus-forward punch foreshadow the 1980s American traditional metal explosion without tipping into later thrash traits.

**Motörhead — Overkill (Overkill, 1979)**
A speeded-up, riff-first assault that sharpened heavy metal's sense of momentum while keeping the core vocabulary of classic metal intact. The relentless drive and raw tone made it a key reference point for traditional metal bands seeking maximum energy without thrash's rhythmic language.

## NWOBHM & UK Metal Surge (1979–1985)

**Samson — Riding with the Angels (Shock Tactics, 1979)**
A foundational scene track that distills NWOBHM values: tight rhythm section, direct riffs, and a chorus designed for sweaty club singalongs. It's an emblem of the movement's 'working-band' realism and efficiency.

**Saxon — Wheels of Steel (Wheels of Steel, 1980)**
An archetypal heavy metal anthem where riff economy and chant-ready hooks do the heavy lifting. Saxon's muscular, no-frills approach became central to what 'traditional heavy metal' means in practice.

**Diamond Head — Am I Evil? (Lightning to the Nations, 1980)**
A cornerstone of the NWOBHM riff canon, marrying ominous minor-key tension to a memorable, chantable chorus. Its riff vocabulary and pacing influenced an enormous amount of later traditional metal while remaining stylistically distinct from full thrash.

**Angel Witch — Angel Witch (Angel Witch, 1980)**
A textbook NWOBHM title track: dark, catchy riffs and a lean arrangement that keeps the guitars front-and-center. The song's sinister melodicism became a key reference point for later traditional metal revivals.

**Def Leppard — Wasted (On Through the Night, 1980)**
Early Def Leppard at their most metal: fast, tight riffing and a youthful, streetwise bite rooted in the UK scene. It captures the NWOBHM's push toward speed and precision without adopting thrash's rhythmic aesthetics.

**Witchfynde — Give 'Em Hell (Give 'Em Hell, 1980)**
A gritty, riff-first cut that sits squarely in the NWOBHM club circuit aesthetic: direct hooks, tough tone, and minimal ornamentation. Its rawness and immediacy are exactly the 'metal-as-live-band' ethos of the period.

**Quartz — Stand Up and Fight (Stand Up and Fight, 1980)**
Quartz deliver a hard-hitting, riff-centric statement that helped define the Midlands-to-nationwide metal pipeline. The track's sturdy groove and straightforward riff craft represent the movement's core DNA.

**White Spirit — Midnight Chaser (White Spirit, 1980)**
A cult favorite that highlights NWOBHM's melodic sophistication: bright hooks, nimble riffs, and a tight arrangement. It's often cited as a key 'lost classic' that later traditional metal acts rediscovered.

**Fist — Turn the Hell On (Turn the Hell On, 1980)**
A working-class NWOBHM banger built on blunt riffs and a swaggering chorus. It captures the scene's pragmatic songwriting: heavy, memorable, and made to hit hard live.

**Tygers of Pan Tang — Hellbound (Spellbound, 1981)**
A high-energy NWOBHM cut that showcases the movement's blend of punchy riffs and melodic, almost pop-sure choruses. Its streamlined aggression made it a scene staple and a template for many later 'new traditional' bands.

**Girlschool — Hit and Run (Hit and Run, 1981)**
A defining heavy metal single from the era's hard-touring circuit, combining brisk tempo with tough, riff-forward hooks. It also broadened the genre's visibility and lineage beyond the usual male-fronted narrative of early metal history.

**Demon — Night of the Demon (Night of the Demon, 1981)**
A theatrically charged yet still riff-led NWOBHM classic, showing how traditional metal can add drama without leaving its core vocabulary. Its hooky chorus and mid-tempo stomp fed into the genre's later arena-sized ambitions.

**Holocaust — The Small Hours (The Nightcomers, 1981)**
A cult NWOBHM deep cut prized for its melancholy melodicism and weighty riff progression. The song's atmosphere and harmonic choices became a quiet influence on later traditional metal bands looking for darker emotional colors.

**Praying Mantis — Cheated (Time Tells No Lies, 1981)**
A classic example of NWOBHM's pop-smart melodicism built on a metal foundation: crisp riffs, soaring chorus, and tight pacing. It shows the genre's capacity for elegance without sacrificing heaviness.

**More — Warhead (Warhead, 1981)**
A pure adrenaline shot of NWOBHM: tight, fast riffing and a chorus engineered for crowd response. Its no-nonsense approach makes it a reliable 'textbook' example of classic heavy metal energy.

**Saracen — Crusader (Heroes, Saints & Fools, 1981)**
A heroic, melody-rich take on NWOBHM that foreshadows later traditional metal's 'epic' tendencies while staying riff-led. The song's big chorus and martial feel made it a beloved cult staple.

**Iron Maiden — Hallowed Be Thy Name (The Number of the Beast, 1982)**
A definitive NWOBHM statement: galloping rhythms, melodic twin‑guitar lines, and narrative drama built into the riffing itself. It codified the 'classic heavy metal' balance of speed, melody, and precision without drifting into thrash's attack.

**Tank — Shellshock (Filth Hounds of Hades, 1982)**
An aggressive, street-level take on NWOBHM that keeps the guitars raw and the groove relentless. It's a great example of 'heavy metal first' intensity that stops short of thrash's palm-muted machine-gun language.

**Raven — All for One (All for One, 1983)**
Raven's 'athletic' heavy metal: speed, showmanship, and riff clarity in equal measure. The track helped push classic metal toward higher velocity while keeping the songcraft anchored in heavy metal rather than thrash.

**Satan — Trial by Fire (Court in the Act, 1983)**
A razor-sharp example of NWOBHM precision: intricate riffs, dramatic leads, and clear separation of parts. It demonstrates how classic heavy metal can be technical and tense while remaining distinct from progressive or thrash frameworks.

**Jaguar — Axe Crazy (Power Games, 1983)**
Fast, streamlined heavy metal that captures the era's appetite for velocity and grit. Its driving riffs and compact arrangement make it a benchmark for speed-leaning traditional metal without crossing into thrash.

**Tokyo Blade — If Heaven Is Hell (Tokyo Blade, 1983)**
Tokyo Blade embody the melodic side of NWOBHM: bright twin-guitar hooks and a chorus that sticks immediately. The track's balance of toughness and tunefulness became a key ingredient in later traditional metal revivalism.

**Grim Reaper — See You in Hell (See You in Hell, 1983)**
A later-wave NWOBHM anthem with a massive chorus and a sharp, mid-tempo drive. It became a gateway track for fans who love heavy metal's singable, fist-raising side.

**Rock Goddess — My Angel (Rock Goddess, 1983)**
A standout showing how early-80s heavy metal could be both tough and hook-oriented without sliding into pop-rock. Its riffing and vocal phrasing place it firmly within the traditional metal framework of the era.

**Battleaxe — Burn This Town (Burn This Town, 1983)**
A hard-edged, chant-ready NWOBHM track built for the pub-to-club circuit, with riffs that feel made for synchronized head-nods. It stands as a strong snapshot of the scene's durable 'straight heavy metal' core.

**Cloven Hoof — Gates of Gehenna (Cloven Hoof, 1984)**
An occult-tinged yet fundamentally traditional metal track that shows the movement's darker theatrical streak. Its riff shapes and chorus design are pure classic heavy metal, influential on later underground revivals.

**Avenger — Blood on the Snow (Blood Sports, 1984)**
A punchy later-wave entry with fast riffs and a clear, anthemic structure. It's a strong example of how NWOBHM aesthetics persisted into the mid‑80s even as other metal styles took over the spotlight.

**Blitzkrieg — Inferno (A Time of Changes, 1985)**
A key UK scene anthem known for its dramatic riffing and urgent, driving tempo. It distills NWOBHM's combination of darkness and hookcraft while remaining distinct from thrash's rhythmic vocabulary.

## Global Classic Heavy Metal Peak (1982–1989)

**Ozzy Osbourne — Crazy Train (Blizzard of Ozz, 1980)**
A cornerstone of traditional heavy metal's riff-and-hook economy, powered by a instantly recognizable guitar figure and emphatic vocal phrasing. Its blend of heaviness, melody, and flashy lead work became a template for countless classic metal singles.

**Anvil — Metal on Metal (Metal on Metal, 1982)**
A self-reflexive heavy metal anthem that celebrates the genre's identity through pure riff craft and working-class intensity. Anvil's persistence and sound influenced later waves of traditional metal, especially bands seeking unvarnished heaviness.

**Barón Rojo — Los Rockeros Van al Infierno (Volumen Brutal, 1982)**
A cornerstone of Spanish heavy metal, pairing sturdy riffs with anthemic vocal hooks tailored for mass participation. Its cultural impact helped solidify a distinct Iberian heavy metal identity within the broader classic metal wave.

**Dio — Holy Diver (Holy Diver, 1983)**
The definitive 'classic heavy metal' single of the early 80s: a huge, minor-key riff, commanding mid-tempo drive, and a chorus built for mythic grandeur. It crystalized the genre's post‑NWOBHM sound—bigger production, sharper hooks, and virtuosic guitar—without drifting into adjacent subgenres.

**Accept — Balls to the Wall (Balls to the Wall, 1983)**
A heavyweight European anthem defined by palm-muted chug and regimented rhythmic punch, yet still firmly rooted in classic heavy metal rather than thrash. The chorus and gang-vocal energy helped set the standard for arena-sized traditional metal.

**Mötley Crüe — Shout at the Devil (Shout at the Devil, 1983)**
A signature example of 80s heavy metal's darker, anthemic side: a menacing riff, marching rhythm, and ritualistic chorus delivery. The track helped define how 'big' and cinematic classic metal could sound without relying on thrash speed or groove-metal syncopation.

**Quiet Riot — Metal Health (Bang Your Head) (Metal Health, 1983)**
A landmark mainstream breakthrough that still operates on classic heavy metal fundamentals: a driving riff, simple but forceful structure, and a chant-like refrain. Its success helped normalize heavy metal on pop radio and MTV in the early 80s.

**Krokus — Screaming in the Night (Headhunter, 1983)**
A hard-hitting European take on 80s heavy metal, centered on a chunky, mid-tempo riff and a chorus designed for maximum singalong. The track embodies the genre's straightforward power and touring-band toughness.

**Mercyful Fate — Evil (Melissa, 1983)**
A defining occult-tinged heavy metal track with intricate, melodic riffing and a vocal style that stretches the genre's theatrical possibilities. Its guitar interplay and dark aura became hugely influential for later traditional metal without being 'black metal' in form.

**Heavy Load — Stronger Than Evil (Stronger Than Evil, 1983)**
A key Scandinavian early entry that fuses gritty riffs with a heroic melodic sensibility. It's historically important for showing that 'classic heavy metal' was already a trans-European language by the early 80s.

**Manilla Road — Necropolis (Crystal Logic, 1983)**
A cult epic that proves heavy metal's core vocabulary can sustain long-form narrative intensity without shifting into doom or prog metal. The song's riff cycles and lead-guitar storytelling influenced later underground traditional and 'epic heavy metal' scenes.

**Helix — Rock You (No Rest for the Wicked, 1983)**
A Canadian staple built from tough, memorable riffs and a chorus that's pure bar-band metal adrenaline. It captures the 'working' side of classic heavy metal—direct, hooky, and designed to hit hard live.

**W.A.S.P. — I Wanna Be Somebody (W.A.S.P., 1984)**
A lean, aggressive slice of 80s heavy metal built on a blunt main riff and a massive, attitude-forward chorus. It captures the genre's theatrical swagger while keeping the musical language strictly traditional metal.

**Twisted Sister — Burn in Hell (Stay Hungry, 1984)**
A tougher, more metal-leaning cut from a band often reduced to party anthems: serrated riffs, dramatic vocal lines, and a hook built for arenas. It shows how 80s traditional metal could be both theatrical and genuinely heavy.

**Dokken — Unchain the Night (Tooth and Nail, 1984)**
A classic LA-era heavy metal track where precision riffing and melodic guitar harmony coexist with a sleek, radio-ready finish. Its tight arrangement and 'guitar song' architecture became influential for later traditional metal and hard-edged rock acts.

**Ratt — Round and Round (Out of the Cellar, 1984)**
A definitive 80s metal single that keeps the core ingredients intact: riff-led verses, a huge chorus, and a lead-guitar identity that's inseparable from the song. It represents the era's hook-heavy side of classic metal without losing metallic bite.

**Armored Saint — Can U Deliver (March of the Saint, 1984)**
A key US traditional metal track with muscular riffing and an anthemic, fist-raising chorus. It shows American heavy metal's distinct emphasis on punch and clarity while staying firmly outside thrash's tighter, more percussive riff language.

**Ángeles del Infierno — Maldito Sea Tu Nombre (Pacto con el Diablo, 1984)**
A defining Latin-language heavy metal track with a strong, riff-led chassis and a chorus that functions like a stadium chant. It's historically important for expanding the genre's canon beyond the Anglo-American axis while remaining stylistically orthodox.

**Sortilège — Délire d'un Fou (Métamorphose, 1984)**
A French classic heavy metal cut that balances melodic finesse with hard-edged riff authority. Its dramatic vocal delivery and guitar lines exemplify how traditional metal adapted across European scenes in the mid‑80s.

**Warlord — Black Mass (And the Cannons of Destruction Have Begun..., 1984)**
A foundational US underground heavy metal track with dramatic chord progressions, martial rhythms, and unmistakably traditional riff language. It became a touchstone for later revivalists seeking 'epic' scope inside classic metal forms.

**Yngwie J. Malmsteen's Rising Force — Far Beyond the Sun (Rising Force, 1984)**
A flagship of neoclassical heavy metal guitar, translating Baroque-inspired runs into a strict metal framework of riffs, pulse, and solo-driven drama. Its influence on traditional metal lead playing is enormous, even for bands far removed from the shred scene.

**Black 'n Blue — Hold Back the Night (Black 'n Blue, 1984)**
A sharp 80s traditional metal cut that spotlights riff clarity and melodic guitar identity rather than stylistic crossover. It's a representative example of how the era's scene could produce durable metal craft beyond the biggest names.

**Witch Cross — Axe Dance (Fit for Fight, 1984)**
A cult Danish classic heavy metal track defined by bright, catchy riffs and an urgent, forward-driving tempo. It's often cited as a hidden gem of Europe's mid‑80s traditional metal underground.

**Loudness — Crazy Nights (Thunder in the East, 1985)**
A landmark Japanese heavy metal hit that combines bright hooks with razor-clean guitar work and driving rhythm. Loudness helped internationalize classic heavy metal in the mid‑80s, and this track is their signature statement.

**Stryper — To Hell with the Devil (To Hell with the Devil, 1986)**
A polished yet unmistakably heavy metal anthem built on bright guitar harmonies, strong riffs, and a massive chorus. It's historically significant for how it brought traditional metal aesthetics to audiences who didn't necessarily identify with the genre's usual imagery.

**Chastain — Mystery of Illusion (Ruler of the Wasteland, 1986)**
A virtuosic yet straight heavy metal track where cutting riffs support standout vocal power and flamboyant lead guitar. It helped define the 'US traditional' school—heavy, melodic, and technically sharp without thrash aggression.

**King Diamond — Abigail (Abigail, 1987)**
A narrative-driven heavy metal centerpiece where theatrical vocals sit atop clearly traditional riffing and classic metal lead work. It's influential for proving that concept-album storytelling can thrive inside strict heavy metal idioms.

**Leatherwolf — The Calling (Leatherwolf, 1987)**
A technical yet still traditional metal track known for its triple-guitar attack and tightly arranged riffs. It's a great example of how late-80s heavy metal could be virtuosic without turning into progressive metal proper.

**Anthem — Bound to Break (Bound to Break, 1987)**
Anthem's high-powered take on traditional heavy metal: crisp riffing, urgent tempo, and a chorus built for momentum. It represents the Japanese scene's precision and intensity while staying within classic metal boundaries.

**Warlock — All We Are (Triumph and Agony, 1987)**
A definitive heavy metal singalong with a driving riff and a chorus engineered for communal voice. Warlock's presence also broadened the genre's representation, and this track remains one of the era's most enduring traditional metal anthems.

**U.D.O. — Animal House (Animal House, 1987)**
A quintessential German traditional metal track: tight, mid-tempo riffing and an ironclad vocal performance built for maximum impact. It's a strong example of classic heavy metal's discipline and punch outside the most commercially visible bands.

**Pretty Maids — Future World (Future World, 1987)**
A hook-rich European heavy metal single that keeps riffs central while embracing glossy, major-key uplift. It's a prime example of 80s traditional metal's ability to sound triumphant and radio-ready without leaving the genre's stylistic borders.

**Lita Ford — Kiss Me Deadly (Lita, 1988)**
A mainstream-facing heavy metal hit that still rests on a solid riff backbone and classic verse/chorus metal structure. It's significant for how it brought guitar-driven traditional metal to broader audiences at the tail end of the decade.

**Lizzy Borden — Me Against the World (Master of Disguise, 1989)**
An under-sung late-80s classic that blends sharp riffing with a memorable, anthemic chorus. It captures the era's theatrical edge while remaining grounded in straight heavy metal rather than glam-pop crossover.

## Tradition Kept Alive (1990–2009)

**Skid Row — Slave to the Grind (Slave to the Grind, 1991)**
A rare early‑90s mainstream track that leans decisively into traditional heavy metal weight: thicker riffs, tighter aggression, and a less 'party rock' attitude. It shows classic metal songcraft surviving into the decade without adopting groove-metal syncopation or alternative-rock aesthetics.

**Fight — Into the Pit (War of Words, 1993)**
A stripped-down, riff-driven heavy metal track that emphasizes tightness and intensity over stylistic fusion. It's an important document of early‑90s traditional metal persistence—classic riffs, classic vocals, classic momentum.

**Bruce Dickinson — Accident of Birth (Accident of Birth, 1997)**
A late‑90s traditional metal resurgence moment: melodic, riff-led writing with a dramatic, heroic chorus. It helped demonstrate that classic heavy metal aesthetics could feel contemporary without becoming power metal or modern extreme metal.

**Halford — Resurrection (Resurrection, 2000)**
A modern-era statement of classic heavy metal intent—big riffs, soaring vocal lines, and an arena-sized chorus built on traditional metal harmonic language. It became a rallying point for fans seeking a return to 'straight heavy metal' principles at the turn of the millennium.

**Blaze Bayley — Silicon Messiah (Silicon Messiah, 2000)**
A solid example of 2000-era traditional heavy metal that keeps riffs and vocal narrative at the center, avoiding nu-metal or extreme-metal tropes. Its earnest, anthemic construction made it a cult cornerstone for modern classic-metal audiences.

**The Lord Weird Slough Feg — High Passage / Low Passage (Down Among the Deadmen, 2000)**
An underground classic that reasserts heavy metal's core riff grammar through literate storytelling and agile, old-school guitar work. It's influential within the later traditional metal revival for proving that creativity and 'classic' authenticity can coexist.

**Wolf — Voodoo (Black Wings, 2002)**
A key early-2000s revival track: punchy NWOBHM-derived riffs, propulsive tempo, and a chorus designed for live impact. Wolf helped bridge the gap between 80s classic metal and the later NWOTHM wave.

**3 Inches of Blood — Deadly Sinners (Advance and Vanquish, 2004)**
A modern cult anthem that channels traditional metal's riffing and heroic vocal energy into a high-velocity package. It became a gateway track for a new generation discovering classic heavy metal values outside the retro-nostalgia trap.

**Bullet — Heading for the Top (Heading for the Top, 2006)**
A straight-ahead Swedish heavy metal anthem built from tight riffs and an instantly memorable chorus. Bullet's sound captures the 'classic metal' aesthetic in a post‑2000 context with no reliance on thrash or groove-metal traits.

**Sister Sin — On Parole (Switchblade Serenades, 2008)**
A hard-driving, riff-first track that keeps traditional heavy metal's chorus craft and guitar tone intact in the late‑2000s landscape. It's a strong example of how classic metal attitude and structure survived into a period dominated by other metal styles.

## NWOTHM & Modern Traditional Heavy Metal (2010–2025)

**Enforcer — Katana (Diamonds, 2010)**
A flagship track of the 2010s traditional metal revival, combining high-speed riffing with unmistakably classic heavy metal melodic phrasing. It helped define the NWOTHM sound as something living and urgent rather than museum-piece retro.

**White Wizzard — High Speed GTO (Over the Top, 2010)**
A bright, riff-forward anthem that re-centers classic heavy metal's twin priorities: hook and guitar identity. It became a recognizable calling card for the modern revival's love of sharp choruses and old-school lead work.

**In Solitude — Witches Sabbath (The World. The Flesh. The Devil, 2011)**
A modern classic that captures heavy metal's darker melodicism with refined songwriting and a distinctly contemporary sense of space. Crucially, it stays rooted in traditional riff language rather than drifting into doom, goth, or extreme-metal hybrids.

**High Spirits — Another Night (Another Night, 2011)**
An exuberant, chorus-driven take on traditional heavy metal that highlights the genre's 'uplifting' side without becoming power metal. Its clean riffs and singalong melodies made it a key entry point for newer fans of classic metal forms.

**Steelwing — Full Speed Ahead (Reset, Reboot, Redeem, 2012)**
A quintessential NWOTHM sprint: sharp, fast riffs and a big, no-frills chorus aimed straight at the classic heavy metal sweet spot. It demonstrates how the revival scene updated 80s energy with modern tightness while keeping the style pure.

**Cauldron — Chained Up in Chains (Chained to the Wheel, 2012)**
A sleek, streetwise traditional metal track that prioritizes riff clarity and song shape over genre-blending. Cauldron's approach helped diversify the revival beyond pure NWOBHM worship into a broader classic heavy metal palette.

**Hitten — First Strike (First Strike, 2014)**
A modern Spanish traditional metal anthem with crisp riffing and a chorus engineered for instant memorability. It shows how classic heavy metal's core language continues to regenerate in regional scenes without leaning on adjacent genres.

**Skull Fist — Head of the Pack (Chasing the Dream, 2014)**
A defining 2010s 'young band' heavy metal track: fast, bright riffs, shoutable hooks, and a relentless sense of momentum. Skull Fist became emblematic of the revival's fan-first energy and devotion to classic metal fundamentals.

**RAM — Machine Invaders (Forced Entry, 2015)**
A modern traditional metal highlight built around razor-edged riffing and a tightly controlled, punchy arrangement. It's exemplary of the Swedish scene's ability to sound both classic and sharply contemporary without borrowing from extreme-metal styles.

**Ambush — Firestorm (Desecrator, 2015)**
A straight heavy metal anthem with galloping rhythms and a chorus designed for fist-in-the-air unity. Ambush helped cement Sweden as a central hub of 2010s traditional metal by sticking rigorously to classic riff grammar.

**Night Demon — Screams in the Night (Curse of the Damned, 2015)**
A modern 'textbook' heavy metal song: lean structure, urgent tempo, and melodic hooks riding on a firm riff chassis. Night Demon's work became a reference point for US-based NWOBHM-inspired metal in the 2010s.

**Eternal Champion — I Am the Hammer (The Armor of Ire, 2016)**
A defining statement of modern epic-leaning traditional metal that still stays anchored in classic heavy metal riffs and pacing. Its mythic atmosphere and disciplined riff craft made it a cult benchmark for the genre's contemporary storytelling side.

**Striker — Phoenix Lights (Stand in the Fire, 2016)**
A high-energy modern heavy metal track built on crisp riffing and a chorus that hits with classic immediacy. It exemplifies how revival-era bands balanced speed and melody without moving into thrash aggression.

**Sumerlands — The Guardian (Sumerlands, 2016)**
A riff-driven modern classic with a strong, traditional chorus and a distinctly old-school guitar vocabulary. It's influential within the US revival for capturing 80s heavy metal 'feel' in a fresh, uncluttered production style.

**Satan's Hallow — Satan's Hallow (Satan's Hallow, 2017)**
A modern cult anthem that nails classic heavy metal's balance of darkness, melody, and direct hookcraft. Its straightforward riff architecture makes it a clear, unambiguous example of traditional metal in the 2010s.

**Air Raid — Night Comes Calling (Power Dive, 2017)**
A Swedish revival standout built from bright, melodic riffs and tightly arranged twin-guitar leads. It demonstrates the scene's commitment to traditional heavy metal's melodic clarity and forward drive.

**Portrait — Bloodbath (Crossroads, 2017)**
A theatrical yet strictly traditional heavy metal track that uses dark atmosphere without slipping into goth or extreme-metal forms. Portrait's dramatic vocal style sits atop classic riff shapes, making the genre identity unmistakable.

**Traveler — Street Machine (Traveler, 2019)**
A modern speed-leaning heavy metal anthem that keeps its foundation in classic metal riffing and structure. Traveler represent the new generation's ability to sound urgent and aggressive while staying within traditional metal boundaries.

**Riot City — Burn the Night (Burn the Night, 2019)**
A modern Canadian breakout that embraces classic heavy metal's high-register vocal drama and razor-sharp riffing. It's become a contemporary scene touchstone for fans chasing 'pure' traditional metal energy.

**Tailgunner — Guns for Hire (Guns for Hire, 2023)**
A recent example of the genre's continuing vitality: galloping riffs, bright leads, and a chorus built for communal shout-alongs. It shows how modern traditional metal can sound contemporary while remaining stylistically orthodox.
