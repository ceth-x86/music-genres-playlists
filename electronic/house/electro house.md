# Electro House

## Foundations & breakthrough (2002–2006)

**Benny Benassi presents The Biz — Satisfaction (Satisfaction (Single), 2002)**
A foundational early electro-house shockwave: distorted bass, blunt drums, and a minimal, meme-proof hook. It helped define the genre's "brash + physical" club identity.

**Boogie Pimps — Somebody to Love (Somebody to Love (Single), 2003)**
A crossover-friendly electro-house flip that shows how the style could reframe familiar vocal material into peak-time pressure. Its simplicity is the point: groove, hook, and a thick low-end punch.

**Alter Ego — Rocker (Rocker (Single), 2004)**
An early blueprint for the "one-riff monster" electro-house idea—hypnotic, mechanical, and relentlessly club-functional. It sits right on that electro/house fault line the genre thrives on.

**Mylo — Drop the Pressure (Destroy Rock & Roll, 2004)**
Bouncy bass and an instantly recognizable vocal idea make it a template for electro-house as pop-forward club music. It's bright and accessible without losing the forward drive electro-house needs.

**DJ Tocadisco — Nobody (Likes the Records That I Play) (Nobody (Likes the Records That I Play) (Single), 2004)**
A defining early-2000s electro-house attitude piece: cheeky vocal concept, crisp drums, and electro stabs that lock into the grid. It's built for DJs—tight, direct, and loud in all the right places.

**Tiga — Pleasure from the Bass (Pleasure from the Bass (Single), 2004)**
A bridge between electroclash-era aesthetics and fully-formed electro house: rubbery bass, sharp edges, and a club-first arrangement. It's "minimal parts, maximal impact."

**Soulwax — NY Excuse (NY Excuse (Single), 2005)**
Gritty, rock-leaning energy translated into electro-house structure—driving drums plus that unmistakably thick, gritty synth attitude. It captures the genre's love for distortion without becoming messy.

**Coburn — We Interrupt This Program (We Interrupt This Program (Single), 2005)**
A classic "DJ tool" that's all about a memorable synth motif and forward momentum. It's electro house at its most functional: tension, release, repeat—no fluff.

**The Drill — The Drill (The Drill (Single), 2005)**
One of the era's most recognizable sound-design hooks, built to cut through loud rooms. Beatport explicitly tags it as electro house—useful confirmation for a track that sits close to other club substyles.

**Fedde Le Grand — Put Your Hands Up For Detroit (Put Your Hands Up For Detroit (Single), 2006)**
A peak example of electro house going mainstream without softening the drop logic. It became a cultural shorthand for mid-2000s club electro, and it famously topped UK charts in the same moment as "Yeah Yeah."

**Hi_Tack — Say Say Say (Waiting 4 U) (Say Say Say (Waiting 4 U) (Single), 2005)**
Electro house with a big, immediate vocal hook and a clean, punchy low-end—exactly the combination that made the style travel beyond specialist dance floors. It's upbeat, tight, and unapologetically "mainroom."

**Outwork feat. Mr Gee — Elektro (Elektro (Single), 2006)**
A minimal vocal idea over a driving electro-house chassis—tight drums, tough bass, and lots of space for DJs to work with. It's a reminder that "electro house" can be spare, not only maximal.

**Bodyrox feat. Luciana — Yeah Yeah (Yeah Yeah (Single), 2006)**
A defining vocal electro-house record—attitude, bounce, and a drop designed to explode in clubs. It hit UK #2 at the same time Fedde le Grand was #1, a neat snapshot of electro-house dominance in that moment.

**Mason — Exceeder (Exceeder (Single), 2006)**
One of the most enduring riffs in electro-house history: instantly recognizable, endlessly reusable in sets. The arrangement is pure club engineering—build, slam, repeat.

**Vandalism — Never Say Never (Turn the World On, 2006)**
Electro house with punky, shouty energy—showing how the genre absorbed indie/rock attitude while keeping house structure. It's brash in the way early electro house should be.

## Bloghouse / French-touch revival & Euro club boom (2006–2008)

**Justice vs Simian — We Are Your Friends (We Are Your Friends (Single), 2006)**
A defining "bloghouse" anthem: chopped vocal obsession over a pounding electro-house frame. It helped turn electro house into a youth-culture phenomenon, not just a club sound.

**Digitalism — Pogo (Idealism, 2007)**
Bright, video-gamey synth optimism fused with a proper electro-house kick-and-drop structure. It's the genre at its most celebratory without losing impact.

**Boys Noize — & Down (Oi Oi Oi, 2007)**
A harder, more industrial-leaning electro-house stance: sharp edges, ruthless groove, and a bassline that bites. It's built like a weapon—no wasted bars.

**MSTRKRFT — Easy Love (The Looks, 2006)**
A crunchy, compressed, neon-lit electro-house cut that sits perfectly between indie-dance and peak-time club pressure. The sound palette screams mid-2000s electro in the best way.

**SebastiAn — Ross Ross Ross (Ross Ross Ross (EP), 2006)**
Hyper-aggressive, clipped, and metallic—this is electro house as maximal attitude and sound design. It helped define the "ravey" French edge of the style.

**DJ Mehdi — Signatune (Signatune (Single), 2006)**
A minimal-but-mean electro-house groove that proves you don't need huge chords to dominate a floor. It's all about the rhythm, the bass weight, and the swagger.

**Simian Mobile Disco — Hustler (Attack Decay Sustain Release, 2007)**
Electro house that feels like a machine coming to life: looping synth cells, crisp drums, and relentless forward motion. It's a great example of the genre thriving in live-band-adjacent contexts.

**Goose — Black Gloves (Bring It On, 2006)**
Big, dramatic builds with a stadium-sized electro-house payoff—indie-dance scale, club architecture. It's the "festival before festivals" version of electro house.

**Treasure Fingers — Cross the Dancefloor (Cross the Dancefloor (Single), 2006)**
Fidgety, playful, and punchy—electro house filtered through quick edits and bouncy bass. It captures the era when the genre felt like a new toy every week.

**Surkin — Radio Fireworks (Radio Fireworks (EP), 2007)**
A squealing-lead, high-voltage electro-house rush that's all about energy management. The drop feels both chaotic and tightly engineered.

**Hervé — Rave 2 the Grave (Rave 2 the Grave (Single), 2007)**
A raw, rave-referential electro-house banger that leans into grime and distortion. It's a key example of the UK's rougher, cheekier electro-house angle.

**Kurd Maverick — The Rub (The Rub (Single), 2007)**
Stripped and hypnotic—an electro-house groove that wins through repetition and low-end insistence. It's the kind of track that teaches DJs patience: let the riff do the work.

**Lützenkirchen — 3 Tage Wach (3 Tage Wach (Single), 2008)**
Aggressive German vocal-hook electro house—simple ingredients, enormous payoff. It's built for mass singalong and mainroom pressure at once.

**Crookers — Love to Edit (Love to Edit (Single), 2008)**
A swaggering, edit-culture take on electro house—choppy, cheeky, and very "of the blog era." It shows how electro house absorbed hip-hop pacing without changing the 4/4 core.

**Fake Blood — Mars (Mars (Single), 2008)**
A masterclass in "one huge riff": a devastating saw-bass idea plus club-perfect drums. It's minimal in composition, maximal in impact.

**Proxy — Raven (Raven (EP), 2008)**
Dark, mechanical electro house—hard edges, tight groove, and a sinister atmosphere. It's the genre's colder, industrial side done right.

**The Bloody Beetroots feat. Congorock — Rombo (Rombo (EP), 2008)**
Distorted, noisy, and proudly excessive—electro house as rave-culture spectacle. It's built around shock-and-awe drops with real rhythmic discipline underneath.

**Nero — Act Like You Know (Act Like You Know (Single), 2008)**
A sleek, ominous electro-house cut that hints at the heavier directions dance music would take soon after. The bass and drums are locked, but the mood is pure menace.

**Boy 8-Bit — Infected (Infected (Single), 2008)**
Playful, squelchy, and bass-forward—this is electro house with personality. The sound design is quirky without sacrificing club weight.

**Les Petits Pilous — Wake Up (Wake Up (Single), 2008)**
A maximal, noisy bloghouse-style electro-house piece that pushes energy to the edge. It's messy in a controlled way—the drop still lands like a hammer.

## Dirty Dutch & mainroom electro house (2009–2011)

**Sidney Samson — Riverside (Riverside (Single), 2009)**
The siren-ish lead and stomping groove became a Dirty Dutch calling card. It's a template track: huge lead, tight drums, instant mainroom reaction.

**Chuckie — Let The Bass Kick (Let The Bass Kick (Single), 2009)**
Pure command: minimalist vocal instruction + a drop engineered for maximum physical response. It's a no-nonsense electro-house tool that DJs can build whole sets around.

**Wolfgang Gartner — Wolfgang's 5th Symphony (Wolfgang's 5th Symphony (Single), 2009)**
Intricate melodic writing (almost "classical" in shape) translated into electro-house punch. It shows you can be musically clever without drifting into progressive-house sentimentality.

**Bart B More feat. MC Flipside — So It Goes (A Day In The Life) (So It Goes (A Day In The Life) (Single), 2009)**
Electro house with hip-house swagger: vocal presence, chunky bass, and relentless drive. It's party music that still hits with electro-house force.

**A-Trak — Heads Will Roll (A-Trak Remix) (Heads Will Roll (A-Trak Remix) (Single), 2009)**
A landmark "indie to electro-house" remix: the drop is pure mainroom pressure, but the source vocal stays iconic. It captures how electro house conquered blogs, not just clubs.

**Afrojack — Take Over Control (Take Over Control (Single), 2010)**
Vocal-led electro house that still keeps a hard-edged drop and tight low-end. It's a peak example of Dutch electro house scaling up to festival/pop reach without losing the stomp.

**Porter Robinson — Say My Name (Say My Name (Single), 2010)**
Early Porter: electro-house structure with the first hints of the hyper-detailed "complextro" approach. The drop's micro-variations keep it exciting even on repeat.

**Skrillex — Kill Everybody (My Name Is Skrillex (EP), 2010)**
A hyperactive, aggressive electro-house track that foreshadows the coming era of heavier sound design. It bridges bloghouse punch with the next generation's maximal synth obsession.

**Felguk — 2nite (2nite (Single), 2010)**
Brazilian electro-house muscle: punchy drums, big lead energy, and a no-frills mainroom arrangement. It's designed to slam, not to drift.

**DJ Bam Bam feat. Mr. Robotic — Watch The Club Go (Watch The Club Go (Single), 2010)**
Straightforward, functional electro house with classic "club chant" framing. It's the kind of record that works because it's engineered for loud rooms, not headphones.

**Alex Mind — Bring Back Boogie (Bring Back Boogie (Single), 2010)**
A throwback-leaning electro-house idea—simple hook, modern kick/bass weight. It shows how the genre can reference earlier dance motifs while keeping a contemporary slam.

**Basto — Gregory's Theme (Gregory's Theme (Single), 2011)**
A big melodic hook riding a Dirty Dutch-compatible groove—uplifting without turning into progressive-house glow. The lead is catchy, but the drop stays physical.

**Hardwell — Encoded (Encoded (Single), 2011)**
Early Hardwell electro house: clean arrangement, aggressive bass, and an efficient mainroom payoff. It's built around clarity—every element exists to hit harder.

**Deniz Koyu — Tung! (Tung! (Single), 2011)**
Bright, piercing lead work with a tight electro-house engine underneath. It's a peak "hands up, then drop" record that stays on the right side of electro-house punch.

**Bingo Players — Cry (Just a Little) (Cry (Just a Little) (Single), 2011)**
Vocal emotion in the topline, but the drop is still chunky electro-house stomp. It's a strong example of electro house balancing melody and mainroom aggression.

**Vato Gonzalez feat. Foreign Beggars — Badman Riddim (Badman Riddim (Single), 2011)**
Dirty Dutch aggression meets rap/grime energy—electro house as a pressure cooker, not a singalong. The drop is all impact and attitude.

**Moguai — Mpire (Mpire (Single), 2011)**
Darker, cinematic electro-house energy that still sticks to mainroom logic. The bass design and arrangement are built for peak-time tension and release.

**R3hab — Mrkrstft (Mrkrstft (Single), 2011)**
A festival-era electro-house cut with gritty stabs and a direct drop. It's a good snapshot of how the sound was tightening into bigger, simpler lead statements.

**Sandro Silva & Quintino — Epic (Epic (Single), 2011)**
A bridge track between Dirty Dutch electro house and the coming big-room wave: huge lead, huge kick, minimal distractions. It's "one idea, maximum crowd response."

**Spencer & Hill — Cool (Cool (Single), 2009)**
Polished, mainroom electro house with a sleek groove and a big drop focus. It sits right in that late-2000s moment when electro-house mixes started sounding stadium-ready.

## Complextro & sound-design peak (2011–2012)

**Zedd — Shave It (Shave It (Single), 2011)**
A signature complextro/electro-house clinic: rapid-fire synth "callouts," tight edits, and a drop that constantly mutates. It's dense without being cluttered.

**Madeon — Icarus (Icarus (Single), 2012)**
Sparkling chords and intricate drop detailing—electro house with a very "designed" feeling. It's emotional, but still engineered to punch.

**Knife Party — Internet Friends (Rage Valley (EP), 2012)**
A cheeky vocal-sample premise paired with brutal electro-house drums and a slam-drop arrangement. It's built for the moment the crowd recognizes the line—and then the floor drops out.

**Feed Me — One Click Headshot (One Click Headshot (Single), 2012)**
Video-game energy translated into electro-house structure: bouncy bass, sharp drums, and hyper-clean impact. It's fun, but it hits hard.

**Dada Life — Kick Out the Epic Motherf**ker (Kick Out the Epic Motherf**ker (Single), 2011)**
Maximal electro house: hype vocal, oversized drop, and an arrangement that exists to push adrenaline. It's the genre as cartoon violence—in a good way.

**Mord Fustang — Lick the Rainbow (Lick the Rainbow (Single), 2011)**
Saturated bass and candy-colored synths—electro house that's playful but still heavyweight. The drop is clean, loud, and unmistakably early-2010s.

**Savant — Sledgehammer (Sledgehammer (Single), 2011)**
Hyper-detailed synthwork with constant variation: electro house treated like a sound-design showcase. It's relentless, but the groove stays locked.

**The M Machine — The Promise (Metropolis Pt. I, 2012)**
Futuristic, melodic electro house that keeps the drums and bass firmly in "mainroom" territory. It's cinematic without turning into a different genre.

**Kill The Noise — Saturn (Saturn (Single), 2012)**
Metallic bass design and a hard, disciplined arrangement—electro house with an industrial sheen. It's a peak-time weapon that still feels "crafted."

**Autoerotique — Turn Up the Volume (Turn Up the Volume (Single), 2011)**
A simple vocal hook + a drop engineered for maximum club response. It's electro house stripped to its most effective essentials.

**Tommy Trash — Luv U Giv (Luv U Giv (Single), 2011)**
Big, bright lead energy sitting on a properly heavy electro-house foundation. It's upbeat and festival-friendly while still being a true "drop record."

**Krewella — Alive (Alive (EP), 2012)**
Pop-forward vocals riding a clear electro-house drop architecture. It's a strong example of electro house supporting songcraft without losing the slam.

**Dyro — Leprechauns & Unicorns (Leprechauns & Unicorns (Single), 2012)**
Classic saw-driven mainstage electro house: punchy, direct, and designed for maximum peak-time lift. The title is silly—the drop isn't.

**Dzeko & Torres — Hey (Hey (Single), 2012)**
Big-hook electro house with a straightforward festival arrangement—build, drop, repeat. It's engineered to be instantly playable in mainroom sets.

**Pegboard Nerds — Pressure Cooker (Pressure Cooker (Single), 2012)**
Quirky synth personality with serious electro-house impact. It's high-energy and playful, but the drums/bass are pure mainstage.

**Overwerk — Daybreak (Daybreak (Single), 2012)**
Polished, uplifting electro house that still lands with weight. The melodic elements are bright, but the groove remains club-first.

**Sander van Doorn — Koko (Koko (Single), 2011)**
A chant-driven, minimal-hook electro house track built for brutal efficiency. It shows how a trance/techno-adjacent producer can speak fluent electro-house drop language.

**Starkillers — Pressure (Pressure (Single), 2011)**
Gritty electro house with a tight groove and a big-room-ready drop. It's a strong example of early-2010s mainroom sound: clean, loud, and direct.

**Far Too Loud — 600 Years (Firestorm EP, 2012)**
Relentless midrange bass aggression and a drop designed to overwhelm. It's electro house pushed toward maximal intensity without leaving the genre's core structure.

**Cazzette — Beam Me Up (Beam Me Up (Single), 2012)**
Bouncy, vocal-driven electro house with a clean, energetic payoff. It's playful on top, heavy underneath—the genre's sweet spot.

## Late mainstage electro house & big-riff hybrids (2013–2015)

**Martin Garrix — Animals (Animals (Single), 2013)**
A defining big-riff electro-house moment: stripped arrangement, iconic lead, enormous drop impact. It helped set the festival template for the mid-2010s.

**Showtek & Justin Prime — Cannonball (Cannonball (Single), 2013)**
Pure mainstage electro house: one massive riff, tight build, maximum slam. It's engineered to detonate in big rooms.

**DVBBS & Borgeous — Tsunami (Tsunami (Single), 2013)**
A festival weapon with a huge, aggressive lead and minimal distractions. It's electro house reduced to its most physical essentials.

**MAKJ — Springen (Springen (Single), 2013)**
Punchy, Dutch-influenced electro house with a direct drop and clean drum programming. It's built for mixing and impact, not ornament.

**Deorro — Five Hours (Five Hours (Single), 2014)**
Long-form, DJ-friendly electro house built around a memorable riff and extended energy control. It works as both a peak-time hit and a mixing tool.

**Tujamo — Drop That Low (When I Dip) (Drop That Low (When I Dip) (Single), 2014)**
Bounce-forward electro house with a callout vocal and a drop designed for movement. It keeps the genre's "brash fun" intact while sounding modern.

**Nitro Fun — New Game (New Game (Single), 2014)**
Chiptune flavor + electro-house drop architecture—bright, fast, and extremely clean. It shows the genre can be playful without losing power.

**Rogue — Exogenesis (Exogenesis (Single), 2014)**
Melodic on top, but still driven by a proper electro-house kick/bass engine. The drop hits with clarity rather than sheer distortion.

**Stonebank — Blast From The Past (Blast From The Past (Single), 2014)**
Retro-gaming sound design fused with modern electro-house slam. The arrangement is pure "build → drop," executed with precision.

**F.O.O.L — Highway (Highway (Single), 2014)**
Darker, cinematic electro house with a driving bassline and a big, clean drop. It's moodier than earlier eras, but still unmistakably electro house.

**Botnek — Deeper Love (Deeper Love (Single), 2014)**
Heavy stabs, strong groove, and a drop that prioritizes weight over melody. It's electro house as a blunt instrument—perfectly controlled.

**Henry Fong — Rave Tool (Rave Tool (Single), 2014)**
A functional "tool track" built around a simple hook and maximum club usability. It's designed to be dropped into sets as instant energy.

**Ummet Ozcan — The Hum (The Hum (Single), 2015)**
A sticky vocal motif paired with a tough, mainstage-ready drop. It's a good example of electro house staying hooky without becoming progressive-house.

**VINAI — Bounce Generation (Bounce Generation (Single), 2013)**
High-energy, stomping electro house aimed squarely at festivals. The drop is blunt and physical—classic "hands up, then jump" design.

**Dannic — Clobber (Clobber (Single), 2014)**
A clean, punchy mainroom electro-house cut built around drop efficiency. Everything is there to hit hard and mix easily.

## Modern loyalists (2021)

**Mike Cervello & Curbi — The Drum (The Drum (Single), 2021)**
A modern mainstage electro-house cut that keeps the genre's classic kick/bass pump and drop-first logic. It's contemporary in mix, traditional in structure.

**BounceMakers, Raven & Kreyn — Ready (Ready (Single), 2021)**
Clean, big-lead electro house with a tight build and a straightforward payoff. It's designed for immediate DJ usability.

**Mike Candys — Vibe (Vibe (Single), 2021)**
Groove-forward electro house that focuses on a simple hook and steady dancefloor pressure. It's less about complexity, more about function.

**Tommie Sunshine & Fahjah — Get Down (Get Down (Single), 2021)**
Party-energy electro house with classic vocal framing and a drop engineered to lift the room. It nods to earlier eras while sounding updated.

**DEKOVA — Imma' Be (Imma' Be (Single), 2021)**
A modern rework approach: recognizable top idea translated into a mainstage electro-house drop. The impact comes from bass weight and clean arrangement.

**Hotcaller — Drowning (RIP) (Drowning (RIP) (Single), 2021)**
Darker electro-house mood with a heavier, more aggressive drop color. It shows how the style persists by leaning into weight and attitude.

**Cleez — Everybody's Free (To Feel Good) (Everybody's Free (To Feel Good) (Single), 2021)**
A classic-hook reinterpretation built around electro-house drop mechanics. It's a reminder that the genre still loves big, recognizable moments.

**MARTEN HØRGER & Donkong — Believe In Me (Believe In Me (Single), 2021)**
Mainstage electro house with extra bounce and a modern low-end presentation. The structure stays faithful: build tension, then hit the drop hard.

**Costel Van Dein — Bylbo4ka (Bylbo4ka (Single), 2021)**
A more leftfield modern entry that still centers the electro-house drop and bass impact. It's electro house surviving through fresh sound choices.

**Stephan F — Sax! (Sax! (Single), 2021)**
A classic "instrument hook + drop" electro-house formula in current form. It's simple, loud, and built to land instantly in a club mix.
