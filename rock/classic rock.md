# Classic Rock

## Foundations (1964–1969)

**The Kinks — You Really Got Me (Kinks, 1964)**
A two-chord riff so influential it's practically a blueprint for hard-edged guitar rock. Its distortion and power-chord insistence shaped the core guitar language that classic rock stations still treat as ground zero.

**The Animals — House of the Rising Sun (The Animals, 1964)**
An old folk ballad transformed into electrified rock drama with a signature organ line and unforgettable vocal phrasing. It's a key British Invasion track that broadened what mainstream rock songs could sound like.

**The Who — My Generation (My Generation, 1965)**
A foundational rock anthem that put youth identity and loud guitar power at the center of the genre's self-image. The stuttering vocal hook and raw band attack directly prefigure classic rock's arena-sized shout-alongs.

**The Yardbirds — Heart Full of Soul (Having a Rave Up with The Yardbirds, 1965)**
An early milestone in the move from blues-based rock toward fuzz, raga-inflected textures and more adventurous riff writing. It sits in the pre-classic-rock moment where radio-friendly hooks meet expanding guitar sonics.

**The Beach Boys — Wouldn't It Be Nice (Pet Sounds, 1966)**
Studio perfectionism meets rock exuberance: layered harmonies and inventive production serve a relentlessly optimistic hook. It's a foundational example of how classic rock's canon includes high craft, not just grit.

**Cream — Sunshine of Your Love (Disraeli Gears, 1967)**
A prototypical power-trio statement: huge riff, thick tone, and a chorus built for mass singalong. It's also a decisive step toward the 'guitar hero' aesthetics that dominate classic rock canons.

**The Doors — Light My Fire (The Doors, 1967)**
An FM-rock cornerstone that proved extended, improvisation-friendly tracks could still be pop hits. The organ-led arrangement and dramatic dynamic arc became a model for album rock's crossover into radio staples.

**Van Morrison — Brown Eyed Girl (Blowin' Your Mind!, 1967)**
Perfectly distilled pop-soul rock with an instantly recognizable riff and a vocal that feels conversational and intimate. Classic rock radio embraced it as proof that 'classic' can mean bright, melodic, and timeless.

**Procol Harum — A Whiter Shade of Pale (Procol Harum, 1967)**
Baroque organ textures and enigmatic imagery helped widen rock's expressive vocabulary beyond blues and teen romance. It's an early classic-rock staple for its dramatic build and instantly iconic melodic hook.

**The Moody Blues — Nights in White Satin (Days of Future Passed, 1967)**
A cornerstone of symphonic-leaning album rock, pairing lush orchestration with a dark, yearning vocal. Its long-form feel and recurring motifs helped define the 'serious album track' identity later central to classic rock.

**Traffic — Dear Mr. Fantasy (Mr. Fantasy, 1967)**
A moody, groove-centered album cut that became a radio staple, illustrating how the late '60s moved rock toward longer forms. Its interplay of organ, guitar and vocal phrasing is classic-rock atmosphere in embryo.

**The Jimi Hendrix Experience — All Along the Watchtower (Electric Ladyland, 1968)**
Hendrix's definitive cover: dense studio craft, a searing lead tone, and rhythmic precision that rewrote what rock guitar could do in three-and-a-half minutes. It's a permanent classic-rock touchstone for reinterpretation-as-innovation.

**Janis Joplin — Piece of My Heart (Cheap Thrills, 1968)**
Janis's vocal is a masterclass in blues-derived intensity, turning a pop-soul song into raw rock catharsis. The performance helped establish the 'big voice' frontperson archetype that classic rock celebrates.

**The Band — The Weight (Music from Big Pink, 1968)**
A roots-rock hymn with a communal, band-in-a-room feel that influenced countless classic-rock singer-songwriters and Americana-leaning acts. Its storytelling realism and stacked harmonies also forecast the 1970s album-rock ethos.

**Steppenwolf — Born to Be Wild (Steppenwolf, 1968)**
Often cited as the moment 'heavy metal thunder' entered the mainstream vocabulary, it's a hard-driving rocker built for loud playback. Its motorik riff and shoutable chorus made it an enduring classic-rock anthem.

**The Guess Who — These Eyes (Wheatfield Soul, 1968)**
A sophisticated, harmony-rich hit that helped carry Canadian rock into the classic-rock story. Its blend of soul inflection and rock arrangement became a recurring FM-radio sweet spot.

**The Beatles — Come Together (Abbey Road, 1969)**
One of the defining late‑period Beatles grooves: a swampy bassline, clipped rhythm guitar and a vocal that feels both bluesy and modern. It helped codify the album-oriented, riff-led side of classic rock rather than the earlier singles-only pop model.

**The Rolling Stones — Gimme Shelter (Let It Bleed, 1969)**
Built on one of rock's most ominous riffs, it captures the late‑'60s turn toward heavier, darker, more cinematic rock songwriting. Its apocalyptic mood and dynamic build became a template for classic‑rock radio's 'serious' side.

**Creedence Clearwater Revival — Fortunate Son (Willy and the Poor Boys, 1969)**
A tight, riffy protest song whose punchy brevity stands out amid late-'60s excess. It remains central to classic rock because it marries social commentary to instantly memorable, guitar-driven craft.

**Santana — Evil Ways (Santana, 1969)**
A defining early Latin-rock crossover: blues-rock guitar phrasing rides on a percussive, danceable groove. It expanded classic rock's palette without leaving the guitar-led center.

## Album-Rock Expansion (1970–1974)

**Black Sabbath — Paranoid (Paranoid, 1970)**
A compact distillation of heaviness—monolithic riffing with pop-level memorability. It's pivotal to classic rock's harder edge, bridging blues-based rock into metal-adjacent intensity without leaving radio.

**Derek and the Dominos — Layla (Layla and Other Assorted Love Songs, 1970)**
One of rock's definitive tragic love narratives, powered by a riff that locks into the song's emotional urgency. The track's structure and production set a benchmark for melodrama that still lives in classic rock balladry.

**Chicago — 25 or 6 to 4 (Chicago II, 1970)**
A brass-driven rock classic that showed horns could be as forceful and riff-like as guitars. Its shifting meters and extended form helped normalize 'album-length thinking' in mainstream rock.

**Led Zeppelin — Stairway to Heaven (Led Zeppelin IV, 1971)**
A quintessential album-rock epic that made the long-form rock track a mainstream expectation. Its gradual escalation from folkish intimacy to amplified climax became a defining classic-rock narrative arc.

**David Bowie — Changes (Hunky Dory, 1971)**
A pivot-point between singer-songwriter introspection and glam-era theatricality, built on a hook that feels instantly familiar. Classic rock leans on it to mark the early '70s moment when rock absorbed art-pop sophistication.

**T. Rex — Bang a Gong (Get It On) (Electric Warrior, 1971)**
Glam sparkle with a pounding backbeat and a proto-disco handclap feel, yet still unmistakably rock. It captures classic rock's ability to fuse pop immediacy with guitar-driven swagger.

**Elton John — Tiny Dancer (Madman Across the Water, 1971)**
A masterclass in wide-screen melodic writing and emotional restraint, anchored by a piano part that feels orchestral. It's classic rock's soft power: big feeling without sacrificing musicianship or depth.

**Rod Stewart — Maggie May (Every Picture Tells a Story, 1971)**
Often treated as the template for the confessional rock hit—folk storytelling translated into band-driven radio. Its groove and melodic rise turned the 'singer as narrator' into a classic-rock norm.

**Jethro Tull — Aqualung (Aqualung, 1971)**
A riff-heavy meditation on faith, fatigue and social observation, balancing acoustic nuance with electric heft. It's classic rock's quintessential early-'70s realism: grand themes, grounded textures.

**Yes — Roundabout (Fragile, 1971)**
Yes at their most accessible: virtuosic, but anchored by a bass riff and chorus that stick on first listen. It became a classic-rock gateway to progressive musicianship without the barrier of long suites.

**Deep Purple — Smoke on the Water (Machine Head, 1972)**
Perhaps the most famous riff in rock history, turning a real-world tour anecdote into myth. Its simplicity, tone and singable chorus exemplify why guitar hooks rule classic rock programming.

**Neil Young — Heart of Gold (Harvest, 1972)**
A deceptively simple song that became the era's most famous acoustic-to-FM crossover. Its intimacy, harmonica color and universal hook helped define the 'album track as hit single' model.

**The Doobie Brothers — Listen to the Music (Toulouse Street, 1972)**
A clean, mid-tempo groove that helped cement the sunny, riff-led West Coast side of classic rock. It's also a key example of 1970s radio rock leaning into rhythmic lightness without losing guitar identity.

**Steely Dan — Reelin' in the Years (Can't Buy a Thrill, 1972)**
Steely Dan's polished cynicism meets muscular guitar leads, embodying the 'smart' side of classic rock. Its tight arrangement and sardonic lyric show how sophistication can still be riff-forward and radio-ready.

**Alice Cooper — School's Out (School's Out, 1972)**
A shock-rock anthem that turned teenage ritual into rock spectacle, with a chorus engineered for mass chanting. It's essential classic rock as cultural event—where attitude is as important as harmony.

**Aerosmith — Dream On (Aerosmith, 1973)**
Aerosmith's breakthrough ballad that merges bluesy vocal drama with arena-scale guitar textures. It helped define the classic-rock power ballad's emotional and sonic blueprint.

**Lynyrd Skynyrd — Free Bird ((Pronounced 'Lĕh-'nérd 'Skin-'nérd), 1973)**
A monumental Southern-rock-adjacent statement that classic rock canonizes for its slow-burn build and cathartic guitar coda. Even in edited form, it epitomizes the era's faith in long, emotional rock narratives.

**The Allman Brothers Band — Ramblin' Man (Brothers and Sisters, 1973)**
A relaxed, melodic hit that helped carry jam-band energy into mainstream radio. Its feel-good swing and twin-guitar conversation made it a staple of classic-rock road-trip playlists.

**ZZ Top — La Grange (Tres Hombres, 1973)**
A greasy Texas shuffle built on one of the most imitated riffs in bar-band history. It's classic rock as pure groove: minimal changes, maximum attitude, and a guitar tone everyone recognizes.

**Paul McCartney & Wings — Band on the Run (Band on the Run, 1973)**
A cinematic narrative of escape with tight songwriting discipline—proof that 'classic rock' also means story craft. The track's dynamic pacing and chorus lift made it a cornerstone of 1970s album-rock ambition.

**Billy Joel — Piano Man (Piano Man, 1973)**
A piano-led portrait of the barroom underclass that became a singalong standard. Classic rock embraces it for turning everyday detail into myth, with a melody designed for communal memory.

**Pink Floyd — Time (The Dark Side of the Moon, 1973)**
An archetypal 'serious' album track—precise arrangement, philosophical lyric, and a climax engineered for big-room replay. It's central to classic rock because it rewards loud listening and repeated attention.

**Queen — Killer Queen (Sheer Heart Attack, 1974)**
A perfect example of Queen's ability to compress theatricality into a crisp, radio-sized single. It's classic rock because it fuses virtuoso arrangement detail with an instantly memorable chorus.

**Bad Company — Can't Get Enough (Bad Company, 1974)**
A straightforward, riff-first rocker that helped mid-'70s 'supergroup' rock feel inevitable on FM radio. Its economy and punch show classic rock's preference for big hooks over complexity when the song demands it.

**Genesis — The Carpet Crawlers (The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway, 1974)**
A melodic centerpiece from a rock opera that demonstrates progressive rock's narrative ambition in song form. Its recurring theme and emotive vocal make it one of the most 'classic-rock playable' moments of prog.

## FM Golden Age (1975–1979)

**Bruce Springsteen — Born to Run (Born to Run, 1975)**
A defining heartland-rock anthem that crystallized classic rock's romance with cars, escape, and possibility. Its cinematic arrangement and singable chorus made it a permanent FM-radio ritual.

**Foghat — Slow Ride (Fool for the City, 1975)**
A slow-burning riff-and-groove juggernaut designed for maximum loudspeaker impact. It's classic rock's bar-band engine room: extended form, simple harmony, and a hook that lives in the rhythm section.

**Nazareth — Love Hurts (Hair of the Dog, 1975)**
A definitive 1970s hard-luck ballad where raspy vocal drama meets a chorus that feels inevitable. Classic rock kept it in heavy rotation as the genre's blueprint for emotional weight without softening the guitar core.

**Gary Wright — Dream Weaver (The Dream Weaver, 1975)**
A dreamlike, keyboard-forward rock single that still 'plays' like classic rock because the groove and melody are so strong. It captures the mid‑'70s FM taste for atmosphere, big hooks, and radio-friendly mystique.

**Eagles — Hotel California (Hotel California, 1976)**
Classic rock's definitive 'mythic America' song: lush guitars, narrative lyric, and a chorus that invites communal catharsis. Its extended outro also embodies the era's love of guitar solos as storytelling.

**Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers — American Girl (Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers, 1976)**
A lean, jangly rocker that bridges 1960s pop immediacy with 1970s album-rock bite. It's essential to classic rock for its perfectly balanced riff, tempo, and youthful urgency.

**Boston — More Than a Feeling (Boston, 1976)**
A near-perfect power-pop-meets-arena-ambition single that became a core classic-rock radio pillar. Its chorus uplift and guitar layering distilled the 'FM anthem' into four minutes.

**Kansas — Carry On Wayward Son (Leftoverture, 1976)**
A progressive-leaning hit that still behaves like a classic-rock single: sharp riffs, dramatic stops, and a chorus that lands hard. It's a key case of virtuosity made radio-friendly.

**Peter Frampton — Show Me the Way (Frampton Comes Alive!, 1976)**
A live-album signature that proved concert recordings could become definitive versions for radio audiences. Its talk-box guitar and call-and-response structure embody classic rock's love of performance spectacle.

**Thin Lizzy — The Boys Are Back in Town (Jailbreak, 1976)**
A pub-rock swagger anthem that became an international shorthand for rock's 'we're back in town' bravado. The twin-guitar interplay and chant-ready chorus make it a perennial classic-rock opener.

**Blue Öyster Cult — (Don't Fear) The Reaper (Agents of Fortune, 1976)**
Atmospheric riffing meets pop-song clarity, creating a blueprint for the 'mysterious' side of classic rock. Its chorus and chiming lead figure became a universal reference point for guitar-driven mood.

**Steve Miller Band — Fly Like an Eagle (Fly Like an Eagle, 1976)**
An airplane-hangar-wide groove that still feels nimble, built on crisp rhythm guitar and a floating chorus. It's a classic-rock staple because it blends funk-like pocket with rock tone and scale.

**Bob Seger — Night Moves (Night Moves, 1976)**
A reflective, mid-tempo classic that helped define 'night-drive' rock as a lyrical and sonic mood. Its narrative detail and slow build are core to classic rock's nostalgia engine.

**Fleetwood Mac — Go Your Own Way (Rumours, 1977)**
A perfect fusion of rhythmic drive and melodic melancholy, anchored by a guitar line that feels both biting and elegant. It's a peak example of 1970s classic rock craftsmanship: tight, emotional, and endlessly replayable.

**Electric Light Orchestra — Mr. Blue Sky (Out of the Blue, 1977)**
Orchestral pop-rock exuberance at stadium scale, but with meticulous arrangement detail. It's classic rock because it proves maximalism can still be hook-driven and instantly recognizable.

**Foreigner — Cold as Ice (Foreigner, 1977)**
A cold, minimal riff and icy vocal attitude that helped define late-'70s rock's harder, more modern sheen. It's classic rock's bridge from blues roots to sharper, more urban AOR textures.

**Styx — Come Sail Away (The Grand Illusion, 1977)**
A grand, melodic anthem that embodies the late-'70s belief in uplift as a rock virtue. The piano-led intro-to-chorus arc is a classic-rock radio structure in miniature.

**Heart — Barracuda (Little Queen, 1977)**
A feminist-tinged hard-rock warning shot with one of the decade's most famous bass/guitar hooks. It's classic rock at its most visceral: sharp riff, sharper attitude, no wasted motion.

**Meat Loaf — Bat Out of Hell (Bat Out of Hell, 1977)**
Operatic melodrama turned into tight rock structure, with a chorus that feels like theater in a bar. It's classic rock because it elevates excess into unforgettable form and keeps the riffs central.

**Jackson Browne — Running on Empty (Running on Empty, 1977)**
One of the definitive 'life on the road' documents, with a chorus that feels like a deep breath on the highway. Classic rock values it as a bridge between singer-songwriter intimacy and full-band propulsion.

**REO Speedwagon — Roll with the Changes (You Can Tune a Piano, but You Can't Tuna Fish, 1978)**
A tempo-shift showcase that turned band precision into pure adrenaline, with a chorus built for shouted participation. It's a classic rock staple for translating 'album musicianship' into stadium energy.

**Journey — Wheel in the Sky (Infinity, 1978)**
A wide-screen AOR single with a skybound chorus and instantly identifiable guitar motif. It's classic rock because it's engineered for repeat play—clean lines, big feelings, and impeccable pacing.

**Warren Zevon — Werewolves of London (Excitable Boy, 1978)**
A wry, cinematic rocker whose chorus became a cultural catchphrase. It's classic rock as storytelling-by-groove: sly lyric, relaxed tempo, and a riff that sounds like a grin.

**Dire Straits — Sultans of Swing (Dire Straits, 1978)**
A guitar-pop classic that made understated virtuosity feel effortless. Its fingerpicked riff, conversational vocal and dynamic restraint became a key strand of classic rock's 'tasteful' tradition.

**Toto — Hold the Line (Toto, 1978)**
A hard-driving piano riff paired with massive chorus harmonies—early evidence that AOR could be both tough and melodic. It's classic rock because it sits perfectly between bar-band grit and radio polish.

**Van Halen — Runnin' with the Devil (Van Halen, 1978)**
A debut statement of modern guitar heroics, with brown-sound tone and a rhythm section that swings. It's a cornerstone of classic rock's late‑'70s shift toward flashier, high-energy playing.

**Cheap Trick — Surrender (Heaven Tonight, 1978)**
A power-pop gem that classic rock adopted because the chorus is unbeatable and the guitars stay upfront. It's proof the format's canon rewards perfect songs even when they're tightly compact.

**Gerry Rafferty — Baker Street (City to City, 1978)**
A sax-led noir single that became shorthand for late-'70s urban romanticism on rock radio. It's classic rock because its melody, production atmosphere and solo are instantly time-stamping yet timeless.

**Supertramp — The Logical Song (Breakfast in America, 1979)**
A sleek, mid-tempo lament with a chorus engineered for mass singalong and late-night radio. It represents the late-'70s moment when classic rock absorbed pop clarity without losing instrumental authority.

**AC/DC — Highway to Hell (Highway to Hell, 1979)**
A blunt, perfectly titled anthem that signaled classic rock's harder turn at the end of the decade. Its riff and chorus are engineered for volume—exactly how the format wants to be heard.

## Radio Era & Late Classics (1980–1985)

**Pat Benatar — Hit Me with Your Best Shot (Crimes of Passion, 1980)**
A lean, no-nonsense hit that made female-fronted hard rock unavoidable on mainstream radio. Its punchy guitar figure and call-to-arms chorus are classic rock's 'FM fighter' archetype.

**Rush — Tom Sawyer (Moving Pictures, 1981)**
A perfect example of early‑'80s 'precision rock': odd meters made catchy through riffs and a huge chorus. It cemented progressive musicianship as something classic rock radio could still program in heavy rotation.

**Joan Jett & the Blackhearts — I Love Rock 'n Roll (I Love Rock 'n Roll, 1981)**
A cover that became the definitive version for many listeners, built on brute simplicity and pure shout-along energy. It's classic rock because it distills the genre to its core promise: loud fun with a giant hook.

**Billy Squier — The Stroke (Don't Say No, 1981)**
Billy Squier's prime riff-rock calling card: a groove that struts and a chorus that explodes without overcomplication. It helped define the early‑'80s classic-rock sound as leaner, tighter, and more drum-forward.

**The J. Geils Band — Centerfold (Freeze-Frame, 1981)**
A cheeky, riff-led pop-rock smash that shows how early‑'80s classic rock could be danceable without losing guitars. Its stop-start hook and shoutable chorus made it a perennial party staple on rock radio.

**John Cougar Mellencamp — Hurts So Good (American Fool, 1982)**
A heartland-rock crossover that turned blue-collar optimism into anthemic radio form. Classic rock holds it as a bridge from 1970s album storytelling to 1980s hit-making efficiency.

**The Alan Parsons Project — Eye in the Sky (Eye in the Sky, 1982)**
A sleek, cinematic soft-AOR classic that shows the format's taste for polished mood pieces. Its chorus and vocal restraint make it a staple for late-night classic-rock programming.

**Asia — Heat of the Moment (Asia, 1982)**
A key 'supergroup' moment where prog chops are compressed into pure radio adrenaline. Classic rock embraced it because the riffs are immediate even as the playing is virtuosic.

**Def Leppard — Photograph (Pyromania, 1983)**
A stadium-sized chorus wrapped around crisp guitar layers and a meticulously engineered drum sound. It's a signature of 1980s classic rock's shift toward high-gloss production without losing riff priority.

**Night Ranger — Sister Christian (Midnight Madness, 1983)**
A power-ballad apex: chiming intro, huge chorus, and a slow-build structure engineered for lighters-in-the-air catharsis. Classic rock embraces it as the moment 1980s melody and production scale fully met arena emotion.

**Stevie Ray Vaughan and Double Trouble — Pride and Joy (Texas Flood, 1983)**
A modern blues-rock benchmark that reasserted feel, tone and swagger in the era of gated drums. Its guitar phrasing and shuffle groove keep it permanently in classic rock rotation.

**Scorpions — Rock You Like a Hurricane (Love at First Sting, 1984)**
Germany's most iconic classic-rock export: a riff built to be chanted and a chorus that detonates on impact. It captures the 1980s appetite for bigger hooks, bigger drums, and bigger guitar tone.

**Bryan Adams — Summer of '69 (Reckless, 1984)**
A nostalgia narrative that became a communal singalong in bars, cars and stadiums, regardless of when it was written. It's classic rock because it mythologizes rock's own past in a hook everyone knows.

**Sammy Hagar — I Can't Drive 55 (VOA, 1984)**
A pure, grin-in-the-wind rocker built around a one-line premise and relentless momentum. It's classic rock as lifestyle anthem—guitars upfront, drums big, and a chorus made for open-road volume.

**Don Henley — The Boys of Summer (Building the Perfect Beast, 1984)**
Don Henley's definitive solo single: glossy production, narrative lyric, and a chorus that feels like sunburnt memory. It's classic rock's 1980s version of California myth-making—cleaner, but still guitar-led.

## Coda & Legacy (1986–1991)

**Bon Jovi — Livin' on a Prayer (Slippery When Wet, 1986)**
A peak-'80s arena-rock single that classic rock stations adopted as a forever anthem. The talk-box hook and towering chorus show how the format's 'classic' era expanded into late‑'80s pop-metal songcraft.

**Europe — The Final Countdown (The Final Countdown, 1986)**
An instantly recognizable synth fanfare paired with a rock chorus that became a global sporting-event staple. It's classic rock because it shows how the genre's anthem-making migrated into glossy mid‑'80s production.

**Guns N' Roses — Sweet Child o' Mine (Appetite for Destruction, 1987)**
A hard-rock ballad with a pop-perfect chorus and one of the most famous opening riffs in rock. It's crucial to the classic-rock story as the moment late‑'80s guitar bands reclaimed radio dominance.

**Whitesnake — Here I Go Again (Whitesnake, 1987)**
A power ballad built on a gospel-like build and a chorus designed for mass catharsis. Classic rock programming keeps it as the definitive example of late‑'80s big-hair rock emotional engineering.

**INXS — Need You Tonight (Kick, 1987)**
A sleek dance-rock crossover that kept guitars in the mix while leaning into funk and club rhythm. It's a late‑'80s 'modern classic' that many classic‑rock stations use to mark the genre's expansion beyond straight blues-based rock.

**Extreme — More Than Words (Extreme II: Pornograffitti, 1990)**
Acoustic intimacy turned into a mainstream rock event, proving classic rock could still value vulnerability alongside riffs. Its fingerpicked hook and harmony writing helped define early‑'90s radio's softer edge.

**The Black Crowes — Hard to Handle (Shake Your Money Maker, 1990)**
A bar-band classic revived for a new decade, connecting 1970s roots rock to 1990s mainstream rock tastes. Classic rock embraced it because it sounds 'timeless'—riffy, loose, and vocally immediate.

**Queensrÿche — Silent Lucidity (Empire, 1990)**
A prog-metal band's crossover moment: introspective lyric, widescreen chorus, and a guitar solo designed for classic-rock radio. It shows how the format's borders can expand when the songwriting is unmistakably strong.

**Tom Cochrane — Life Is a Highway (Mad Mad World, 1991)**
A highway metaphor made literal: big chorus, driving rhythm, and a hook that fits the classic-rock road-song tradition perfectly. It's a late entry to the canon that many stations treat like an older standard.

**Lenny Kravitz — It Ain't Over 'til It's Over (Mama Said, 1991)**
A modern throwback that filtered soul, funk and classic rock through crisp early‑'90s production. The melody and groove place it in the lineage of 1970s radio rock even as the sheen is contemporary.
