# Metalcore

## 1991–1999: Metallic Hardcore Foundations

**Starkweather — Murder in Technicolor (Crossbearer, 1992)**
Chaotic, dissonant riffing and abrupt tempo pivots make this a key proto-document for metalcore's abrasive, technical side. It showed early on that metalcore could be as structurally adventurous as it was heavy.

**Earth Crisis — Firestorm (Firestorm (EP), 1993)**
An era-defining anthem of 90s metalcore that married militant hardcore cadence to crushing, downtuned metal riffs. Its stop-start breakdown logic became a blueprint for countless metallic-hardcore bands.

**Merauder — Master Killer (Master Killer, 1995)**
Thick, groove-laden chugs and sledgehammer breakdowns made this a cornerstone of beatdown-leaning metalcore. It's still a reference point for the genre's toughest, most physical riff language.

**Vision of Disorder — Element (Vision of Disorder, 1996)**
A defining example of metalcore separating from straight hardcore via heavier riffing and more expansive song dynamics. The track's mix of urgency and metallic weight helped normalize the genre's hybrid identity.

**Overcast — Apocalypse Upon Us (Fight Ambition to Kill, 1997)**
An early bridge between Gothenburg-style melody and hardcore breakdown architecture, pointing straight toward melodic metalcore. Its harmonized lead sensibility became a major ingredient in the next wave.

**Shai Hulud — My Heart Bleeds the Darkest Blood (Hearts Once Nourished with Hope and Compassion, 1997)**
A cornerstone of melodic, thoughtful metalcore writing built on rapid-fire riffs and impassioned delivery. It helped prove the genre could be intricate and lyrical without sacrificing impact.

**Strife — Blistered (In This Defiance, 1997)**
A hard-driving, metallic hardcore track whose bounce and breakdown placement made it a live-set staple. It illustrates the late-90s pathway from hardcore scenes into metalcore's heavier, riff-led form.

**Cave In — Juggernaut (Until Your Heart Stops, 1998)**
A heavy, riff-dense statement from the metallic-hardcore branch of metalcore, emphasizing forward momentum and crushing stops. It influenced later bands that prized heaviness and musicianship over polish.

**Morning Again — Murder You Call War (As Tradition Dies Slowly, 1998)**
Relentless tempos and brutally efficient breakdown drops capture the Florida/SE U.S. heavy-hardcore current that fed metalcore's evolution. It's a prime example of extreme intensity delivered with hardcore discipline.

**Zao — To Think of You Is to Treasure an Absent Memory (Where Blood and Fire Bring Rest, 1998)**
A key track showing metalcore's capacity for dark atmosphere, dynamics, and emotional weight alongside crushing riffs. It became a touchstone for bands exploring mood and narrative intensity within metalcore.

**All Out War — Truth in the Age of Lies (For Those Who Were Crucified, 1999)**
Thrash-derived riffing welded to hardcore rhythm and breakdown structure makes this a prototypical crossover metalcore cut. Its balance of speed and stomp influenced many riff-forward metalcore approaches.

**Arkangel — Within the Walls of Babylon (Prayers Upon Deaf Ears, 1999)**
A flagship H8000-era European metalcore track built on massive grooves and bleak, oppressive tone. It highlights how the genre's hardcore/metal hybrid evolved differently across scenes outside the U.S.

**Disembodied — 7 Stitches (Heretic, 1999)**
An ultra-heavy, breakdown-centric early blueprint for the brutally downtuned 2000s sound. Its emphasis on riff weight and rhythmic violence became foundational for mosh-driven metalcore.

**Poison the Well — Nerdy (The Opposite of December… A Season of Separation, 1999)**
A landmark track that helped steer metalcore toward sharper structures and heightened emotional volatility. Its stop-start riffs and cathartic dynamics shaped the genre's early-2000s vocabulary.

**Turmoil — The Discipline of Self Loathing (The Process of…, 1999)**
A harsh, abrasive document of late-90s metalcore where hardcore chaos meets metallic heft. It's influential for its swing-heavy breakdown feel and relentless vocal intensity.

**Undying — This Day All Gods Die (This Day All Gods Die, 1999)**
A key melodic metalcore blueprint that integrates twin-lead harmonies and tremolo picking into hardcore breakdown architecture. It helped crystallize the sound that would dominate the genre's next decade.

## 2000–2004: Second Wave Codification

**Converge — Concubine (Jane Doe, 2001)**
A ferocious standard-bearer for chaotic metalcore: dissonant riffing, frantic pace, and zero wasted motion. It raised the bar for intensity and became a benchmark for aggressive, structurally daring metalcore.

**Hatebreed — I Will Be Heard (Perseverance, 2002)**
A stripped-down, stomping anthem that distills metalcore into chunky riffs, gang-shout energy, and massive breakdown momentum. Its simplicity and attitude made it a gateway track for the genre's tougher side.

**Killswitch Engage — My Last Serenade (Alive or Just Breathing, 2002)**
A defining melodic metalcore track, pairing harmonized leads and tight breakdowns with a massive, memorable hook. It helped codify the genre's 'melody + violence' formula for the mainstream era.

**Norma Jean — Memphis Will Be Laid to Waste (Bless the Martyr and Kiss the Child, 2002)**
Chaotic riffs and breakdown-driven pacing capture the early-2000s surge of raw, unpredictable metalcore. Its abrasive energy and scene-crossing feature made it a touchstone for that turbulent second-wave sound.

**As I Lay Dying — 94 Hours (Frail Words Collapse, 2003)**
A textbook example of modern melodic metalcore, fusing fast tremolo riffs and harmonized leads with decisive breakdown drops. It helped set the template for the 2000s wave of riff-focused, Gothenburg-influenced metalcore.

**Avenged Sevenfold — Unholy Confessions (Waking the Fallen, 2003)**
A key early-2000s track that brought dual-guitar harmony and metal technique into a metalcore frame. It showed how the genre could sound 'metal' in a classic sense while keeping hardcore rhythm and breakdown logic.

**Bleeding Through — Love Lost in a Hail of Gunfire (This Is Love, This Is Murderous, 2003)**
A signature blend of metalcore brutality and dark, atmospheric flourish, expanding the genre's emotional palette. Its dramatic pacing and crushing heaviness became a fan-canon reference point.

**Darkest Hour — Veritas, Aequitas (Hidden Hands of a Sadist Nation, 2003)**
A razor-sharp showcase of melodic lead craft inside a fully metalcore structure. It demonstrates how melodic death metal influence could intensify metalcore without eclipsing breakdown-driven impact.

**Every Time I Die — Ebolarama (Hot Damn!, 2003)**
A swaggering, punk-charged take on metalcore that keeps hardcore attitude front and center. Its riff hooks and breakdown tension helped define the genre's rock 'n' roll-leaning branch.

**Atreyu — Lip Gloss and Black (The Curse, 2004)**
A hook-forward metalcore single that popularized scream/clean contrast and chorus-first writing. It helped cement the genre's radio-friendly architecture without abandoning breakdown payoffs.

**Bury Your Dead — Losin' It (Cover Your Tracks, 2004)**
A pure breakdown anthem that represents beatdown-flavored metalcore at its most direct. Its crowd-commanding simplicity made it a cornerstone for hardcore-leaning, pit-centric metalcore.

**Caliban — The Beloved and the Hatred (The Opposite from Within, 2004)**
A tight, aggressive statement of early-2000s European metalcore, balancing sharp riffs and big breakdowns with urgency. It's emblematic of how quickly the genre globalized beyond its U.S. origins.

**Eighteen Visions — Tower of Snakes (Obsession, 2004)**
Sleek groove, theatrical delivery, and heavy breakdown punctuation made this a defining 'scene era' metalcore track. It influenced the genre's stylistic presentation as much as its sound.

**God Forbid — Antihero (Gone Forever, 2004)**
Thrashy riffing and melodic lead work meet disciplined breakdown structure in a highly refined metalcore package. It highlights the NJ/NY axis as a major engine of musicianship-forward metalcore.

**Haste the Day — The Closest Thing to Closure (Burning Bridges, 2004)**
Concise, emotionally charged metalcore that pairs melodic hooks with cleanly placed breakdowns. It represents the mid-2000s move toward tighter songwriting while staying unmistakably heavy.

**Heaven Shall Burn — The Weapon They Fear (Antigone, 2004)**
A relentless, politically charged melodic metalcore track built on precision riffing and uncompromising intensity. It became a flagship for the German scene's world-class contribution to the genre.

**It Dies Today — A Threnody for Modern Romance (The Caitiff Choir, 2004)**
A dramatic, chorus-oriented piece that captures the 2004–05 swing toward bigger, more anthemic melodic metalcore. Its theatrical tension-and-release is classic metalcore dynamics done with polish.

**Martyr A.D. — American Hollow (On Earth As It Is in Hell, 2004)**
Crushing low-end groove and bleak atmosphere exemplify the heavier, mosh-centric strand of early-2000s metalcore. It's a key reference for bands that prioritize weight and breakdown gravity.

**Shadows Fall — The Light That Blinds (The War Within, 2004)**
A gateway-era track that helped define the 'new wave' sound around metalcore, built on modern riffcraft and adrenaline. It sits near the metalcore/modern-metal line while still speaking the genre's rhythmic language.

**Unearth — My Will Be Done (The Oncoming Storm, 2004)**
An archetype of melodic metalcore: twin-guitar harmonies, fast tremolo riffs, and perfectly timed breakdown drops. Its guitar-forward approach influenced generations of riff-centric metalcore bands.

## 2005–2009: Mainstream Peak & Global Expansion

**Bullet for My Valentine — Tears Don't Fall (The Poison, 2005)**
A polished melodic metalcore hit that proved the genre could deliver arena-sized choruses without losing bite. Its balance of hook, riff, and breakdown became a reference model for mid-2000s mainstream metalcore.

**Demon Hunter — Not I (The Triptych, 2005)**
A hook-driven metalcore track with tight riffing, clear chorus writing, and weighty breakdown punctuation. It represents how the Christian metalcore circuit helped professionalize and popularize the sound.

**Throwdown — Forever (Vendetta, 2005)**
A turning-point track that leans hard into metal riffing while retaining hardcore swagger and stomp. It helped define tough, groove-based metalcore built for maximum crowd response.

**Trivium — Pull Harder on the Strings of Your Martyr (Ascendancy, 2005)**
A defining 2005 crossover of metalcore breakdown energy with thrash and classic-metal riff language. It showed that metalcore technique could scale toward more traditional metal structures without losing impact.

**All That Remains — Six (The Fall of Ideals, 2006)**
A showcase of melodic metalcore guitar athleticism, pairing fast riffing with crisp melodic lead work. It helped set the late-2000s standard for precision, speed, and arena-ready aggression.

**I Killed the Prom Queen — Sharks in Your Mouth (Music for the Recently Deceased, 2006)**
An iconic Australian melodic metalcore track that combines emotive hooks with sharp riffing and crushing breakdowns. It helped place the Australian scene on the global metalcore map.

**Misery Signals — The Failsafe (Mirrors, 2006)**
Intricately arranged metalcore that channels emotion through harmony, rhythm, and tension rather than sheer volume. It became a benchmark for the genre's more mature, harmonically rich songwriting.

**Walls of Jericho — A Trigger Full of Promises (With Devils Amongst Us All, 2006)**
A fearless, hardcore-rooted metalcore statement powered by relentless vocals and pit-ready riffs. It's a key example of how metalcore's stylistic range expanded while keeping hardcore intensity intact.

**August Burns Red — Composure (Messengers, 2007)**
Technical, tightly structured metalcore with signature rhythmic phrasing and cleanly articulated riffs. It helped codify the musicianship-driven wing of late-2000s metalcore without drifting into prog territory.

**Blessthefall — Guys Like You Make Us Look Bad (His Last Walk, 2007)**
A quintessential 2007 'scene era' metalcore track: big chorus, sharp breakdown drops, and polished production. It reflects the Rise/Hot Topic ecosystem that popularized metalcore for a new audience.

**Oh, Sleeper — Vices Like Vipers (When I Am God, 2007)**
Dynamic, narrative-intense metalcore that builds toward massive breakdown catharsis. It captures the late-2000s shift toward heavier low-end while keeping melodic clarity and structure.

**Parkway Drive — Carrion (Horizons, 2007)**
A modern classic built on huge riff motifs and perfectly staged breakdown impacts. It embodies metalcore as live-culture music—designed to move crowds as much as to impress musicians.

**Still Remains — The Worst Is Yet to Come (The Serpent, 2007)**
Soaring chorus melody meets aggressive riffing in a clean, accessible melodic metalcore package. It's emblematic of the mid-2000s sweet spot between heaviness and singalong hookcraft.

**Attack Attack! — Stick Stickly (Someday Came Suddenly, 2008)**
A landmark of electronicore that spliced synth lines into metalcore breakdown templates. It helped launch a wave of late-2000s hybrid bands built on the same drop-and-hook architecture.

**For Today — Saul of Tarsus (Ekklesia, 2008)**
A relentless, message-driven metalcore track built on fast riffs and militant breakdown cadence. It represents the late-2000s re-emphasis on heaviness and intensity within melodic metalcore.

**Architects — Early Grave (Hollow Crown, 2009)**
A sharp, anxious riff-and-breakdown masterclass that helped define the modern UK metalcore sound. Its disciplined rhythmic focus foreshadowed the tighter, more percussive 2010s direction.

**Asking Alexandria — The Final Episode (Let's Change the Channel) (Stand Up and Scream, 2009)**
A maximalist 2009 metalcore single built from big hooks, dramatic pacing, and theatrical breakdown drops. It became a defining artifact of the 'scene' era and its larger-than-life approach.

**Miss May I — Architect (Apologies Are for the Weak, 2009)**
Punchy melodic metalcore with rapid riffing and a memorable chorus, delivered with modern 2009 polish. It captures the era's formula of speed, melody, and breakdown payoff in tight pop-like form.

**The Devil Wears Prada — Assistant to the Regional Manager (With Roots Above and Branches Below, 2009)**
A chaotic, playful metalcore track that still hinges on classic stop-start riffing and a huge breakdown release. It represents the genre's willingness to be unpredictable while staying breakdown-driven.

**We Came as Romans — To Plant a Seed (To Plant a Seed, 2009)**
Synth-laced, uplifting metalcore that pairs harsh verses with soaring, positive choruses. It helped define a wave of metalcore where anthem and breakdown coexist in the same emotional register.

## 2010–2014: Modern Metalcore Takes Shape

**Abandon All Ships — Take One Last Breath (Geeving, 2010)**
Electronicore metalcore that layers bright synth hooks over chugging breakdown architecture. It captures the early-2010s turn toward dancefloor textures inside a metalcore framework.

**Motionless in White — Abigail (Creatures, 2010)**
Gothic, theatrical metalcore that balances dark melody with blunt-force breakdowns. It helped broaden metalcore's aesthetic possibilities beyond the standard melodic-death template.

**Of Mice & Men — Second & Sebring (Of Mice & Men, 2010)**
An early-2010s anthem that perfected the clean-chorus/harsh-verse dynamic for a new generation of fans. Its emotional clarity and breakdown timing made it a template for mainstream metalcore songwriting.

**The Word Alive — The Hounds of Anubis (Deceiver, 2010)**
Hyper-energetic, polished metalcore that anchors hooky choruses to tight riff-and-breakdown mechanics. It's emblematic of the Warped Tour-era metalcore circuit at full momentum.

**Woe, Is Me — Vengeance (Number[s], 2010)**
Hook-forward metalcore with shoutable refrains and breakdown-driven pacing. It represents the early-2010s blend of pop sensibility and hardcore structure that dominated the festival circuit.

**Adept — Dead Weight (Death Dealers, 2011)**
A sleek Scandinavian take on modern metalcore built on crisp guitar tone and a huge melodic chorus. It shows how European bands adapted 2000s melodic metalcore into sharper 2010s production.

**Like Moths to Flames — The Worst in Me (When We Don't Exist, 2011)**
A riff-led modern metalcore track that keeps melody and aggression in tight balance. It exemplifies how 2011-era bands modernized the classic template without drifting into post-hardcore.

**Memphis May Fire — The Sinner (The Hollow, 2011)**
Groove-heavy metalcore with clear verse/chorus architecture and a punishing breakdown core. It helped cement the 2010s model of arena-ready clean hooks anchored by heavyweight drops.

**The Plot in You — Premeditated (First Born, 2011)**
A darker, confessional strain of metalcore that combines heavy framework with narrative intensity. It's representative of the early-2010s push toward mood and lyric-driven catharsis within metalcore.

**Bury Tomorrow — Lionheart (The Union of Crowns, 2012)**
A UK melodic metalcore anthem with an enormous chorus and driving riff energy. It captures the early-2010s UK resurgence and its blend of hardcore urgency and singalong scale.

**Crown the Empire — Machines (The Fallout, 2012)**
Cinematic, polished metalcore that uses synth accents and breakdown punctuation to heighten drama. It's a defining example of the 2012 wave where metalcore embraced big production and concept-ready hooks.

**Gideon — No Love/No One (Milestone, 2012)**
Beatdown-leaning metalcore stripped to brutal essentials: blunt riffs, hardcore bark, and crushing breakdowns. It marks the early-2010s return of tougher, more hardcore-rooted metalcore alongside glossy mainstream acts.

**While She Sleeps — Seven Hills (This Is the Six, 2012)**
Punk-charged UK metalcore with gang vocals, frantic riffs, and huge breakdown payoffs. It captures the UK scene's knack for combining hardcore energy with metalcore structure.

**Bring Me the Horizon — Shadow Moses (Sempiternal, 2013)**
A pivot-point track that couples modern metalcore riffing with electronic sheen and a massive hook. It helped define how 2013 metalcore could be both heavy and stadium-scale.

**Fit for a King — Bitter End (Creation/Destruction, 2013)**
Crunchy, melody-forward metalcore with emphatic breakdown placement and modern mix clarity. It represents the 2013 wave that refreshed classic melodic metalcore tropes for a new audience.

**I See Stars — Filth Friends Unite (New Demons, 2013)**
A high-impact electronicore/metalcore hybrid that marries EDM textures to brutal breakdown drops. It illustrates metalcore's continued openness to digital sound design without losing its core mechanics.

**Sworn In — Hypocrisy (The Death Card, 2013)**
Raw, venomous nu-metal-influenced metalcore built on downtuned bounce riffs and pit-ready drops. It's an early marker of the 2010s 'nu-metalcore' tilt that would grow in the next decade.

**Beartooth — In Between (Disgusting, 2014)**
An anthemic, punk-injected metalcore track with a huge chorus and straightforward heavy riffing. It helped usher in a more direct, singalong-friendly approach to 2014-era metalcore.

**Issues — Stingray Affliction (Issues, 2014)**
A bold fusion of pop/R&B vocal language with metalcore breakdown structure and chug riffs. It's a defining 2014 statement of how far metalcore could stretch sonically while staying breakdown-centric.

**Kublai Khan TX — The Hammer (Balancing Survival & Happiness, 2014)**
Uncompromising beatdown metalcore designed for pure physical impact. It embodies the hardcore-rooted strain that remained vital beside the genre's more melodic mainstream forms.

## 2015–2025: Contemporary Metalcore (Hybrid & Heavy)

**Wage War — Stitch (Blueprints, 2015)**
A mid-2010s melodic metalcore standout that re-centered razor riffs and breakdowns around a stadium-sized chorus. Its balance of accessibility and heaviness became a modern benchmark.

**Code Orange — Forever (Forever, 2017)**
Abrasive, industrial-tinged metalcore that pushes texture and dynamics without leaving the breakdown-driven core. It influenced a darker, more experimental late-2010s turn within the genre.

**Stray From The Path — Goodnight Alt-Right (Only Death Is Real, 2017)**
Politically charged metalcore powered by hardcore urgency, groove riffs, and punchy breakdowns. It shows how the genre can function as protest music while staying sonically uncompromising.

**Crystal Lake — Apollo (Helix, 2018)**
Surgical, high-energy metalcore that blends extreme-metal intensity with tight breakdown architecture. It's a standout example of Japan's scene making a major global imprint.

**Ice Nine Kills — Stabbing in the Dark (The Silver Scream, 2018)**
Horror-cinematic metalcore that pairs theatrical storytelling with crushing, well-timed breakdowns. It demonstrates late-2010s metalcore's embrace of concept, spectacle, and big hooks.

**Jesus Piece — Oppressor (Only Self, 2018)**
Hardcore-anchored metalcore brutality built from minimalist, devastating riff shapes and oppressive groove. It's emblematic of the 2018 revival of raw, pit-first metalcore ethos.

**Vein.fm — Virus://Vibrance (Errorzone, 2018)**
A chaotic, abrasive return to metalcore's dissonant roots, full of sudden turns and violent dynamics. It helped inspire a late-2010s wave of unpredictability and rawness in metalcore.

**Knocked Loose — Mistakes Like Fractures (A Different Shade of Blue, 2019)**
A defining modern hybrid of hardcore speed and metalcore breakdown heaviness delivered with ruthless precision. It became a flagship for the current pit-centric, hardcore-forward metalcore resurgence.

**Thornhill — Where We Go When We Die (The Dark Pool, 2019)**
Atmospheric, melody-rich modern metalcore that balances heaviness with spacious textures and dynamic range. It represents the late-2010s trend toward mood-driven metalcore without losing core impact.

**Alpha Wolf — Akudama (A Quiet Place to Die, 2020)**
Compact, explosive nu-metalcore built on bounce riffs, tight grooves, and brutal drop moments. It's a benchmark for the 2020 era's impact-first, short-form metalcore writing.

**Loathe — Two-Way Mirror (I Let It In and It Took Everything, 2020)**
A genre-blurring modern metalcore track that introduces shoegaze/alt textures while keeping heavy framework and tension. It influenced the more atmospheric, boundary-pushing side of 2020s metalcore.

**Tallah — Overconfidence (Matriphagy, 2020)**
Hyperactive, percussive nu-metalcore that revives turn-of-the-millennium bounce inside a breakdown-first structure. It's a clear statement of the 2020s nu-metalcore revival's energy and sound design.

**Imminence — Paralyzed (Heaven in Hiding, 2021)**
Violin-led melodic metalcore that stays heavy while adding a distinctive timbral identity. It exemplifies the 2021 wave of cinematic, emotionally vivid metalcore craftsmanship.

**Landmvrks — Lost in a Wave (Lost in the Waves, 2021)**
French modern metalcore with sharp groove, big hooks, and urgent pacing. It highlights the genre's thriving international scenes and contemporary songwriting polish.

**Bad Omens — ARTIFICIAL SUICIDE (The Death of Peace of Mind, 2022)**
Industrial-leaning modern metalcore with aggressive groove, electronic texture, and a crushing breakdown payoff. It captures the 2022 shift toward darker, synth-inflected heaviness within the genre's core language.

**Bleed from Within — Levitate (Shrine, 2022)**
A 2022 example of 'arena heavy' metalcore where massive riffs meet refined hooks and tight structure. It shows the genre's modern power-metal scale without drifting into adjacent styles.

**Currents — The Death We Seek (The Death We Seek, 2023)**
Heavily downtuned modern metalcore that channels emotion through controlled dynamics and huge catharsis. It reflects 2023's balance of crushing weight and melodic clarity in contemporary production.

**Polaris — Inhumane (Fatalism, 2023)**
Urgent, riff-driven modern metalcore with a devastating breakdown at its center. It demonstrates how the genre's core mechanics remain potent in 2023's high-production context.

**Silent Planet — Antimatter (SUPERBLOOM, 2023)**
Ambitious modern metalcore that pairs crushing low end with expansive ambience and sharp thematic focus. It reflects 2023's push toward cinematic scope while staying firmly metalcore in structure.

**Spiritbox — Cellar Door (The Fear of Fear (EP), 2023)**
Hypnotic groove, electronic sheen, and explosive heaviness combine into a forward-looking metalcore statement. It's a standout 2023 example of innovation that still revolves around breakdown-driven impact.
