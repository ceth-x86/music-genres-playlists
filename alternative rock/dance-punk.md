# Dance-Punk

## First Wave (1978–1984)

**Gang of Four — Damaged Goods (Entertainment!, 1979)**
Angular guitar stabs and an elastic funk bassline turn punk tension into dance-floor propulsion. A foundational punk-funk single that set the template for later dance‑punk revivals.

**Talking Heads — Life During Wartime (Fear of Music, 1979)**
A paranoid lyric delivered over a locked-in, relentlessly motoric groove that's as danceable as it is tense. It's a key early proof that art‑punk could be built for the floor.

**James Chance and the Contortions — Contort Yourself (Buy, 1979)**
No wave abrasion collides with disco command: jagged horns, snarled vocals, and a groove that won't let up. A downtown New York cornerstone for the genre's 'mutant disco' DNA.

**The Slits — Typical Girls (Cut, 1979)**
Post‑punk bite with a singable hook and a rhythm that swings rather than charges. Its feminist perspective and danceable pulse made it a crucial early dance‑punk adjacent anthem.

**The Pop Group — She Is Beyond Good and Evil (Y, 1979)**
Funk rhythm section + avant‑punk dissonance, with a groove that feels both ecstatic and confrontational. It helped define dance‑punk's idea that politics and the dancefloor can coexist.

**Delta 5 — Mind Your Own Business (Mind Your Own Business (single), 1979)**
Choppy guitars, prominent basslines, and call‑and‑response vocals make punk feel irresistibly danceable. A defining example of women-led post‑punk feeding directly into dance‑punk's core vocabulary.

**Bush Tetras — Too Many Creeps (Too Many Creeps (EP), 1980)**
Minimal, bass-led New York punk-funk with wiry guitars and nervy momentum. A scene-defining track that later dance‑punk bands mined for its stripped-down groove discipline.

**A Certain Ratio — Shack Up (Shack Up (single), 1980)**
Manchester post‑punk at its most club-ready: crisp guitar chops riding a deep funk pocket. A bridge between UK post‑punk and the dance‑punk sensibility of the 2000s.

**Defunkt — Make Them Dance (Defunkt, 1980)**
Downtown New York funk-punk that uses horns as rhythmic punctuation rather than decoration. It captures the genre's early 'band as dance engine' approach in its rawest form.

**Pylon — Cool (Gyrate, 1980)**
Athens art‑punk with a relentless, body-forward rhythm section and wiry guitar shapes. A regional classic showing dance‑punk could thrive outside the NYC/UK axis.

**Au Pairs — It's Obvious (Playing with a Different Sex, 1981)**
Taut, percussive guitar and bass lock into a groove that's built to move while staying confrontational. A landmark of dance‑punk's feminist and art‑school post‑punk lineage.

**ESG — UFO (ESG (EP), 1981)**
An ultra-stripped groove that became legendary for how much dance energy it gets from so little. Its influence radiated outward through sampling culture and later dance‑punk minimalism.

**Lizzy Mercier Descloux — Wawa (Mambo Nassau, 1981)**
Afro‑funk rhythmic feel filtered through an art‑punk sensibility, with a dancefloor first arrangement. It widens dance‑punk's map beyond Anglo‑American post‑punk sources.

**Medium Medium — Hungry, So Angry (The Glitterhouse, 1981)**
'White‑funk' post‑punk: twitchy guitars, buoyant bass, and a groove that stays lean and restless. A classic UK example of punk energy reorganized around dance rhythm.

**Maximum Joy — Stretch (Stretch (single), 1981)**
Dubby low end and skittering percussion drive a sleek, nocturnal groove. It's a key Bristol-connected waypoint between post‑punk and dance‑punk's bass-forward future.

**Material — Bustin' Out (Memory Serves, 1981)**
Experimental post‑disco instrumentation and downtown avant instincts welded to a dance pulse. It shows the genre's early openness to studio experimentation without losing body-moving function.

**Shriekback — My Spine (Is the Bassline) (Care, 1982)**
A self-aware ode to the bassline that turns art‑punk irony into pure groove. It's a sharp example of dance‑punk's love of rhythm-section foregrounding.

**Liquid Liquid — Cavern (Optimo, 1983)**
Hypnotic, percussion-heavy post-disco with one of the most influential bass figures in this entire lineage. A pillar for dance‑punk and for the broader downtown/club crossover.

**Cristina — What's a Girl to Do (Sleep It Off, 1984)**
Deadpan vocal delivery over sleek, slightly alien disco‑punk backing. A cult snapshot of the early-80s downtown scene where dance‑punk's attitude was being codified.

**Konk — Your Life (Your Life (single), 1984)**
Quirky, tight punk‑funk that keeps the groove central even as the arrangement gets playful. A solid late first‑wave example of dance‑punk's continuity into the mid‑80s.

## Second Wave Ignition (1999–2004)

**Le Tigre — Deceptacon (Le Tigre, 1999)**
Guitar-driven rhythmic snap and chantable vocals, with riot‑grrrl politics wired straight into club energy. A defining late‑90s track that helped reopen the punk-to-dance pipeline.

**Peaches — Fuck the Pain Away (The Teaches of Peaches, 2000)**
Minimal electro-punk provocation built for sweaty rooms: blunt rhythm, blunt message. It foregrounds dance‑punk's queer/sex-positive, confrontational club lineage.

**The Faint — Agenda Suicide (Danse Macabre, 2001)**
Cold synth textures and guitar attack fused into a grimly danceable pulse. It's a key early‑2000s statement of how dance‑punk could embrace electronic timbres without losing edge.

**ADULT. — Hand to Phone (Resuscitation, 2001)**
Cold, skeletal electronics and punk posture from Detroit's electroclash orbit. A key example of dance‑punk's adjacent machine aesthetics feeding back into rock audiences.

**Radio 4 — Dance to the Underground (Gotham!, 2002)**
A tight post‑punk riff machine built for the floor, balancing angular guitars with a club-minded drive. One of the clearest early markers of the DFA-adjacent revival sound.

**The Rapture — House of Jealous Lovers (Echoes, 2002)**
Cowbell disco, sharp guitars, and ecstatic release in a track that practically named the 2000s dance‑punk comeback. It became a blueprint for indie bands who wanted club impact.

**LCD Soundsystem — Losing My Edge (Losing My Edge (single), 2002)**
Spoken-word bravado over a groove that nods to disco while keeping punk impatience. A manifesto track that helped codify the revival's self-mythology and dancefloor priorities.

**Q and Not U — Soft Pyramids (Different Damage, 2002)**
D.C. art‑punk turns rhythmic complexity into physical momentum, with vocals that feel percussive. It shows dance‑punk's crossover with post‑hardcore brains and groove.

**Hot Hot Heat — Bandages (Make Up the Breakdown, 2002)**
Hyper-kinetic post‑punk pop with a hook that lands like a dance command. It helped make 'indie that you can dance to' feel mainstream in the early 2000s.

**The Sounds — Living in America (Living in America, 2002)**
Scandinavian new‑wave rock with a dance pulse and anthem build. It represents the European pop-facing end of dance‑punk's early-2000s resurgence.

**Electric Six — Danger! High Voltage (Fire, 2003)**
Glam-disco rock as dance‑punk gateway: four-on-the-floor swagger with a punkish sneer. A singalong staple that brought dance‑punk attitude into larger rooms.

**Yeah Yeah Yeahs — Date with the Night (Fever to Tell, 2003)**
A sprinting guitar track that turns punk intensity into dance-floor velocity. It's emblematic of early NYC bands translating underground tension into kinetic release.

**!!! — Me and Giuliani Down by the School Yard (A True Story) (Louden Up Now, 2004)**
A long-form, percussion-forward jam that turns indie-rock into communal dance ritual. Its build-and-release structure became a model for dance‑punk's marathon floor anthems.

**Death From Above 1979 — Romantic Rights (You're a Woman, I'm a Machine, 2004)**
A brutal bass-and-drums attack that still grooves, proving dance‑punk can be heavy without sacrificing movement. A defining example of the genre's 'punk body' meeting dance insistence.

**Moving Units — Between Us & Them (Dangerous Dreams, 2004)**
Wiry guitars and pulse-driven arrangement capture early‑2000s West Coast dance‑punk in concise pop form. It's a great case of the genre's club logic translated into band songwriting.

**Liars — There's Always Room on the Broom (They Were Wrong, So We Drowned, 2004)**
Ritualistic chant, odd timbres, and a relentless beat push dance‑punk toward the avant-garde. It's important for showing that 'danceable' doesn't have to mean conventional.

**Franz Ferdinand — Take Me Out (Franz Ferdinand, 2004)**
A pop-perfect arrangement that pivots from guitar rock to dance-floor structure in real time. One of the era's most recognizable post‑punk-to-club crossover hits.

**The Bravery — An Honest Mistake (The Bravery, 2004)**
New-wave synth sheen and rock drive designed for club play. A clear snapshot of mid‑2000s dance‑punk's radio-friendly, hook-forward side.

**Cut Copy — Saturdays (Bright Like Neon Love, 2004)**
Indie synth-dance framed with band energy and emotional lift. It marks the point where dance‑punk's rhythmic logic blends into the broader indie‑dance ecosystem.

**VHS or Beta — Night on Fire (Night on Fire, 2004)**
A sleek disco beat under indie guitars, beloved by early indie‑club DJs. It captures dance‑punk's 'live band, club timing' sweet spot.

## Peak Era (2005–2009)

**CSS — Let's Make Love and Listen to Death from Above (Cansei de Ser Sexy, 2005)**
Playful, shouty vocals over a beat that treats punk as dance music. A global breakout that helped define mid‑2000s indie‑dance nightlife.

**Datarock — Fa-Fa-Fa (Datarock Datarock, 2005)**
Handclaps, synthy stabs, and post‑punk funk discipline in a compact, instantly danceable song. A signature Scandinavian contribution to the genre's peak era.

**Test Icicles — Circle. Square. Triangle. (For Screening Purposes Only, 2005)**
Spiky, frantic, and short: punk riffs arranged with dance urgency. A blueprint for the abrasive side of the 2005 'new rave' wave.

**Shitdisco — I Know Kung Fu (I Know Kung Fu, 2005)**
Chaotic DIY disco‑punk with shouting hooks and cheap electronic bite. A cult indie-sleaze anthem where the 'punk' part stays loud and messy.

**Every Move a Picture — Signs of Life (Every Move a Picture, 2005)**
Twitchy guitar interplay and a disco pulse in crisp, compact songwriting. A strong example of mid‑2000s UK dance‑punk craft.

**Thunderbirds Are Now! — From Skaters to Professors (Justamustache, 2005)**
Angular post‑punk made physical, with a beat that never stops pushing. A favorite for showing dance‑punk's art-rock tension without sacrificing motion.

**Bloc Party — Banquet (Silent Alarm, 2005)**
Staccato guitars and danceable drumming that turned post‑punk into club fuel. One of the essential mid‑2000s 'indie you can dance to' tracks.

**Arctic Monkeys — I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor (Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not, 2005)**
A garage-punk rush with dancefloor-ready tempo and chorus release. Not pure dance‑punk, but hugely important to indie-dance nights that fed the scene.

**The Gossip — Standing in the Way of Control (Standing in the Way of Control, 2006)**
A huge disco-punk groove with a commanding vocal that became a cross‑scene anthem. It's one of the clearest 'textbook' dance‑punk singles of the 2000s.

**Shout Out Out Out Out — The Sentence (Not Saying / Doing, 2006)**
Synth-punk energy translated into live-band propulsion and chantable phrases. A Canadian scene classic that keeps dance‑punk DIY and communal.

**The Automatic — Monster (Not Accepted Anywhere, 2006)**
Quirky synth-and-guitar stomp with an instantly memorable chorus. A mainstream example of dance‑punk's hook-first, danceable structure.

**Goose — Bring It On (Bring It On, 2006)**
Driving club-rock hybrid that plays like a band performing electronic momentum. A Benelux festival staple that kept dance‑punk energy in large spaces.

**Digitalism — Pogo (Idealism, 2007)**
A frantic synth-rock groove that keeps punk speed while aiming straight at the club. A defining track for the mid‑2000s 'indie meets dance' collision.

**Klaxons — Golden Skans (Myths of the Near Future, 2007)**
Neon 'new rave' glitter over a punkish rhythmic chassis. A landmark of the UK moment when dance‑punk aesthetics hit the mainstream.

**New Young Pony Club — Ice Cream (Fantastic Playroom, 2007)**
Minimal, bass-first groove and clipped vocals that feel made for dark rooms. A perfect example of dance‑punk's ability to sound sleek without losing bite.

**You Say Party — The Gap (Between the Rich and the Poor) (Hit the Floor!, 2007)**
Bright riffs and bittersweet melody riding a steady dance groove. It represents the melodic, pop-leaning turn dance‑punk took in the late 2000s.

**We Are Wolves — Magique (Total Magique, 2007)**
Electro-tinged dance‑punk with sharp riffs and a club-ready pulse that still feels like a band in motion. A key Montréal example of mid‑2000s dance‑punk leaning into electronics without losing bite.

**Does It Offend You, Yeah? — We Are Rockstars (You Have No Idea What You're Getting Yourself Into, 2008)**
Big riffs and big synths arranged like a club set, with mosh energy baked in. A peak 'blog era' track that fused rock bravado with dance-floor function.

**The Presets — My People (Apocalypso, 2008)**
Stadium-scale chant hooks riding a relentless pulse. It shows how dance‑punk expanded into festival-sized electronic-rock hybrids.

**Hadouken! — That Boy That Girl (Music for an Accelerated Culture, 2008)**
Shout vocals and punk energy welded to a hard electronic beat. A key UK track where dance‑punk overlaps with electro and grime-era urgency.

**Late of the Pier — Heartbeat (Fantasy Black Channel, 2008)**
Art-school eccentricity and jagged guitars riding a steady dance groove. A cult cornerstone for dance‑punk's weird, theatrical side.

**Friendly Fires — Paris (Friendly Fires, 2008)**
Polished indie-dance propulsion with live-band spring in its step. A gateway track that kept dance‑punk rhythms on festival and radio playlists.

**The Ting Tings — That's Not My Name (We Started Nothing, 2008)**
Clipped vocal attitude and a beat that lands like a command. A pop-scale hit that shows dance‑punk's capacity for massive hooks without losing snap.

**Ladyhawke — My Delirium (Ladyhawke, 2008)**
Guitars sparkle over a pounding, club-tuned arrangement. It captures the late‑2000s push toward glossy, 80s-leaning dance‑punk.

**The Whip — Trash (X Marks Destination, 2008)**
Four-on-the-floor insistence with sharp guitar/synth hooks built for the club. A functional 'weapon' track in the genre's peak dancefloor period.

**The Virgins — Rich Girls (The Virgins, 2008)**
Indie sleaze swagger compressed into a sharp, dance-ready single. A snapshot of late‑2000s club-rock attitude in New York.

**Professor Murder — Dutch Kiss (Mystery of Pop, 2008)**
Jittery guitar funk and chantable hooks packaged like a party-starter. A cult NYC dance‑punk track that captures the era's art-school swagger and dance-floor intent.

**Crystal Castles — Alice Practice (Crystal Castles, 2008)**
Distorted electronics and screamed hooks, built like a punk track but aimed at the club. Influential for the darker, noisier branch of dance‑punk-adjacent nightlife.

**Foals — Balloons (Antidotes, 2008)**
Mathy guitar angles locked to a tight groove, balancing brains and body. It's a key example of dance‑punk's more intricate, kinetic side.

**Midnight Juggernauts — Into the Galaxy (Dystopia, 2008)**
Synth-led dance-rock with band dynamics and widescreen chorus. It points to the genre's poppy, festival-scale direction in the late 2000s.

**Matt and Kim — Daylight (Grand, 2009)**
Minimal duo dynamics with a shouted hook that plays like a dance-floor singalong. One of the era's most accessible entry points into dance‑punk.

**The Bloody Beetroots — Warp 1.9 (Romborama, 2009)**
Over-the-top rave-punk aggression with mosh-friendly dynamics. Captures the late‑2000s fascination with merging club drops and punk physicality.

**The Juan MacLean — Happy House (The Future Will Come, 2009)**
DFA-style disco propulsion stretched into a full, driving song-form with rockish momentum. It shows how dance‑punk's scene logic expanded into longer, DJ-friendly structures while keeping live-band energy.

**WhoMadeWho — Hello Empty Room (The Plot, 2009)**
Live-band pulse with electronic sheen and a groove-first mindset. A strong European example of dance‑punk's post-2000s refinement.

## Diversification (2010–2017)

**Two Door Cinema Club — What You Know (Tourist History, 2010)**
Bright arpeggios and a relentlessly danceable drive, turning guitar-pop into floor-friendly motion. It represents how dance‑punk rhythms persisted in 2010s indie without the original scene buzz.

**Hot Chip — I Feel Better (One Life Stand, 2010)**
Disco pulse and band-like dynamics, polished for late-night play. A track that shows dance‑punk's groove priorities evolving into warmer, synth-forward indie dance.

**Sleigh Bells — Rill Rill (Treats, 2010)**
Noise-textured guitars set against a snapped, danceable beat. It helped reassert punk timbre inside pop/dance frameworks in the early 2010s.

**Holy Ghost! — Wait & See (Holy Ghost!, 2011)**
DFA-adjacent disco-punk polish with a springy bassline and clear hook. A post-blog era example of dance‑punk's commitment to groove craft.

**The Death Set — They Come to Get Us (Michel Poiccard, 2011)**
Shout-along punk euphoria engineered for relentless dancing. A cult favorite that keeps the genre's sweat-and-catharsis core intact.

**YACHT — Dystopia (The Earth Is on Fire) (Shangri-La, 2011)**
Deadpan satire riding a clean electronic pulse with punk posture. It demonstrates dance‑punk's long-standing link between danceability and social commentary.

**STRFKR — Golden Light (Reptilians, 2011)**
Light, radiant indie-pop that still moves with dance-first timing. It marks the genre's softer 2010s branch where propulsion matters more than abrasion.

**Metronomy — The Look (The English Riviera, 2011)**
Minimal groove, clipped motifs, and immaculate timing. A refined template for 'tight band + dance beat' aesthetics in 2010s indie.

**Funeral Party — Finale (The Golden Age of Knowhere, 2011)**
Big chorus drama over dance-rock propulsion. A snapshot of early-2010s alt acts borrowing dance‑punk's club-ready arrangement logic.

**DZ Deathrays — The Mess Up (Bloodstreams, 2012)**
Hard-guitar energy paired with a driving rhythm section that keeps the body moving. It shows how dance‑punk can lean garage-punk without losing dance function.

**Factory Floor — Two Different Ways (Factory Floor, 2013)**
Austere, physical repetition with live drums/bass and cold synths. A key 2010s turn back toward minimal, industrial-tinged dance‑punk.

**The Oral Cigarettes — Kyōran Hey Kids!! (Fixion, 2016)**
High-speed J‑rock adrenaline with lockstep rhythm and shoutable hooks. It's a modern example of dance‑punk's translation into non‑Western rock scenes.

**Parquet Courts — Human Performance (Human Performance, 2016)**
Post‑punk elasticity and rhythmic snap built for motion, not just mood. It shows dance‑punk's lyrical/introspective turn while keeping the groove central.

**Shopping — In Other Words (The Official Body, 2016)**
Lean, repetitive grooves with wiry guitar and relentless forward motion. A 2010s update of classic dance‑punk economy: no excess, all propulsion.

**Guerilla Toss — Can I Get the Real Stuff?? (Eraser Stargazer, 2016)**
Funk-punk chaos with rubbery bass and tumbling drums aimed at ecstatic release. It expands dance‑punk toward psychedelic and experimental territory without losing the beat.

**Priests — JJ (Nothing Feels Natural, 2017)**
Sharp, percussive post‑punk tension that stays danceable and urgent. It underscores how dance‑punk's political bite remains viable in the late 2010s.

## New Wave of Dance‑Punk (2018–2025)

**Bodega — How Did This Happen?! (Endless Scroll, 2018)**
Jittery guitars and tight funk rhythm in a modern NYC reboot of classic dance‑punk. Its cynical humor and dance-floor bite make it a late‑2010s scene touchstone.

**Warmduscher — Whale City (Whale City, 2018)**
Sleazy punk-funk groove with bass-forward swagger and club pacing. A strong example of the genre's 'mutant disco' lineage resurfacing in London.

**Viagra Boys — Sports (Street Worms, 2018)**
Sardonic vocal delivery over a stomping, danceable post‑punk groove. It helped make the club‑pit crossover feel central again in late‑2010s dance‑punk adjacent scenes.

**Shame — One Rizla (Songs of Praise, 2018)**
Racing momentum and sharp rhythmic turns built for both pogo and dance. A key UK revival track that recharges the genre's nervous energy.

**Public Practice — Fate (Gentle Grip (EP), 2018)**
Clean bass/guitar interplay over a straight club pulse with minimalist discipline. One of the clearest contemporary statements of 'dance first, punk attitude.'

**Working Men's Club — Valleys (Working Men's Club, 2020)**
Cold, propulsive basslines fused to electronic throb and stark vocal mood. It captures dance‑punk's ongoing machine‑human tension in the 2020s.

**Gustaf — Mine (Audio Drag for Ego Slobs, 2021)**
Bright, wiry guitars and chugging rhythm with playful vocal presence. A New York highlight that returns dance‑punk to lean band dynamics and humor.

**Yard Act — The Overload (The Overload, 2022)**
Spoken delivery over locked-in groove, turning social observation into danceable propulsion. It shows how modern acts fold dance‑punk rhythm into narrative songwriting.

**Model/Actriz — Mosquito (Dogsbody, 2023)**
Punishing yet danceable percussive intensity, pushing dance‑punk into noise-rock catharsis. A key 2020s example of the genre getting heavier without losing motion.

**Friedberg — Hardcore Workout Queen (Hardcore Workout Queen, 2024)**
Disco-punk strut with sharp guitars and self-aware hedonism. A modern track that updates dance‑punk's club attitude for the 2020s.
