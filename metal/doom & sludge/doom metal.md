# Doom Metal

## Foundations & First Wave (1970–1990)

**Black Sabbath — Black Sabbath (Black Sabbath, 1970)**
The opening tritone-laden riff and dirge pacing effectively blueprint doom's atmosphere of dread. Even as proto-doom within early heavy metal, it set the tonal vocabulary that later doom metal formalized.

**Witchfinder General — Death Penalty (Death Penalty, 1982)**
A key UK early-80s statement that slows NWOBHM energy into something darker and heavier. Its occult menace and blunt riffing helped define the first-wave British doom feel.

**Trouble — Psalm 9 (Psalm 9, 1984)**
An early US cornerstone that proves doom could be heavy, melodic, and classically structured without speed. The song's solemn riff cadence and clear vocal delivery became a template for traditional doom.

**Black Hole — Blind Men and Occult Forces (Land of Mystery, 1985)**
A cult Italian track that captures early doom's horror-tinged melodicism and weighty mid-tempo crush. It's a vivid reminder that doom's underground spread in Europe was already happening by the mid-80s.

**Pentagram — Forever My Queen (Relentless, 1985)**
Pentagram's riffcraft and song economy became foundational for American doom's "garage-Sabbath" lineage. This track's swaggering heaviness and hooky structure has echoed through countless later trad-doom bands.

**Candlemass — Solitude (Epicus Doomicus Metallicus, 1986)**
A defining epic-doom anthem where operatic vocals and cathedral-sized melodies sit on truly heavy, slow riffs. It's one of the clearest moments where doom becomes grand, theatrical, and formally "metallic."

**Paul Chain — Violet Theatre (In the Darkness, 1986)**
Paul Chain's psychedelic-horror sensibility gives doom an uncanny, surreal edge while staying riff-centered. The track is a classic example of Italy's idiosyncratic 80s doom underground.

**Saint Vitus — Born Too Late (Born Too Late, 1986)**
A quintessential Saint Vitus statement: gritty, downbeat, and stubbornly slow, with punk-seasoned attitude. It helped cement doom as an underground metal identity rather than just a Sabbath influence.

**Dream Death — Sealed in Blood (Journey into Mystery, 1987)**
A late-80s bridge between traditional doom weight and the darker, harsher extremity that would feed death-doom. The riffs stay doom-first, but the overall heaviness points toward the genre's next mutation.

**Count Raven — The Poltergeist (Storm Warning, 1990)**
A Swedish classic that channels Sabbathian groove into a tighter, 90s-ready traditional doom form. Its balance of swing, weight, and clear riff narrative made it a touchstone for the coming wave of trad doom.

## Classic Consolidation & Epic Doom (1991–1999)

**Cathedral — Ebony Tears (Forest of Equilibrium, 1991)**
From Cathedral's pure-doom era, this track is thick, mournful, and deliberately slow, with a massive low-end presence. It's historically important as part of the early-90s UK pivot that helped doom regain underground momentum.

**Confessor — Condemned (Condemned, 1991)**
Progressive doom done without losing the genre's gravity: technical riffs, odd turns, and tense vocals still land on a doom-weight foundation. It's a landmark for showing doom could be complex and cerebral while staying slow and heavy.

**Revelation — Salvation's Answer (Salvation's Answer, 1991)**
A cornerstone of the early-90s US traditional doom underground, with huge riff blocks and memorable melodic lead work. Revelation helped keep doom's classic metal DNA intact during a decade dominated by other extreme styles.

**Solitude Aeturnus — Mirror of Sorrow (Into the Depths of Sorrow, 1991)**
A definitive US epic-doom performance with soaring vocals and long, mournful melodic phrases over weighty riff cycles. It captures the genre's ability to feel both monumental and intimately tragic in the same breath.

**Penance — Words of Dissent (The Road Less Travelled, 1992)**
A rough-edged, riff-driven example of early-90s American doom that prioritizes groove and grit over polish. The song embodies the scene's cult-era heaviness while still delivering strong traditional hooks.

**Unorthodox — Suicide King (Asylum, 1992)**
Dark, oppressive traditional doom with a distinctly early-90s underground tone and pacing. It's a key "Hellhound-era" example where classic doom riffing is pushed toward a more severe, claustrophobic mood.

**Veni Domine — Fall Babylon Fall (Fall Babylon Fall, 1992)**
A progressive/concept-minded take on doom that expands the style's harmonic palette while keeping the slow riff backbone dominant. It's important for illustrating how traditional doom absorbed prog structure without turning into a different genre.

**Internal Void — A Blessing in Disguise (Standing on the Sun, 1993)**
An American scene staple that balances crushing mid-tempo riffs with a sense of classic-song momentum. It's one of those tracks that demonstrates how 90s traditional doom stayed hooky and heavy without crossover dilution.

**Memento Mori — The Riddle (Rhymes of Lunacy, 1993)**
Swedish doom that threads epic melody through tough, straightforward riffing, bridging classic doom and a more melodic European sensibility. The track is a strong example of early-90s Scandinavia shaping its own doom accent.

**Iron Man — The Passage (The Passage, 1994)**
Raw, Sabbath-rooted doom that became a cult touchstone for later traditional revivalists. The track's simple, massive riff narrative captures doom's core "weight over speed" ethos.

**Solstice — Earthsong (Lamentations, 1994)**
A majestic UK epic-doom centerpiece built on long-form melody and a slow, processional sense of drama. It's one of the clearest mid-90s statements of epic doom as serious, emotionally high-impact metal.

**Wretched — Golden Child (Psychosomatic Medicine, 1994)**
A cult American doom track that pairs dramatic arrangement with heavyweight riffing and somber melodic turns. It's representative of the mid-90s underground where doom's classic form survived in small but influential circles.

**Abstrakt Algebra — Stigmata (Abstrakt Algebra, 1995)**
Leif Edling's mid-90s experiment stretches doom into progressive, almost labyrinthine riff architecture without abandoning heaviness. It's historically significant as proof that doom's evolution wasn't only about getting slower or fuzzier.

**Memory Garden — Tides (Tides, 1996)**
A melodic Scandinavian doom landmark that updates classic riffing with strong lead-guitar storytelling. It helped define a cleaner, more melodic European traditional doom sound in the late 90s.

**Forsaken — The Healer (Evermore, 1997)**
A key example of doom's globalization: Maltese epic doom with big, sorrowful melodies and a truly classic metal sense of pacing. The track earns its place as a scene-defining song from outside the usual doom power centers.

**Scald — Night Sky (Will of the Gods Is Great Power, 1997)**
A rare Russian epic-doom gem with huge, mythic atmosphere and a deeply mournful melodic core. Its cult reputation rests on how fully it captures "heroic tragedy" within strict doom tempos.

**Dawn of Winter — In the Valley of Tears (In the Valley of Tears, 1998)**
A late-90s European traditional doom statement that emphasizes slow, crushing riff weight and bleak mood over flash. It's influential as part of the wave that carried classic doom aesthetics into the 2000s underground.

**Pagan Altar — Judgement of the Dead (Volume 1, 1998)**
Occult British doom with a vintage tone that connects early doom tradition to the 90s revival era. The songwriting is unmistakably doom-first: heavy, slow, and melodically haunted.

**While Heaven Wept — Thus With a Kiss I Die (Sorrow of the Angels, 1998)**
A major epic-doom track where dynamics and melody are used for real emotional catharsis, not just scale. It's crucial for understanding how late-90s doom laid groundwork for the 2000s/2010s "emotional epic" tradition.

**Doomsword — Swords of Doom (Doomsword, 1999)**
Italian epic doom that fuses doom's weight with classic-metal grandeur and battle-ready march rhythms. It's a defining sword-and-sorcery doom track that stays firmly within doom's pacing and harmonic language.

## Modern Traditional & Epic Doom (2000–2025)

**Pale Divine — Judas Wheel (Thunder Perfect Mind, 2001)**
An early-2000s US revival highlight that keeps the riffs huge and the song structure adventurous without sliding into adjacent styles. It shows traditional doom surviving and evolving when the genre was still far from fashionable.

**Krux — In the White (Krux, 2002)**
A modern epic-doom benchmark where classic Candlemass-scale melodicism meets heavier contemporary production. The song demonstrates how 2000s bands refreshed traditional doom's drama while staying stylistically pure.

**Reverend Bizarre — Doom Over the World (In the Rectory of the Bizarre Reverend, 2002)**
A manifesto-like epic built on uncompromising slowness and monolithic riff authority. It became central to the 2000s doom underground by embodying the genre's "pure doom" ethos with total conviction.

**Orodruin — Epicurean Mass (Epicurean Mass, 2003)**
A no-frills traditional doom statement that prioritizes riff narrative and solemn pacing. Its title-track heft helped make Orodruin a key carrier of classic doom aesthetics into the 2000s.

**Spiritus Mortis — Doom Mood (Spiritus Mortis, 2004)**
Finnish traditional doom that embraces classic metal songwriting while keeping doom's downbeat gravity dominant. It's a crisp example of how the 2000s scene revived vintage doom without relying on stoner or sludge crossover.

**Isole — Forevermore (Forevermore, 2005)**
Swedish epic doom in direct conversation with Candlemass, but with its own melodic voice and modern clarity. The track stands as a 2000s European standard for slow grandeur and memorable chorus-scale hooks.

**Mirror of Deception — Haunted (Shards, 2006)**
German traditional doom distilled into tight, riff-led songwriting with a strong sense of shadowy atmosphere. It captures the 2000s "new traditional doom" wave at its most disciplined and heavy.

**Warning — Footprints (Watching from a Distance, 2006)**
A minimalist modern doom classic where emotional impact comes from restraint, patience, and perfectly weighted riff changes. Its influence is huge on later melodic/traditional doom for proving less can hit harder.

**Hour of 13 — Call to Satan (Hour of 13, 2007)**
Occult-leaning traditional doom that keeps metal riffing and doom pacing in the foreground. It's a strong example of modern bands adding bite and personality without leaving the genre's textbook fundamentals.

**Isen Torr — Mighty and Superior (Mighty and Superior, 2008)**
American epic doom with mythic storytelling, long melodic lines, and a deliberate, heroic pacing. The track's cult status reflects how well it captures "grand tragedy" within strict doom tempos.

**The Gates of Slumber — Conqueror (Conqueror, 2008)**
A late-2000s US heavyweight that feels like a marching war hymn built from massive riff blocks. It helped reassert traditional doom's physical, riff-first power during the genre's modern revival.

**Atlantean Kodex — The White Goddess (The Golden Bough, 2010)**
A defining German epic-doom statement where mythopoetic lyrics and towering melodies ride genuinely crushing riffs. It demonstrates how doom can be expansive and majestic while remaining slow, heavy, and harmonically dark.

**Jex Thoth — Nothing Left to Love (Jex Thoth, 2010)**
Ritualistic occult doom built on hypnotic repetition and a commanding vocal presence. It shows doom's ability to feel ceremonial and entrancing without crossing over into stoner rock or gothic metal territory.

**Argus — A Curse on the World (Boldly Stride the Doomed, 2011)**
US epic doom that welds anthemic chorus power to doom's slow, minor-key gravity. It's a clear example of "doom as traditional metal" where the doom element stays primary rather than decorative.

**Lord Vicar — Between the Blue Temple and the North Tower (Signs of Osiris, 2011)**
Finnish traditional doom with vintage tone and long-form structure, emphasizing riff architecture and slow narrative development. It's a modern exemplar of classic doom songwriting discipline.

**Altar of Oblivion — Where Darkness Is Light (Grand Gesture of Defiance, 2012)**
A modern epic-doom highlight that leans theatrical yet stays doom-dominant through pacing and riff weight. The track shows how grand vocals and heavy-metal drama can coexist with strict doom fundamentals.

**Pilgrim — Quest (Misery Wizard, 2012)**
A 2010s revival anthem that balances punchy modern production with classic doom pacing and riff logic. It's important for representing a newer generation writing traditional doom songs with real narrative lift.

**Avatarium — Moonhorse (Avatarium, 2013)**
A long, heavy centerpiece where Leif Edling's doom sensibility is expressed through modern arrangement and dramatic build. It retains doom's defining traits—slow weight, minor-key grandeur, riff primacy—throughout.

**Procession — To Reap Heavens Apart (To Reap Heavens Apart, 2013)**
Chilean epic doom at peak cinematic scale: patient riff drops, soaring melodies, and a sense of vast, mythic space. It's scene-defining proof that the modern trad/epic doom movement is truly global.

**Below — Across the Dark River (Across the Dark River, 2014)**
A pure Swedish traditional doom title track that distills heavy melancholy into classic pacing and riff storytelling. It's a modern reference point for bands chasing "textbook doom" without crossover influences.

**Capilla Ardiente — Towards the Midnight Ocean (Bravery, Truth and the Endless Darkness, 2014)**
A monumental epic-doom track that unfolds like a slow-moving seafaring tragedy, with huge melodic arcs over crushing riffs. It helped establish Capilla Ardiente as one of the key 2010s epic-doom bands.

**Cross Vault — Home (Spectres of Revocable Loss, 2014)**
Modern doom that uses space, atmosphere, and patient tension while keeping metal heft and doom pacing dominant. It's a strong example of post-2010 traditional doom embracing melancholy as structure, not just mood.

**Crypt Sermon — Out of the Garden (Out of the Garden, 2015)**
A modern US epic-doom classic that marries 70s-prog ambition to strict doom riff weight and pacing. It quickly became a reference point for the 2010s wave of traditional-leaning doom bands.

**Sorcerer — The Dark Tower of the Sorcerer (In the Shadow of the Inverted Cross, 2015)**
A triumphant Swedish epic-doom comeback where towering riffs support a genuinely heroic chorus. It's important for showing how late-period doom can feel classic in spirit without sounding like pastiche.

**Messa — Leah (Feast for Water, 2018)**
Contemporary doom that expands the harmonic palette with dark-jazz shading while keeping the riff weight and slow tension primary. It's a key example of modern doom innovating from inside the style rather than by crossover.

**Smoulder — Ilian of Garathorm (Times of Obscene Evil and Wild Daring, 2019)**
Sword-and-sorcery epic doom with strong narrative build and melodic hooks that still sit on doom's heavy, slow backbone. It exemplifies the modern wave's renewed love for fantasy grandeur within strict doom form.

**Apostle of Solitude — When The Darkness Comes (Until the Darkness Goes, 2021)**
Emotion-forward modern traditional doom that stays grounded in riff craft and patient pacing. The track shows how contemporary bands can be deeply melodic while remaining unmistakably doom metal.

**Khemmis — Living Pyre (Deceiver, 2021)**
Melodic modern doom where dual-guitar harmonies and tight songwriting sit on a classic slow-weight foundation. It represents the 2010s/2020s approach to traditional doom: big hooks, but doom-first structure.

**Pallbearer — Where The Light Fades (Mind Burns Alive, 2024)**
A modern epic-doom centerpiece built on long melodic arcs and controlled, patient heaviness. It exemplifies the contemporary "emotional epic" direction while remaining rooted in classic doom pacing.

**Angel of Damnation — Warning from the Sky (Ethereal Blasphemy, 2025)**
Late-era underground traditional doom that keeps riffs and minor-key grandeur front and center. It highlights the continuing strength of the 2020s trad-doom circuit beyond the genre's most famous names.

## Funeral Doom (1993–2025)

**Thergothon — Stream From the Heavens (Stream From the Heavens, 1994)**
One of the foundational funeral-doom recordings: extreme slowness, cold atmosphere, and a truly funereal sense of motion. This track essentially codified the subgenre's core parameters for the decades that followed.

**Funeral — Taarene (Tragedies, 1995)**
A Norwegian early classic that frames doom's heaviness in a deeply mournful, romantic atmosphere. It's historically important for shaping funeral doom's melodic and emotional language in the mid-90s.

**Skepticism — The March and the Stream (Stormcrowfleet, 1995)**
A cornerstone of the Finnish funeral-doom sound, with organ-like keys and a procession rhythm that feels like a slow ritual. The track's structure became a template for countless later funeral-doom bands.

**Shape of Despair — Angels of Distress (Angels of Distress, 2001)**
A melodic funeral-doom benchmark where vast keyboards and slow, sorrowful lead lines create sustained emotional weight. It's crucial for defining the 2000s style of "beautiful" funeral doom.

**Wormphlegm — In an Excruciating Way... (In an Excruciating Way..., 2001)**
A brutal, oppressive take on funeral doom where heaviness and ugliness are pushed to the extreme. It's a key counterpoint to the more romantic strain, showing the subgenre's capacity for sheer sonic misery.

**Worship — Last Tape Before Doomsday (Last Tape Before Doomsday, 2002)**
A cult early-2000s funeral-doom statement built on minimal, crushing repetition and deep atmosphere. Worship helped spread the style beyond Finland and establish funeral doom as a wider international movement.

**Monolithe — Monolithe I (Monolithe I, 2003)**
A monolithic single-track composition that demonstrates funeral doom's comfort with long-form, slowly evolving architecture. It's influential for turning endurance and gradual transformation into the music's main narrative.

**Tyranny — Tides of Awakening (Tides of Awakening, 2004)**
Raw Finnish funeral doom at its most suffocating: relentlessly slow, cavernous, and bleak. It became a touchstone for the more oppressive, minimal end of the funeral-doom spectrum.

**Comatose Vigil — Not a Gleam of Hope (Not a Gleam of Hope, 2005)**
A Russian classic that pairs thick, depressive atmosphere with slow-moving melodic development. It's important for showing how funeral doom globalized into distinctive local expressions.

**Evoken — In Solitary Ruin (Antithesis of Light, 2005)**
Evoken's cathedral-sized sound turned funeral doom into something monumental and almost orchestral in feel. This track is a mid-2000s landmark for the subgenre's "grand" production aesthetic and emotional scale.

**Mournful Congregation — The Monad of Creation (The Monad of Creation, 2005)**
A masterclass in patient melodic doom-building, with long arcs that pay off in devastating slow-motion catharsis. It's one of the defining examples of the Australian school of funeral doom.

**Ahab — The Call of the Wretched Sea (The Call of the Wretched Sea, 2006)**
Ahab's nautical concept turns funeral doom into cinematic storytelling without sacrificing crushing riff weight. The track was a major entry point for many listeners and helped popularize the subgenre's modern era.

**Doom:VS — Aeternus (Aeternus, 2006)**
A melodic Swedish approach to funeral doom where lead guitar and atmosphere carry equal weight. It's a key example of the subgenre's 2000s continuation beyond the original Finnish core.

**Colosseum — The Gate of Adar (Chapter 1: Delirium, 2007)**
One-man Finnish grandeur that layers huge textures over extremely slow structural shifts. It shows funeral doom as both symphonic and suffocating—massive sound with no hurry.

**Esoteric — Circle (The Maniacal Vale, 2008)**
Psychedelic, multi-layered funeral doom that stretches the style into strange, mind-bending territory while staying crushingly slow. Esoteric's approach is pivotal for the subgenre's experimental possibilities.

**The Howling Void — The Womb Beyond the World (The Womb Beyond the World, 2011)**
Atmospheric funeral doom with a more cosmic, spiritual sense of space, emphasizing texture and sustained mood. It's a strong US interpretation of the style's slow ritualism.

**Lycus — Solar Chamber (Chasms, 2016)**
Modern funeral doom built from stark minimalism and slow crescendo writing rather than ornate arrangements. It's an essential example of the subgenre's contemporary American strain.

**Bell Witch — Mirror Reaper (Mirror Reaper, 2017)**
A single-track modern monument that pushes funeral doom toward ritual endurance and emotional immersion. It became a canonical 2010s reference point for how expansive the form can be.

**Un — A Garden Where Nothing Grows (The Tomb of All Things, 2018)**
Contemporary funeral doom that combines crushing slowness with aching melodic passages and grief-soaked atmosphere. It shows the subgenre's continuing vitality and evolution in the late 2010s.

**Atramentus — Stygian (Stygian, 2020)**
A modern "cathedral" funeral-doom statement with organ-like atmosphere and immense, slow-moving weight. It exemplifies the 2020s resurgence of grand, melodic funeral doom.

## Death Doom (1989–2025)

**Paradise Lost — Lost Paradise (Lost Paradise, 1990)**
An early UK blueprint for death-doom: death-metal vocal harshness and doom pacing fused into a single, oppressive language. Its influence runs through both the Peaceville lineage and later melodic death-doom.

**Winter — Servants of the Warsmen (Into Darkness, 1990)**
One of the earliest truly extreme slow death-doom statements, abrasive and frozen in tone. Winter's approach heavily influenced later death-doom and even the emergence of funeral doom's deeper slowness.

**Tiamat — The Sleeping Beauty (Clouds, 1992)**
A key early-90s Scandinavian death-doom track where death-metal intensity is slowed into a bleak, doomy crawl. It helped define the moodier, more atmospheric side of the style.

**Anathema — Sleepless (Serenades, 1993)**
A UK death-doom classic where mournful melody and harsh vocals coexist in a uniquely intimate, sorrow-heavy way. It's pivotal for the genre's shift toward deeper emotional expression.

**Decomposed — Hope Finally Died... (Hope Finally Died..., 1993)**
An underground UK pillar that captures early death-doom's glacial riffs and gritty extremity. Its cult status reflects how foundational this raw form was before the style diversified.

**diSEMBOWELMENT — The Tree of Life and Death (Transcendence into the Peripheral, 1993)**
A genre-defining track famous for cavernous atmosphere and dramatic dynamic shifts between crushing slow sections and eerie space. It's hugely influential as a bridge between death-doom and funeral-doom textures.

**Katatonia — Gateways of Bereavement (Dance of December Souls, 1993)**
Swedish death-doom with signature melancholic melody and a deeply desolate tone. The track's atmosphere became a blueprint for later bands chasing emotional weight within extreme doom tempos.

**My Dying Bride — The Cry of Mankind (Turn Loose the Swans, 1993)**
A canonical, gothic-tinged death-doom anthem where doom pacing remains central and the sense of despair is unmistakable. Its distinctive instrumentation and lyrical intensity made it one of the style's most iconic songs.

**Rippikoulu — Musta seremonia (Musta seremonia, 1993)**
A Finnish classic built from massive, murky riff slabs and a primitive, crushing momentum. It's an essential document of death-doom's most monolithic early form.

**Unholy — Grayish World (From the Shadows, 1993)**
Avant-leaning Finnish death-doom that uses dissonance and unusual structure to deepen the bleak mood. It expanded the genre's expressive range beyond straightforward doom riff cycles.

**October Tide — 12 Days of Rain (Rain Without End, 1997)**
Melodic Swedish death-doom that balances heaviness with graceful, mournful lead writing. It's a key late-90s template for the more melodic, introspective direction of the style.

**Saturnus — Paradise Belongs to You (Paradise Belongs to You, 1997)**
Romantic, melody-forward death-doom that still moves at a true doom pace and weight. It exemplifies the late-90s European strand where sorrowful clean vocals became central.

**Runemagick — Enter the Realm of Death (Enter the Realm of Death, 1998)**
Old-school death-doom rooted in classic death-metal riffing but slowed and weighted into doom gravity. It's an important example of the genre's raw underground continuity into the late 90s.

**Morgion — Cloaked by Ages, Crowned in Earth (Cloaked by Ages, Crowned in Earth, 1999)**
US death-doom with ritualistic pacing and thick, oppressive atmosphere that feels almost ceremonial. It's a cult cornerstone for understanding the American side of death-doom's evolution.

**Swallow the Sun — Through Her Silvery Body (The Morning Never Came, 2003)**
A modern Finnish milestone that helped define 2000s melodic death-doom: heavy riffs, big hooks, and a strong sense of mournful narrative. It shows the style's shift toward more expansive, song-centered writing.

**Coffins — Buried Death (Buried Death, 2005)**
Japanese death-doom that keeps doom weight inside a death-metal frame, sounding filthy, oppressive, and slow. It's a crucial example of the 2000s global spread of the style beyond its early European centers.

**Novembers Doom — The Pale Haunt Departure (The Pale Haunt Departure, 2005)**
A US landmark that brought broad dynamics and strong melody to death-doom without losing heaviness. It helped re-energize the style mid-2000s and influenced many later melodic death-doom bands.

**Daylight Dies — A Dream for the Dead (Dismantling Devotion, 2006)**
Modern death-doom built on sturdy melodic architecture and a deeply elegiac atmosphere. It captures the 2000s wave's blend of harsh heaviness and carefully sculpted sorrow.

**Mourning Beloveth — The Sullen Sulcus (The Sullen Sulcus, 2006)**
Irish death-doom where long-form build and catharsis are central to the song's emotional logic. It's a modern classic that shows how the style can be both crushing and profoundly human.

**Hooded Menace — Fulfill the Curse (Fulfill the Curse, 2008)**
A 2000s revival track that brings old-school death-doom back with thick, sepulchral riffing and a graveyard tone. It's important for the resurgence of swampy, traditionalist death-doom in the modern era.
