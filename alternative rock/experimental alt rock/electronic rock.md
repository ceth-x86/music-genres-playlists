# Electronic Rock

## Foundations & Proto‑Electronic Rock (1962–1977)

**The Tornados — Telstar (Telstar (single), 1962)**
A truly early mainstream moment where an electronic lead timbre carries a rock arrangement into "space‑age" pop. It set the precedent for synthesizer-like hooks functioning as the song's frontman.

**The Beatles — Tomorrow Never Knows (Revolver, 1966)**
The studio becomes an electronic instrument—tape loops, processing, and texture layered onto a rock pulse. This is a foundational blueprint for electronic rock's idea that "band music" can be built from sonic manipulation.

**Silver Apples — Oscillations (Silver Apples, 1968)**
One of the first rock groups centered on oscillators and primitive electronic pulse, creating a machine-like groove with punky minimalism. It prefigures synth‑punk, industrial repetition, and electro‑rock's hypnotic churn.

**The United States of America — The American Metaphysical Circus (The United States of America, 1968)**
Psychedelic rock collage where electronic keyboards and studio trickery are compositional, not ornamental. It anticipates electronic rock's later fascination with montage, timbre, and texture as "riff."

**The Who — Baba O'Riley (Who's Next, 1971)**
Turns a sequenced synth pattern into the emotional engine of a stadium-rock anthem. It normalizes the "loop + band" architecture that later electronic rock repeatedly reactivates.

**Pink Floyd — On the Run (The Dark Side of the Moon, 1973)**
A rock-album centerpiece built from sequencing and sound design, proving electronic rhythm can function as rock dramaturgy. It's electronic motion with rock-scale narrative intent.

**Brian Eno — Needles in the Camel's Eye (Here Come the Warm Jets, 1973)**
Art-glam rock that foregrounds texture and processing rather than traditional riff primacy. Eno's approach anchors electronic rock's studio-built identity: timbre as attitude.

**Kraftwerk — Autobahn (Autobahn, 1974)**
A long-form fusion of guitar, synth melody, and machine discipline that defines the "human/machine" poise. Its motorik logic becomes a core template for later synth-rock and techno-leaning rock hybrids.

**Suicide — Ghost Rider (Suicide, 1977)**
Punk attitude delivered via drum-machine pulse and minimalist synth/guitar. It's proto-electronic rock before the label existed—raw, confrontational, and structurally loop-driven.

**David Bowie — Sound and Vision (Low, 1977)**
Berlin-era Bowie balances pop-rock songcraft with icy synths and deliberately modern rhythmic feel. It becomes a key reference point for post-punk and alternative artists seeking electronic color without losing drama.

## Synth‑Rock & Post‑Punk/New Wave Convergence (1978–1986)

**Blondie — Heart of Glass (Parallel Lines, 1978)**
A rock band fully commits to a disco-synth chassis, proving electronic rhythm and rock charisma can coexist without compromise. Its crossover success foreshadows electronic rock's permanent dancefloor adjacency.

**The Buggles — Video Killed the Radio Star (The Age of Plastic, 1979)**
Synthetic timbres and studio sheen, yet structured like classic pop-rock songwriting. It helped normalize electronics as pop's default language—crucial for later electro-rock hook craft.

**Gary Numan — Cars (The Pleasure Principle, 1979)**
Turns an icy synth riff into the functional equivalent of a guitar hook, with a deliberately "machine" persona. A canonical moment of synth-as-riff in electronic rock genealogy.

**The Cars — Let's Go (Candy‑O, 1979)**
New-wave rock where synth punctuation sharpens the groove rather than softening it. A clean example of electronic texture increasing rock's rhythmic precision and bite.

**Devo — Whip It (Freedom of Choice, 1980)**
Spiky guitars, synth bleeps, and stiff-beat minimalism make the band sound like an assembly line. It's a defining template for mechanized energy—electronic rock as concept and groove.

**Talking Heads — Once in a Lifetime (Remain in Light, 1980)**
Loop-minded production and synthetic pulse sit inside a rock-band framework. Its "live band meets system" logic is central to electronic rock aesthetics.

**Ultravox — Vienna (Vienna, 1980)**
Cinematic synth architecture wrapped around rock drama and dynamic pacing. It helped define European synth-rock's emotional grandeur.

**Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark — Electricity (Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark, 1980)**
Minimal synth hooks and rock-song skeleton executed with punk restraint. A key early statement of electronic rock's "less instrumentation, more impact" principle.

**Japan — Ghosts (Tin Drum, 1981)**
Austere electronics and art-rock melancholy presented with pop clarity. It points to the atmospheric wing of electronic rock where mood is the primary "riff."

**The Human League — Don't You Want Me (Dare, 1981)**
Electronic arrangement that behaves like a rock single—hook, contrast, and narrative tension—powered by drum-machine propulsion. It helps codify the genre's tight, mechanized groove vocabulary.

**Simple Minds — New Gold Dream (81‑82‑83‑84) (New Gold Dream (81‑82‑83‑84), 1982)**
Lush synth layers and driving drums create arena uplift with electronic sheen intact. A major precedent for later anthemic synth-rock.

**A Flock of Seagulls — I Ran (So Far Away) (A Flock of Seagulls, 1982)**
Shimmering synth lines and guitar accents produce a streamlined nocturnal drive. It codifies the bright, melodic end of electronic rock's spectrum.

**Eurythmics — Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This) (Sweet Dreams (Are Made of This), 1983)**
A bass-synth hook with the force of a classic rock riff, paired with hard-edged vocal stance. It demonstrates how electronic rock can feel physical without relying on distortion.

**New Order — Blue Monday (Blue Monday (single), 1983)**
A rock band retools itself around drum-machine sequencing and club arrangement logic. Its structure—builds, breakdowns, machine pulse—is foundational for dance-rock hybrids.

**Cabaret Voltaire — Sensoria (Micro‑Phonies, 1984)**
Industrial-funk repetition with electronic groove and guitar texture in the mix. It foreshadows electro-industrial rock's body-music discipline and abrasive timbre palette.

**Queen — Radio Ga Ga (The Works, 1984)**
Stadium rock translated into synth-led pop-rock with electronic percussion and futurist gloss. It legitimized electronics at the highest commercial tier of rock spectacle.

**Van Halen — Jump (1984, 1984)**
A hard-rock band letting a synth lead carry the entire anthem—no dilution, no apology. It cemented the synth-hook stadium banger as rock mainstream.

**Tears for Fears — Shout (Songs from the Big Chair, 1985)**
Stadium-scale emotional arc built from massive drums and synthetic textures. A precursor to electronic rock that aims for grandeur rather than grime.

**Sigue Sigue Sputnik — Love Missile F1‑11 (Flaunt It, 1986)**
Cyberpunk glam-rock theatrics driven by drum machines and synth riffing. A key early sign of electronic rock's futurist attitude and high-concept sonics.

**Skinny Puppy — Dig It (Mind: The Perpetual Intercourse, 1986)**
Aggressive electronics and distorted textures delivered with rock dynamics and hook memory. A crucial step from post-punk experimentation toward full industrial/electronic rock.

## Alternative Rock Goes Electronic (1990–1999)

**Depeche Mode — Personal Jesus (Violator, 1990)**
A bluesy guitar riff framed by clipped electronic percussion—electronic rock in its most direct "guitar + machine beat" form. It opened a path for rock-oriented songwriting inside electronic production.

**Jesus Jones — Right Here, Right Now (Doubt, 1991)**
Dance-rock that treats samples, sequenced feel, and big guitars as one pop engine. It captures the early‑90s moment when alternative rock learned to move like club music.

**Curve — Chinese Burn (Doppelgänger, 1992)**
Saturated guitar haze plus programmed/processed rhythm creates a sleek nocturnal hybrid. It proved electronic rock can be both abrasive and sensual without losing hook focus.

**Ministry — Just One Fix (Psalm 69, 1992)**
Grinding guitar, sampling, and mechanized groove welded into a relentless march. It crystallizes the industrial side of electronic rock with riffs that hit like physical objects.

**Nine Inch Nails — Closer (The Downward Spiral, 1994)**
A definitive cyber-groove: tight machine rhythm, transgressive hook, and guitar noise as texture. Few tracks embody electronic rock's bodily pulse vs. mechanical control as cleanly.

**Filter — Hey Man Nice Shot (Short Bus, 1995)**
Alternative-rock aggression sharpened by cold, processed production and a loop-like drive. A key example of 90s rock absorbing electronic sonics as attitude, not ornament.

**KMFDM — Juke Joint Jezebel (Nihil, 1995)**
Industrial dance-rock marrying club propulsion with anthem hooks. It helped move electronic rock from niche scene language into soundtrack-ready mainstream presence.

**Republica — Ready to Go (Republica, 1996)**
Bright synth motif and rock-band drive engineered for both radio and the dancefloor. It's a near-perfect snapshot of 90s electronic rock as pop adrenaline.

**Marilyn Manson — The Beautiful People (Antichrist Superstar, 1996)**
A stomping, quantized groove and synthetic noise layers turn rock into factory rhythm. Its cultural reach made industrial-tinged electronic rock a mainstream vocabulary.

**The Smashing Pumpkins — Eye (Lost Highway (soundtrack), 1997)**
A major alt-rock band makes electronics primary—beatwork and synth shimmer drive the song. It's a milestone for "guitar acts" discovering electronic arrangement as the core idea.

**U2 — Mofo (Pop, 1997)**
U2 graft big-beat circuitry and techno pulse onto stadium songwriting. It's a high-profile example of rock institutions committing to electronic dance logic.

**Primal Scream — Kowalski (Vanishing Point, 1997)**
Acid-tinged electronics meet rock heft and paranoia, pushing "band music" toward electronic extremity. A bridge between rave-era production and alternative-rock darkness.

**The Prodigy — Firestarter (The Fat of the Land, 1997)**
Distorted synth riffing and punk snarl generate a rock anthem through electronic means. It normalized heavy electronic rock for massive mainstream crowds.

**The Chemical Brothers — Setting Sun (Dig Your Own Hole, 1997)**
Big-beat breakage and rock swagger share the spotlight, proving fusion can be cinematic and hook-driven. A flagship for festival-scale electronic rock in the late 90s.

**The Crystal Method — Busy Child (Vegas, 1997)**
Breakbeat-driven aggression built for loud systems, with unmistakable rock muscle and attitude. It helped define electronica's rock-facing, riff-minded approach.

**Rammstein — Du Hast (Sehnsucht, 1997)**
Chugging guitars locked to electronic sequencing create a rigid, crowd-unifying groove. A global touchstone for the martial, mechanized end of electronic rock.

**The Cure — Wrong Number (Galore, 1997)**
The Cure update gothic rock with breakbeats and cut-and-paste electronic production. It proves electronic rock can be both danceable and emotionally bleak.

**Garbage — Push It (Version 2.0, 1998)**
Sleek, sample-aware production and synthetic low end reinforce a rock song's tension and release. An exemplary late‑90s blend of alternative songwriting with electronica-era polish.

**Stabbing Westward — Save Yourself (Darkest Days, 1998)**
Darkwave atmosphere and synth texture intensify a guitar-driven chorus rather than replacing it. A defining snapshot of late‑90s electronic rock melancholy and cinematic scale.

**Apollo 440 — Stop the Rock (Gettin' High on Your Own Supply, 1999)**
Big-beat maximalism built around a rock-riff sensibility and chant-ready hooks. It represents electronic rock embracing pure party momentum without losing bite.

## Electro‑Rock Boom and Club‑Rock Crossovers (2000–2009)

**Radiohead — Idioteque (Kid A, 2000)**
A rock band deconstructs itself into samples, drum-machine rhythms, and synthetic tension. It's central to 2000s electronic rock: guitars recede, but rock urgency and dread remain.

**Fischerspooner — Emerge (#1, 2001)**
Electroclash staged as a rock single—hook economy, verse/chorus drama, and performance persona. A defining early‑2000s club-rock crossover statement.

**Ladytron — Seventeen (Light & Magic, 2002)**
Cold metallic synths and guitar accents engineered for dancefloor tension and pop immediacy. It became a blueprint for modern synth-rock cool and austerity.

**The Rapture — House of Jealous Lovers (Echoes, 2003)**
Post-punk guitars played like funk stabs over a disco pulse—dance-punk's foundational move. It proved electronic-rock energy can be maximal without heavy distortion.

**Le Tigre — TKO (This Island, 2004)**
Drum machines, spiky guitar, and chant-ready vocals fused into activist electro‑punk. A landmark in DIY electronic rock that still hits like a live band.

**The Faint — Agenda Suicide (Danse Macabre, 2004)**
Tight electronic drums and sharp synth lines drive a rock song with punk velocity. The Faint's nervous, danceable intensity became key for mid‑2000s indie electro-rock.

**The Killers — Somebody Told Me (Hot Fuss, 2004)**
A synth-led hook and guitar propulsion make new-wave revival feel like modern alternative rock. It opened mainstream doors for electronic textures in 2000s rock radio.

**Soulwax — NY Excuse (Any Minute Now, 2004)**
Rock treated as club engineering: DJ logic, relentless groove focus, and physical arrangement discipline. A reference point for dance-rock that feels muscular rather than retro.

**Death in Vegas — Scorpio Rising (Scorpio Rising, 2004)**
Psych-rock guitars embedded in sleek electronic production, turning riffs into neon atmosphere. A strong example of cinematic electronic rock that still moves bodies.

**The Bravery — An Honest Mistake (The Bravery, 2005)**
Chop-tight rhythms and synth hooks framed as a rock single with machine polish. It epitomizes electro-rock's mid‑2000s bright, nervous momentum.

**LCD Soundsystem — Daft Punk Is Playing at My House (LCD Soundsystem, 2005)**
Rock swagger and chant cadence filtered through electronic dance minimalism. It demonstrates how irony, groove, and rock posture can coexist without weakening the pulse.

**CSS — Let's Make Love and Listen to Death from Above (Cansei de Ser Sexy, 2005)**
Gleeful collision of synths, guitars, and dance beats delivered with pop immediacy. A signature track of the indie-party branch of electronic rock.

**Goldfrapp — Ooh La La (Supernature, 2005)**
Glam attitude, stomping beat, and glossy synth riffing—electronic rock as disco-rock spectacle. It helped re-legitimize hook-first electro-rock in the mainstream.

**Daft Punk — Robot Rock (Human After All, 2005)**
A guitar sample rebuilt as a sequenced riff-monolith, proving rock signifiers can become machine loops. One of the clearest "rock riff by sequencer" statements.

**Shiny Toy Guns — Le Disko (We Are Pilots, 2006)**
Bright compressed guitars and synth bass locked to club-ready propulsion. It crystallizes the polished, radio-facing form of mid‑2000s electro-rock.

**Ratatat — Loud Pipes (Classics, 2006)**
Electronic rock instrumentals made from chopped guitar samples and hip-hop-adjacent beats. It shows "riff without band": rock energy through collage and loop logic.

**Muse — Starlight (Black Holes and Revelations, 2006)**
Arena dynamics powered by synth texture and precise electronic sheen. Muse demonstrate electronic rock at stadium scale—melodic immediacy with futurist gloss.

**Justice — Genesis (†, 2007)**
Rock impact translated into electronic distortion, chopped rhythm, and maximal pressure. A cornerstone of the bloghouse moment that pulled rock audiences into club sound.

**MGMT — Kids (Oracular Spectacular, 2007)**
Indie songwriting meets enormous synth hooks and rock backbeat propulsion. It helped normalize electronic-rock nostalgia (and irony) for a mass audience.

**The Presets — My People (Apocalypso, 2007)**
Cold rigid synth lines paired with rock-like tension/release designed for communal catharsis. A defining late‑2000s example of disciplined, dark club‑rock power.

**Pendulum — Propane Nightmares (In Silico, 2008)**
Drum-and-bass velocity harnessed into a rock song's hook and structure. Pendulum made the electronic-rock hybrid festival-ready with real band impact.

**Does It Offend You, Yeah? — We Are Rockstars (You Have No Idea What You're Getting Yourself Into, 2008)**
Overdriven synths and strobing beats collide with rock posturing into maximalist electro-rock. It captures the era's appetite for loud, genre-blurring dance-rock bangers.

**Innerpartysystem — Don't Stop (Innerpartysystem, 2008)**
A concise electro-rock anthem where the beat is as aggressive as the guitar. It sits perfectly between indie attitude and club propulsion.

**The Big Pink — Dominos (A Brief History of Love, 2009)**
A wall of synth noise and big chorus songwriting—electronic rock as texture-driven pop. It helped push noisy electronic production into indie-rock mainstream language.

**Editors — Papillon (In This Light and on This Evening, 2009)**
Dark arpeggiated synths and rock drums create a sleek nocturnal drive. A clear case of a guitar band re-centering itself around electronics.

## Modern Electronic Rock Hybrids (2010–2020)

**The Naked and Famous — Young Blood (Passive Me, Aggressive You, 2010)**
Soaring synth hooks and rock-scale lift fused into a widescreen anthem. It helped define 2010s electronic rock built around emotional uplift and "big" choruses.

**Phantogram — When I'm Small (Eyelid Movies, 2010)**
Trip-hop beats, thick synth bass, and guitar texture form a taut rock song by subtraction. It shows how electronic restraint can make rock hit harder through space and groove.

**Crystal Castles — Not in Love (Crystal Castles (II), 2010)**
Cold synth architecture and punchy rhythm underpin a chorus with stadium-scale release. A defining example of 2010s electronic rock's glossy menace and drama.

**Sleigh Bells — Rill Rill (Treats, 2010)**
Detuned guitars and blown-out texture ride an electronic pulse rather than traditional rock drumming. It reframed electronic rock as maximal, noisy, and candy-coated at once.

**M83 — Midnight City (Hurry Up, We're Dreaming, 2011)**
Built like rock—dynamics and catharsis—despite its synth-first palette. Its ubiquity made neon nostalgia a core 2010s electronic-rock affect.

**AWOLNATION — Sail (Megalithic Symphony, 2011)**
A hulking programmed beat and shouted hook create rock impact without conventional rock drumming. A proof-of-concept modern radio hit for electronic rock.

**Breathe Carolina — Blackout (Hell Is What You Make It, 2011)**
Pop-rock songwriting framed by EDM-era synth aggression and electronic percussion. It documents the 2010s turn toward festival electronics inside rock-adjacent forms.

**Imagine Dragons — Radioactive (Night Visions, 2012)**
Stomping electronic percussion engineered for arena choruses, with distorted texture as sonic identity. It became a mainstream blueprint for hook-centric electronic rock.

**Linkin Park — Burn It Down (Living Things, 2012)**
A major rock act pivoting toward synth bass and programmed rhythm while keeping rock tension and vocal urgency. It's electronic production becoming the core, not a layer.

**Metric — Synthetica (Synthetica, 2012)**
A manifesto-title and manifesto-sound: synth leads and rigid pulse with guitar as coloration. It distills electronic rock's key question—what stays "human" when the grid takes over?

**CHVRCHES — The Mother We Share (The Bones of What You Believe, 2013)**
Bright synth melody and rock-style drum energy produce a pop-sized anthem with band momentum. Representative of the clean, melodic wing of 2010s electronic rock.

**Atoms for Peace — Default (Amok, 2013)**
Rhythmic loops and electronic texture merge with live ensemble interplay into hypnotic tension. It presents electronic rock as band minimalism—pulse first, riffs second.

**Crosses — Bitches Brew (†††, 2013)**
Dark electronic atmospheres and guitar weight combine into a sleek nocturnal banger. A modern blueprint for moody, industrial-tinged electronic rock.

**Modestep — Feel Good (Evolution Theory, 2013)**
Dubstep-era sound design fused with rock song structure and vocal immediacy. It shows electronic rock absorbing bass-music aesthetics without abandoning hooks.

**Starset — My Demons (Transmissions, 2014)**
Cinematic synth layers and rock choruses built for maximum emotional lift. It represents the "sci-fi arena" branch of electronic rock—dramatic, polished, huge.

**PVRIS — St. Patrick (White Noise, 2014)**
A dark pulsing synth bassline drives the track while guitars reinforce tension rather than lead. A strong modern alt edge: heavy groove, sharp hooks.

**Twenty One Pilots — Stressed Out (Blurryface, 2015)**
Sparse electronic groove supports a rock-style chorus and emotive vocal urgency. Its reach shows how electronic rock syntax survives even when arrangements are minimal.

**HEALTH — TEARS (Death Magic, 2015)**
A relentless, danceable beat paired with shouted catharsis and grinding synth noise. It bridges club propulsion and rock intensity so the electronics feel physically aggressive.

**Enter Shikari — The Last Garrison (The Mindsweep, 2015)**
Post-hardcore energy welded to electronics: rigid grid, synth stabs, drop-like dynamics. It proves electronic rock's hybrid logic extends into heavier alternative scenes.

**The Black Queen — Ice to Never (Fever Daydream, 2016)**
Cold synth timbres and tight groove framed with dark-pop vocal clarity by industrial/alt veterans. It highlights electronic rock's mature, nocturnal, emotionally shaded mode.

**3TEETH — Atrophy (<shutdown.exe>, 2017)**
Stark industrial electronics and rock aggression built for live impact and political pressure. A modern exemplar of electronic rock's abrasive, protest-ready branch.

**Bring Me the Horizon — MANTRA (amo, 2019)**
A modern rock anthem rebuilt with electronic production: samples, synth layers, tightly edited dynamics. Key to the late‑2010s wave where heavy bands embraced pop-grade electronic gloss.

**half•alive — still feel. (Now, Not Yet, 2019)**
Funk-rock guitar and live feel arranged with electronic tightness and synthetic ear-candy. A bright route for electronic rock that is rhythmic, danceable, and clean-lined.

**Waterparks — Turbulent (FANDOM, 2019)**
Pop-punk songwriting pushed through modern electronic processing and synth accents. It captures how electronic rock techniques became standard mix-language for guitar-forward youth scenes.

**I DON'T KNOW HOW BUT THEY FOUND ME — Choke (Razzmatazz, 2020)**
Retro-synth tone and rock-drama choruses combine into a sharply produced modern single. A 2020s example of electronic rock's ongoing love affair with new-wave sonics and theatrical hooks.
