# Neoclassic folk

## Foundational Works / Early Pioneers

**Dead Can Dance — Cantara (Within the Realm of a Dying Sun, 1987)**
A seminal piece blending Gaelic folk melodies, Middle-Eastern rhythms, and baroque strings. Lisa Gerrard's soaring vocals and orchestration here directly inspired the neoclassical darkwave/dark folk movement.

**Death in June — Death is the Martyr of Beauty (But, What Ends When the Symbols Shatter?, 1992)**
An apocalyptic folk cornerstone enriched with mournful horn and cello accents. This elegiac ballad by DIJ – pioneers of the genre – adds a chamber-music gravitas to their acoustic guitar foundations.

**Current 93 — All the Pretty Little Horses (All the Pretty Little Horses, 1996)**
A haunting lullaby that exemplifies David Tibet's dark folk vision adorned with neoclassical flourishes. Childlike yet eerie, it layers acoustic guitar and violin in a minimalist classical arrangement, foreshadowing later neofolk/classical hybrids.

**Ordo Equitum Solis — Playing with the Fire (Animi Ægritudo, 1992)**
Medieval-tinged darkwave/folk by OES, a French-Italian duo blending ritual folk balladry with ethereal keyboards and zither. Leithana's solemn vocals and Deraclamo's folk guitar here create a melancholic, archaic atmosphere emblematic of early Neoclassical Folk.

**Love Is Colder Than Death — Wild World (Teignmouth, 1991)**
A German ethereal wave classic that merges folk song structure with neo-Renaissance instrumentation. Latin chants, dulcimer, and strings give this track a decidedly neoclassical darkwave feel, making it a blueprint for later heavenly-voices folk projects.

**Collection d'Arnell-Andréa — Tristesse des Mânes (Un Automne à Loroy, 1990)**
One of the earliest French acts to marry mournful cello, keyboard string pads and angelic female vocals. This track's elegiac, chanson-like quality and chamber instrumentation cemented the band as a foundational neoclassical darkwave ensemble.

**sToa — I Wish You Could Smile (Urthona, 1993)**
A German Neoclassical Darkwave gem: piano, violin and Britta Kühlmann's operatic soprano intertwine in a slow, haunting melody. sToa's music (akin to early Dead Can Dance) offers a pure distillation of the genre's ethereal-classical fusion.

**Nature and Organisation — Wicker Man Song (Beauty Reaps the Blood of Solitude, 1994)**
English neofolk with a true chamber music approach. Composed by Michael Cashmore (of Current 93) with string quartet and whispered vocals, this pagan-themed piece is a textbook neoclassical folk example – delicate, arcane, and steeped in Old World atmosphere.

**In The Nursery — To the Faithful (Stormhorse, 1987)**
A martial neoclassical instrumental that predates the term "neofolk." Twin brothers Klive and Nigel Humberstone merge militaristic drums with sweeping neoclassical synth strings. Its cinematic, "industrial folk" mood paved the way for later dark folk orchestration.

**Sol Invictus — In the Rain (Lex Talionis, 1988)**
A pioneering apocalyptic folk track enriched by mournful violin passages from Sol Invictus' early lineup. Tony Wakeford's acoustic guitar and plaintive baritone are elevated by classical string accents, hinting at the Romantic/Poetic Folk direction within Dark Folk.

## Orchestral Neofolk / Chamber Traditions

**Arcana — Angel of Sorrow (Dark Age of Reason, 1996)**
Ethereal neoclassical darkwave from Sweden. This piece features solemn Gregorian-style chants, cathedral-like percussion, and lush string synths, evoking medieval sacred music. Arcana's debut established the genre's atmospheric orchestral template.

**Elend — The Emperor (Leçons de Ténèbres, 1994)**
A bombastic French-Austrian composition merging baroque string arrangements, Latin choir and extreme dynamics. Elend push the neoclassical envelope to cinematic heights – from thunderous orchestration to moments of sublime choral beauty – illustrating the genre's dramatic potential.

**Die Verbannten Kinder Evas — Tales from Eternity (Die Verbannten Kinder Evas, 1995)**
Austrian project (by Summoning members) delivering "darkwave medieval" hymns. This track's slow waltzing rhythm, tender female vocals, and rich keyboard strings embody neoclassical darkwave.

**Dargaard — Eternity Rites (Eternity Rites, 1998)**
Gothic fantasy given musical form: Dargaard combines folk-like medieval melodies with symphonic keyboard orchestrations. Tharen's compositions and Elisabeth's soprano create a majestic, melancholic soundscape.

**Dark Sanctuary — L'Autre Monde (Royaume Mélancolique, 1999)**
French neoclassical darkwave at its finest – a dark atmospheric song with soprano vocals, church bell tolls, and a chamber ensemble feel. Dark Sanctuary's lush string arrangements and Latin choral sections envelop the listener in a brooding, elegiac aura.

**Ataraxia — Astraea (Historiae, 1998)**
Italian neoclassical darkwave blending archaic instrumentation (lute, frame drum) with modern classical sensibilities. Francesca Nicoli's otherworldly vocals and Ataraxia's "archaic meets modern" arrangements shine on this track, bridging medieval folk and art-song.

**Persephone — Immersion (Home, 2006)**
A beautiful exhibit of musical nostalgia by Sonja Kraushofer's project. "Immersion" pairs her crystalline voice with a mournful cello and piano trio, resembling a gothic chamber lied. The piece's neoclassical structure and poetic lyrics epitomize the Romantic/Poetic side of dark folk.

**Unto Ashes — Palästinalied (Empty Into White, 2003)**
A modern interpretation of a 13th-century crusader song, arranged with medieval instruments (oud, dulcimer) and strings. Unto Ashes, an American ensemble, turn a Middle High German hymn into a neoclassical folk anthem – reverent, melodic, and atmospheric.

**Empyrium — Die Schwäne im Schilf (Weiland, 2002)**
Empyrium's acoustic era distilled: this instrumental ("The Swans in the Reeds") paints a nocturnal nature scene through classical guitar, flute, and cello. A sublime example of dark forest folk elevated to art song.

**Tenhi — Hallavedet (Kauan, 1999)**
Finnish dark folk with a minimalist classical touch. "Hallavedet" ("Frost Waters") flows with mournful piano, acoustic guitar arpeggios, and cello, all in a minor key. This piece's wintry ambiance and nature-themed introspection are quintessential to the genre's Nordic branch.

**Vàli — Høstmelankoli (Forlatt, 2004)**
An instrumental purely instrumental neo-folk work. Norwegian musician Vàli uses only acoustic guitar and cello to craft "Autumn Melancholy" – a piece of quiet, profound beauty that exemplifies the intimate, pastoral side of neoclassical folk.

**Nest — The Silvershade Lynx (Trail of the Unwary, 2007)**
Finnish duo Nest incorporate the traditional kantele zither into dark ambient folk. This track adds cello to their signature kantele, creating a reflective instrumental that feels ancient and modern at once.

**Amber Asylum — Heavenstorm (The Natural Philosophy of Love, 2000)**
A Californian quartet known for haunting, disturbing and fragile beauty. "Heavenstorm" unleashes layered violins and cellos over doom-laden drums and whispered female voice. This stormy piece feels like chamber music for a gothic cathedral.

**Autumn Tears — The Hallowing (The Hallowing, 2007)**
A U.S. neoclassical ensemble featuring full orchestra (strings, woodwinds, brass) and choir underpinning operatic vocals. An epic, neoclassical piece with lush strings and orchestral soundscapes, epitomizing the genre's grand, melancholic scope.

**Ashram — Elizabeth (Shining Silver Skies, 2006)**
Italian trio Ashram deliver romantic neoclassical folk with piano, violin and male vocals. "Elizabeth" is a bittersweet mini-opera where classical piano and emotive violin carry a crooning voice. A moving blend of baroque chamber pop and folk sentiment.

**Narsilion — My Falling Darkness (Namarie, 2004)**
Spanish duo fusing Celtic fantasy and darkwave. This track surrounds duet vocals with harp, violin, and symphonic keyboards, creating a dreamy and melancholic sound. Narsilion exemplify the "elfin" wing of neoclassical folk – idyllic yet mournful.

**All My Faith Lost... — Land's End (As You're Vanishing, 2005)**
Italian ensemble known for an ethereal blend of neoclassical, gothic, darkwave and neofolk. "Land's End" features delicate female/male vocals over a gentle string quartet and acoustic guitar.

**Camerata Mediolanense — Lacrime di Gioia (Lacrime di Gioia, 2013)**
An Italian neoclassical darkwave group founded in 1986, famed for blending Renaissance music with dark folk. This track ("Tears of Joy") deploys operatic vocals, martial drums, and string ensemble in a neoclassical arrangement with cello, viola, and polyrhythmic percussion.

**Hexperos — Autumnus (The Garden of the Hesperides, 2007)**
Led by Alessandra Santovito, Hexperos offers elegant neo-Baroque folk. "Autumnus" weds her classically-trained soprano with cello, Celtic harp, and flute, evoking a mythic, autumnal mood. This Italian act demonstrates the genre's overlap with early-music and Celtic influences.

**Forseti — Jenzig (Windzeit, 2002)**
A German dark folk piece dedicated to a Thuringian hill. In "Jenzig" they incorporate cello (by guest Matt Howden of Sieben) to rich effect. The instrumental track's pastoral melody and classical elegance exemplify the "Forest Folk" strand of Neoclassical Folk.

**Orplid — Die Flut (Nächtliche Jünger, 2005)**
Orplid, one of Germany's premier apocalyptic folk duos, here deliver a piano-led art song ("The Flood"). Uwe Nolte's deep voice recites poetry over Frank Machau's neo-Romantic piano and subtle strings, building to a dramatic climax. A perfect example of the genre's Romantic/Poetic Folk direction.

**Fire + Ice — Gaudete (Birdking, 2000)**
British artist Ian Read (ex-Death In June) performs the Latin carol "Gaudete" with a pagan folk twist – cellos, recorders and hand drums accompany his vocals. On this traditional piece they lean into early music, demonstrating how Neoclassical Folk often reimagines medieval songs through a dark lens.

**Darkwood — Flammende Welt (Notwendfeuer, 2006)**
German neofolk outfit known for austere, runic songs. "Flammende Welt" ("World in Flames") stands out for its prominent cello lines weaving around acoustic guitar and whispered vocals. The classical string presence adds depth and sorrow to Darkwood's minimalist folk.

**Sonne Hagal — Midwinternacht (Jordansfrost, 2008)**
A wintry piece by this German project, featuring strummed lute, male chorus, and violin/cello arrangements. On "Midwinternacht" the classical string arrangements and choral elements create an almost hymn-like quality, aligning with Neoclassical Folk's spiritual, solemn side.

**Ordo Rosarius Equilibrio — Reaping the Fallen... (Reaping the Fallen... EP, 2001)**
Swedish duo ORE's erotic apocalyptic folk often features militaristic drums and lush strings. This track layers acoustic guitar and whispered duets over a looping minor-key string theme – a dark cabaret waltz illustrating how martial folk acts use neoclassical elements to elevate mood and drama.

**Backworld — Holy Ground (Holy Fire, 1999)**
Led by American folk troubadour Joseph Budenholzer, Backworld produces gentle dark folk hymns. "Holy Ground" is adorned with sorrowful cello drones and nylon-string guitar, plus Budenholzer's Leonard Cohen-esque baritone. Intimate yet arranged like a chamber folk prayer.

**Gae Bolg and the Church of Fand — Dies Irae (Requiem, 2000)**
A bombastic medieval-folk project from France. Gae Bolg reinterprets the Latin Dies Irae chant with orchestral brass, percussion, and choir. Theatrical and apocalyptic, highlighting the genre's penchant for reimagining liturgical or martial music in a dark folk context.

**Lux Interna — King Winter (God is Not Dead for the Birds, 2013)**
U.S. band Lux Interna infuse Americana-tinged neofolk with cello and violin arrangements. "King Winter" unfolds slowly with acoustic strums, deep vocals, and a mournful cello hovering beneath – like a solemn folk hymn.

**Corde Oblique — Cantastorie (The Stones of Naples, 2009)**
An Italian collective blending Mediterranean folk and neo-classical composition. "Cantastorie" is upbeat yet baroque – intricate classical guitar work, violin, and passionate female vocals give it a Renaissance folk flavor.

**Spiritual Front — Autopsy of a Love (Armageddon Gigolo, 2006)**
Italy's Spiritual Front here offer a morbid love song arranged like a tango for piano, strings, and crooning voice. Think Ennio Morricone meets neofolk: cinematic lounge quality, yet dark lyrics and minor-key string swells root it in the neoclassical folk tradition.

**Eden — The Slow Bells (Healingbow, 1993)**
Australian band Eden were early in fusing gothic rock with folk and neoclassical touches. "The Slow Bells" is a tranquil piece led by hammered dulcimer, soft tenor vocals, and a mournful violin weaving through.

**Ophelia's Dream — Holy Eyes (All Beauty Is Sad, 2000)**
A German neoclassical duo creating "dark romantic" moods. "Holy Eyes" sets poetic English lyrics against dramatic piano chords and lush strings with an almost cinematic build. Cornelia Hermann's vocals soar operatically, perfectly embodying the genre's gothic-classical synergy.

**Mirabilis — The Nightingale (Pleiades, 2004)**
An American female duo combining neo-Medieval folk and classical vocal styles. "The Nightingale" is a traditional folk song rendered with two-part soprano harmonies, harp, and viola. The pure, crystalline voices and ancient melody give it a "heavenly voices" aura.

**Irfan — Hagia Sophia (Seraphim, 2007)**
Bulgarian ensemble Irfan mix Balkan, Middle Eastern and Byzantine influences with Dead Can Dance-inspired ethereal folk. "Hagia Sophia" layers exotic instruments (darbuka, oud) with a stirring choral chant and dark orchestral synths. Mystical and cinematic.

**Daemonia Nymphe — Dance of the Satyrs (Daemonia Nymphe, 2002)**
A Greek project recreating the music of Antiquity with reproductions of ancient lyres and flutes. "Dance of the Satyrs" is instrumental except for ritual chants, featuring classical Greek lyre melodies and frame drums. An entrancing piece bridging historical folk and neoclassical ambient.

## Ambient and Cinematic Directions

**Ulver — Kveldssang (Kveldssanger, 1996)**
A pivotal track from Ulver's acoustic album. A gentle hymn of classical guitar, layered male chorale and flute, it stands as a pure dark folk instrumental. Ulver, known for black metal, here trade distortion for acoustic guitars, cellos and choir vocals. Deeply influential on later nature-themed neofolk acts.

**Nox Arcana — Transylvania (Transylvania, 2005)**
American duo Nox Arcana specialize in gothic ambient soundtracks. "Transylvania" is a dark instrumental with tolling bells, brooding strings, and a haunting piano motif – essentially a neo-classical horror score in miniature.

**Black Tape for a Blue Girl — Across a Thousand Blades (Remnants of a Deeper Purity, 1996)**
From Sam Rosenthal's Projekt Records collective, this song is a darkwave classic that crosses into chamber-folk territory. It features a string quartet (cello, viola) underpinning Oscar Herrera's emotive vocals. Its success helped popularize string-laden ethereal darkwave in the '90s.

**Musk Ox — Un Jardin Au Bout du Monde (Musk Ox, 2007)**
A Canadian instrumental chamber-folk piece. Musk Ox – an ensemble of classical guitar, cello, and violin – create a tranquil yet expansive ambiance, embodying the neoclassical precision and folk earthiness of the genre's ambient side.

**Les Fragments de la Nuit — Dans les ombres du vent (Musique du crépuscule, 2008)**
A French modern classical ensemble. This track ("In the Shadows of the Wind") is entirely instrumental: interwoven piano and violin lines build a nocturnal, waltzing theme. Chamber music that could score a silent folkloric film.

**October Falls — Viima (Marras, 2005)**
October Falls (Finland) began in the dark ambient/folk realm. "Viima" ("Cold Wind") is an early acoustic instrumental showcasing fingerpicked guitar over a backdrop of forest sounds and distant cello drones. Minimal yet cinematic.

**Neun Welten — Jörd (Destrunken, 2009)**
German for "Nine Worlds," Neun Welten play melancholic instrumental folk. "Jörd" opens with pastoral acoustic guitar and slowly adds cello and subtle percussion; its structure is more post-rock or soundtrack-like than song-like. This piece breathes and expands like a misty morning over fields.

**Swans — God Damn the Sun (The Burning World, 1989)**
An unexpected inclusion: New York post-punk legends Swans delivered this dark folk ballad at the end of the '80s. Michael Gira's lament is accompanied by spare piano, acoustic guitar, and mournful cello. Its stark, depressive beauty foreshadowed the gloomy chamber-folk aesthetics later embraced by others in the genre.

**Chelsea Wolfe — Flatlands (Unknown Rooms EP, 2013)**
Contemporary American singer-songwriter often filed under occult folk. "Flatlands" is a standout: stark strings (violin) accompany her haunting voice in a dirge-like folk melody. Its inclusion highlights how newer artists outside the European neofolk scene also tap into Neoclassical Folk's ethos.

## Modern Compositional Folk (2010s–Present)

**ROME — The Accidents of Gesture (Nera, 2006)**
Luxembourgish artist Jérôme Reuter is a leading modern neofolk troubadour. This track is a martial folk anthem with military snare rolls, brass fanfares, and dramatic strings underpinning his baritone. Its polished production shows the genre's evolution into cinematic territory, yet the core – acoustic guitar and melancholic melody – remains pure folk noir.

**Sieben — Love's Promise (Sex and Wildflowers, 2005)**
Sieben (UK violinist Matt Howden) creates one-man "looped" neofolk built from layers of live violin. "Love's Promise" starts with rhythmic percussive bowing, adds multiple violin harmonies and whispered vocals, building to a rich tapestry – all from strings. Innovative and hypnotic, feeling both ancient and avant-garde.

**Twin Bridges — Fertile Ashes (Fertile Ashes, 2023)**
A Portland-based chamber pop / neo-classical folk project. Led by cellist Zach Gerzon, the title track features cello as lead instrument, supported by guitar and hushed vocals, recorded in resonant spaces for organic warmth. A contemporary act bringing Neoclassical Folk into personal, singer-songwriter territory without losing the chamber aura.

**King Dude — River of Gold (Fear, 2014)**
An American artist blending gothic country with dark folk. "River of Gold" is a solemn, gospel-tinged song enriched by violin and piano arrangements. It channels Johnny Cash-esque Americana through a neoclassical lens, illustrating the genre's expansion beyond Europe.

**The Visit — Offering (Through Darkness Into Light, 2015)**
A Canadian duo (cellist Raphael Weinroth-Browne and vocalist Heather Sita Black) producing cello & voice chamber pieces. "Offering" is a stunning showcase of just those two elements: expressive cello arpeggios and a classically-trained mezzo-soprano delivering poetic lyrics.

**Carved in Stone — The Woman and the Dragon (Tales of Glory & Tragedy, 2002)**
Carved in Stone (Germany) is the solo project of vocalist Ilona Jeschke, who creates dark pagan folk with harp and vocals. This track incorporates a string ensemble and choral layers alongside her folk melodies, elevating the simple harp song into a neoclassical lament.

**Sopor Aeternus & The Ensemble of Shadows — Soror (Suicide Waltz) (Es reiten die Toten so schnell, 2003)**
Sopor Aeternus is an icon of gothic music known for incorporating classical baroque instruments. "Soror" is literally a funeral waltz – 3/4 time, with string quartet, tuba, and woodwinds supporting Anna-Varney Cantodea's theatrical vocals. Morbid and beautiful, exemplifying how Neoclassical Folk can overlap with gothic theatricality.

**Kannonau — Romance & Wrath (Kannonau, 2006)**
Italian neo-folk act blending ritual ambient with folk structures. "Romance & Wrath" is driven by martial drums and droning harmonium, but adorned with violin and chanted vocals that give it a liturgical feel. Like a forgotten folk ritual set to a classical dirge.

**Myrkur — Ella (Folkesange, 2020)**
Danish artist Myrkur (Amalie Bruun), known in metal circles, surprised everyone with Folkesange, an album of Scandinavian folk songs. "Ella" features Bruun's crystal-clear voice over nyckelharpa (Nordic keyed fiddle), lyre, and a small string ensemble. A modern example of folk tradition meets neo-classical polish.

**In Gowan Ring — The Wind that Cries (The Glinting Spade, 1999)**
A psychedelic folk project, but on this track B'eirth moves into pastoral classical realms. It starts as a gentle acoustic ballad and gradually introduces cello and recorder, building an atmosphere of Renaissance courtly music filtered through 60s acid folk. Hypnotic and timeless.

**IANVA — La Ballata di San Giusto (Disobbedisco!, 2006)**
Italian collective blending military pop, cabaret and folk. This track is like stepping into a 1940s salon: an upbeat martial drum rhythm, accordion and trumpet fanfares, and a duet of deep male and dramatic female vocals. IANVA's work, often called "folk noir opera," demonstrates a modern, grandiose take on neoclassical folk.

**:Of the Wand & the Moon: — Immer Vorwärts (Sonnenheim, 2005)**
Kim Larsen's Danish neofolk project. "Immer Vorwärts" ("Always Forward") combines strident acoustic strumming with orchestral brass and snare drums. Essentially a marching song turned folk ballad, its triumphant horn sections and cinematic drums illustrate the overlap between neoclassical instrumentation and apocalyptic folk themes.

**Emily Jane White — Dark Undercoat (Dark Undercoat, 2008)**
A contemporary American singer at the crossroads of indie, folk, and gothic Americana. The title track is a stark, brooding folk song adorned with a lonely cello line that shadows her voice. The simplicity of guitar and cello accompaniment gives it a chamber-folk feel.

**Changes — Twilight of the West (Orphan in the Storm, 2004)**
Chicago-based Changes are true pioneers – originally active in the late 1960s and later revived. "Twilight of the West" finds this duo backed by a sorrowful violin and subtle synth strings in addition to their dual acoustic guitars. Even veteran folk musicians embraced neoclassical atmospheres to amplify their message.

**Allerseelen — Santa Sangre (Abenteuerliches Herz, 2002)**
Austrian project led by Gerhard Hallstatt, known for ritualistic industrial-folk. "Santa Sangre" ("Holy Blood") is an instrumental interlude that layers a looping, militant drum rhythm with somber synth-strings and choir samples – a neo-classical martial ambient piece highlighting the genre's mystical and esoteric strains.

**Art Abscons — The Wood Nymphs (Der verborgene Gott, 2010)**
A German act delivering occult folkloristic music. "The Wood Nymphs" is an ethereal track where whispered German vocals and acoustic guitar are enveloped by lush string synths and nature sounds. It feels like a nocturne in a haunted forest.

**Sunset Wings — Castle in the Clouds (Lake of a Red Dawn, 2012)**
Hailing from Kaliningrad, Russia – Sunset Wings produce melodic dark folk steeped in English poetry. "Castle in the Clouds" (inspired by Yeats) features gentle male vocals over acoustic guitar, flute, and cello, with distant chimes. A dreamy, rain-soaked quality.

**Agalloch — Birch White (The White EP, 2008)**
A unique entry by American metal band Agalloch, who on this acoustic EP embraced neofolk/ambient completely. "Birch White" adapts a poem by A.S.J. Tessimond into song, delivered with strummed guitars, subtle percussion, and a mournful violin. Agalloch's foray here was widely appreciated in the neofolk community as a crossover success.

**Bacio di Tosca — Der Wanderer über dem Nebelmeer (Der Tod ist ein Tänzer, 2004)**
Operatic soprano Dörthe Flemming's project sets German Romantic poetry to darkwave music. This song, titled after Caspar David Friedrich's famous painting, features classically-trained vocals over electronic strings and piano. Essentially lied meets darkwave – demonstrating Neoclassical Folk's deep roots in Romantic era aesthetics.

**Aurora Sutra — The Land of Harm and Appletrees (The Land of Harm and Appletrees, 1993)**
A lesser-known early '90s act from Germany. The title track is a floating darkwave piece built on slow tribal drums, atmospheric synth pads, and prominent violin solos. An underground gem that still stands as a gorgeous piece of melancholic violin-led ambient folk.

**Rising Shadows — Embraced by the Shadows (Found in the Cold, 2008)**
A Swedish project blending darkwave and neo-folk. "Embraced by the Shadows" unfolds with piano and cello setting a somber tone, joined by Therése Hansson's soft vocals. The mood is akin to Arcana or Dargaard – gothic and graceful.

**WeltenBrand — The End of the Wizard (Das Rabenland, 1997)**
From Liechtenstein, WeltenBrand fused medieval legend with darkwave. "The End of the Wizard" is a narrative song with spoken male vocals and angelic female chorus over symphonic keyboard and violin. The track feels like a dark fairy tale capturing the neo-medieval facet of Neoclassical Folk.

**Midnight Syndicate — Haunted Nursery (Midnight Syndicate, 1997)**
A piece that exemplifies the haunted ambient aesthetic sometimes embraced by neoclassical folk fans. "Haunted Nursery" is a short instrumental of music box chimes, distant child-like humming, and eerie string pads – a dark lullaby serving as an eerie coda.

**Leonard Cohen — Who by Fire (New Skin for the Old Ceremony, 1974)**
Bonus inclusion: Though predating the genre, Cohen's song – a setting of a Hebrew prayer – is backed by solemn chant-like female vocals and strings. Its dark, spiritual tone and chamber-folk arrangement had a notable influence on early apocalyptic folk musicians. An honorary precursor to Neoclassical Folk's marriage of folk song and liturgical atmosphere.
