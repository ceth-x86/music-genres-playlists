# Shoegaze

## Proto-shoegaze foundations (1985–1989)

**The Jesus and Mary Chain — Just Like Honey (Psychocandy, 1985)**
While predating the term, its feedback-laced guitar haze and softened vocals directly prefigure shoegaze's central sound priorities. It's historically vital for establishing the idea that noise and pop melody can coexist inside a blurred, immersive mix.

**A.R. Kane — Up (69, 1988)**
Often cited as a crucial precursor to shoegaze, this track foregrounds texture—guitars smear into atmosphere and vocals recede into the soundfield. It belongs here because the 'gaze' aesthetic (effects-first immersion) is already dominant rather than merely decorative.

**The Telescopes — Flying (Taste, 1989)**
An early, noisier strain of shoegaze built from feedback loops, drone, and smeared harmonics. It captures the pre-Loveless moment where texture was already becoming the main narrative.

## II. UK & Ireland golden age (1990–1994)

**Pale Saints — Kinky Love (The Comforts of Madness, 1990)**
A classic of the '4AD wing' of shoegaze: crystalline guitars smear into noise while the vocal lines remain ghostly and intimate. Its balance of melody and abrasion became a key template for later bands.

**Lush — Sweetness and Light (Sweetness and Light (EP), 1990)**
A bright, propulsive take on shoegaze where jangling riffs are swallowed by chorus and reverb. Lush show how the style can be energetic and pop-aware while remaining unmistakably 'gaze' in texture.

**Ride — Vapour Trail (Nowhere, 1990)**
Ride's most distilled anthem pairs chiming melodies with oceanic reverb and surging distortion. It codified the genre's 'big chorus, buried voice' dynamic without drifting into britpop or straight alt-rock.

**Chapterhouse — Pearl (Whirlpool, 1991)**
Big, ringing guitar layers and forward-driving drums capture the genre's danceable side without leaving the haze. A quintessential Creation-era cut that helped define the early-90s UK sound.

**Kitchens of Distinction — Drive That Fast (Strange Free World, 1991)**
A bridge between indie-rock songcraft and full shoegaze saturation, with guitars that smear into luminous sheets. It helped widen the genre's emotional palette without slipping into adjacent jangle-pop territory.

**Swervedriver — Duel (Raise, 1991)**
Swervedriver tilt shoegaze toward kinetic, road-motor momentum—still drenched in effects, but with muscular riffs. It's crucial for showing the genre's range beyond pure dreaminess.

**Revolver — Heaven Sent an Angel (Cold Water Flat, 1991)**
A deep-cut classic: soft-focus vocals and layered guitars create a slow-moving cloud of sound. Revolver's understatement represents the genre's quieter, more intimate side.

**My Bloody Valentine — Only Shallow (Loveless, 1991)**
A defining statement of shoegaze: detuned, harmonically rich guitars blur into a single 'engine' while vocals melt into the mix. Its studio techniques and sheer impact became the genre's benchmark for density and sensation.

**The Boo Radleys — Lazarus (Everything's Alright Forever, 1992)**
Before their stylistic pivot, The Boo Radleys delivered dense, spiraling guitars and blissed-out vocals in classic shoegaze fashion. It's an important reminder of how many UK bands briefly lived inside the haze.

**Curve — Coast Is Clear (Doppelgänger, 1992)**
Curve inject industrial-tinged punch into shoegaze's fog, keeping the guitars thick and the vocals half-submerged. The track's hard edges mark an influential boundary between gaze and alternative dance-rock.

**Catherine Wheel — Black Metallic (Ferment, 1992)**
A slow-build epic where tremolo guitar and feedback bloom into a towering wall of sound. It demonstrates how shoegaze can sustain long-form dynamics without becoming post-rock or generic alt.

**Moose — Jack (XYZ, 1992)**
Moose embody the scene's romantic, swirling guitar-pop core—hooky, but coated in chorus and shimmer. This track is emblematic of the UK 'second tier' that nonetheless defined shoegaze's ecosystem.

**Bleach — Trip (Killing Time, 1992)**
A punchy UK example that keeps the vocals recessed and the guitars thick with modulation. It shows shoegaze's ability to be direct and riff-led while still texturally overwhelming.

**The Verve — She's a Superstar (A Storm in Heaven, 1993)**
Early Verve sit squarely in the haze: elastic bass, psychedelic guitar wash, and distant vocals cohere into classic shoegaze propulsion. It marks the genre's overlap with neo-psychedelia without leaving the shoegaze center.

**Adorable — Sunshine Smile (Against Perfection, 1993)**
Adorable capture the tail-end of the UK wave with muscular guitars and wistful vocal lines set deep in the mix. It's a key example of shoegaze evolving toward darker, more anthemic contours.

**Blind Mr. Jones — Elizabeth's Trip (Stereo Musicale, 1993)**
An archetypal 'underground' shoegaze track: buoyant melodies buried under haze and a gentle, narcotic groove. It's beloved for compressing the genre's dream-state into concise pop form.

**Secret Shine — Loveblind (Loveblind (EP), 1993)**
Secret Shine deliver pure shoegaze geometry—tremolo, chorus, and reverb stacked into a bright wall. The track became a cult anchor for later 'rediscovery' waves of the genre.

**Slowdive — When the Sun Hits (Souvlaki, 1993)**
Slowdive fuse haze and songcraft: soft vocals and suspended chords float over slow-motion drum patterns and radiant feedback. It's a textbook example of emotional clarity achieved through sonic overload.

**Spirea X — Speed Reaction (Fireblade Skies, 1994)**
A Scottish cult gem that distills shoegaze's ecstatic rush into swirling, high-gain guitars and breathy vocal lines. Its maximalist shimmer prefigures later revival bands' love for 'sparkle-noise.'

## III. Global 90s diaspora (1990–1997)

**Lilys — Elizabeth Colour Wheel (In the Presence of Nothing, 1992)**
Lilys channel early-90s UK shoegaze sonics with near-obsessive accuracy: tremolo shimmer, dense overdubs, and softened vocals. The track is central to the genre's transatlantic diffusion.

**Drop Nineteens — Kick the Tragedy (Delaware, 1992)**
The definitive American shoegaze single: thick, chorus-soaked guitars and a soaring, half-buried vocal hook. It translated the UK template into a distinctly US college-rock context without losing the genre's fog.

**Swirlies — Pancake (Blonder Tongue Audio Baton, 1992)**
Swirlies push shoegaze into more lo-fi, collage-like territory while keeping the core traits—washy guitars, recessed vocals, and sensation-over-clarity. It's pivotal for the American underground branch of the style.

**Medicine — Time Baby III (Shot Forth Self Living, 1992)**
Medicine intensify shoegaze's noise component—feedback and distortion dominate, but the song remains hypnotically structured. It's a key US document of shoegaze as physical, abrasive sound-mass.

**Bailter Space — Robot World (Robot World, 1993)**
Bailter Space bring a harsher, more mechanical edge to shoegaze's wall-of-sound approach. The track's distortion and repetition show how the style can be muscular without collapsing into straight noise-rock.

**Coaltar of the Deepers — Dear Future (Submerge, 1993)**
Japanese shoegaze at high velocity—dense guitars and ecstatic hooks ride over driving drums. The track is foundational for the later Japanese 'gaze' explosion while remaining firmly within classic shoegaze traits.

**lovesliescrushing — babysbreatbe (bloweyelashwish, 1993)**
An ambient-leaning classic where guitars dissolve into pure texture and the voice becomes another instrument. It captures the genre's 'sonic immersion' ideal at its most narcotic and abstract.

**Sianspheric — Pulsar (Somnium, 1994)**
Canadian shoegaze that leans cosmic without becoming pure space-rock: guitars bloom into wide, sustained sheets and the groove stays anchored. It's a key example of the genre's cinematic scale in the 90s diaspora.

**Starflyer 59 — Blue Collar Love (Gold, 1994)**
A rare case of shoegaze infused with tight, song-forward alternative rock while preserving the haze. The track became a cornerstone for US bands who wanted riffs and walls of sound together.

**The Veldt — Soul in a Jar (Afrodisiac, 1994)**
The Veldt blend shoegaze guitar saturation with funk-inflected rhythm and soulful phrasing, expanding what the style could contain. It's historically important for diversifying the genre's lineage and feel.

**Majesty Crush — Butterflies Don't Go Away (Fan, 1995)**
A beloved Detroit-era shoegaze cut with bright melodies submerged under thick modulation and reverb. It's emblematic of the US regional scenes that carried the sound past the UK's early-90s peak.

**Alison's Halo — Dozen (Eyedazzler, 1995)**
An exemplary 'slow-motion' shoegaze piece: airy vocals drift over chiming guitars and swelling reverb. Widely revered in underground circles, it became a touchstone for later revivalists.

**Hum — Stars (You'd Prefer an Astronaut, 1995)**
A hugely influential 'heavy gaze' prototype: massive, saturated guitars and a sense of enveloping blur drive the song's emotional impact. Later modern shoegaze and grungegaze bands drew heavily on this balance of riffs and haze.

**Bowery Electric — Beat (Beat, 1996)**
A crucial left-field shoegaze entry: guitar haze meets downtempo, hip-hop-informed pulse while keeping the vocals distant. It illustrates how the genre's texture-first logic could migrate into new rhythmic frameworks.

**The Autumns — The Angel Pool (The Angel Pool, 1997)**
A cult favorite of the post-UK era, built on shimmering guitars and slow, enveloping crescendos. The Autumns helped carry shoegaze's emotional grammar into the late 90s without rebranding it as post-rock or dream-pop.

**Half String — Submarine (Salamander, 1997)**
A late-90s American shoegaze standout that keeps the genre's core intact: vocals sunk in the mix and guitars layered into a bright, continuous wash. It shows how shoegaze survived the 90s by moving into smaller regional scenes.

## IV. 2000s underground & nu-gaze ignition (1998–2009)

**Auburn Lull — Snowflake (Alone I Admire, 2001)**
Auburn Lull's slow-moving guitar haze and delicate vocal presence exemplify the genre's gentler, 'glacial' side. It's a key early-2000s document of shoegaze continuing outside the spotlight.

**Astrobrite — Crush (Crush, 2001)**
Astrobrite crystallize the 'micro-detail' side of shoegaze: hyper-compressed guitars stack into a shimmering blur while the melody peeks through like light behind fog. The track became a core reference point for the 2000s underground revival.

**My Vitriol — Always: Your Way (Finelines, 2001)**
A key bridge between classic shoegaze and early-2000s alternative: the guitars form a thick, shimmering wall and the vocal sits inside the texture rather than on top. It's widely regarded as one of the era's most convincing 'post-90s' shoegaze anthems.

**Luminous Orange — Drop You Vivid Colours (Drop You Vivid Colours, 2002)**
A seminal Japanese shoegaze cut that pairs thick guitar wash with direct, memorable songwriting. It captures the early-2000s moment when shoegaze became a living international language, not a UK-only artifact.

**Film School — Pitfalls (Film School, 2002)**
Film School translate classic shoegaze textures into sleek 2000s indie production—guitars remain foggy, vocals stay recessed, and the chorus blooms through distortion. It's emblematic of the genre's post-1990s persistence in the US.

**The Meeting Places — Find Yourself Along the Way (Find Yourself Along the Way, 2003)**
A revival-era cornerstone built on cascading guitar layers and a steady, hypnotic pulse. It shows how shoegaze could return as serious, song-centric craft rather than mere retro pastiche.

**Amusement Parks on Fire — Venus in Furs (Out of the Angeles, 2004)**
A lush, fully saturated 2000s shoegaze anthem—big choruses and thick guitars, with vocals folded into the haze. The track was influential in reintroducing shoegaze to a new indie-rock audience.

**Serena-Maneesh — Drain Cosmetics (Serena-Maneesh, 2005)**
A towering 'noise-gaze' statement: layers of distortion and reverb create a monochrome wall while the rhythm section drives it forward. It's crucial for the mid-2000s European revival that embraced maximal volume without leaving shoegaze's textural core.

**Boris — Farewell (Pink, 2005)**
Boris prove shoegaze can be achieved through sheer texture and sustain: guitars swell into luminous noise while the vocal stays soft and distant. Despite the band's broader catalog, this track is a pure shoegaze moment that influenced many heavier modern acts.

**Engineers — Home (Engineers, 2005)**
Engineers' signature blend of enveloping guitar wash and melancholic melody exemplifies mid-2000s shoegaze's polished, cinematic direction. It's a key UK revival track that stays firmly within shoegaze rather than drifting into dream-pop minimalism.

**Airiel — In Your Room (Winks & Kisses: Melted, 2005)**
A fan-favorite in the modern canon: shimmering guitars and distant vocals create an immersive, 'endless summer' haze. Airiel's approach helped define the melodic, high-sheen revival sound.

**Asobi Seksu — Thursday (Citrus, 2006)**
Asobi Seksu pair Japanese/US indie-pop sensibility with unmistakably shoegaze guitar density—chorus and reverb turn riffs into vapor. The track became a gateway for 2000s listeners discovering shoegaze through contemporary bands.

**Fleeting Joys — Go and Come Back (Despondent Transponder, 2006)**
A loud, Loveless-indebted rush where distortion forms a continuous blanket over bright hooks. It's essential for mapping how the 90s sound was reanimated in the 2000s American underground.

**Silversun Pickups — Lazy Eye (Carnavas, 2006)**
A mainstream-adjacent track whose core is still shoegaze logic: layered, effects-heavy guitars dominate and the mix privileges texture over vocal clarity. It helped reintroduce shoegaze sonics to a broader 2000s alternative audience.

**A Sunny Day in Glasgow — A Mundane and Meaningful Fate (Scribble Mural Comic Journal, 2007)**
A Sunny Day in Glasgow merge shimmering guitar wash with swirling motion and distant vocals, landing squarely in revival-era shoegaze rather than pure dream-pop. This track is pivotal for the late-2000s wave that rebuilt shoegaze's audience.

**LSD and the Search for God — Starting Over (LSD and the Search for God (EP), 2007)**
A modern classic that revives the early-90s wall-of-guitars sound with striking fidelity and emotional punch. Its balance of hook and haze helped catalyze the mid-2000s 'nu-gaze' resurgence.

**A Shoreline Dream — NeverChanger (Recollections of Memory, 2007)**
A Shoreline Dream craft widescreen shoegaze with layered guitars that bloom into sustained haze and vocals set back in the mix. It's a key document of the mid-2000s American circuit keeping shoegaze alive between waves.

**The High Violets — Sun Baby (Cinema, 2008)**
A slow-burning shoegaze highlight that emphasizes atmosphere and sustained guitar bloom without tipping into slowcore. Its understated intensity made it a cult reference for later bedroom and DIY shoegaze acts.

**Tears Run Rings — Always, Sometimes, Seldom, Never (Always, Sometimes, Seldom, Never, 2009)**
Tears Run Rings exemplify shoegaze's 'quiet intensity': slow, enveloping guitar layers and intimate vocals submerged in reverb. The track is often cited in revival circles for its pure, unforced adherence to classic shoegaze atmosphere.

**The Depreciation Guild — Dream About Me (In Her Gentle Jaws, 2009)**
Chiptune-tinged shoegaze that still centers the classic grammar: wide, reverbed guitars and vocals tucked inside the mix. The track is important for showing how shoegaze could absorb new timbres without becoming indietronica proper.

**A Place to Bury Strangers — In Your Heart (Exploding Head, 2009)**
An extreme, feedback-heavy endpoint of shoegaze's noise potential—guitars roar and churn like machinery, yet the song remains hypnotically structured. It helped set the template for the harsher, more abrasive wing of modern shoegaze.

**The Black Ryder — All That We Are (Buy the Ticket, Take the Ride, 2009)**
The Black Ryder fuse classic shoegaze guitar mass with a darker, swaggering rock pulse while keeping the voice submerged. The track captures late-2000s shoegaze's turn toward bigger, more cinematic heaviness.

## V. 2010s revival & diversification (2010–2019)

**The Daysleepers — Drowned in a Sea of Sound (Drowned in a Sea of Sound, 2010)**
A revival-era shoegaze statement built on thick, chiming guitar haze and vocals integrated into the texture. It represents the long-running scene approach where craft and atmosphere matter more than trend cycles.

**Panda Riot — Northern Automatic Music (Northern Automatic Music, 2010)**
Panda Riot helped define the early-2010s US revival with shimmering guitars, soft vocals, and a steady, immersive drive. This track captures the genre's core sensation—melody dissolved into glow—without drifting into adjacent indietronica.

**Air Formation — Daylight Storms (Daylight Storms, 2010)**
Air Formation's sound is pure shoegaze: bright, multi-tracked guitars dissolve into a single shimmering field while vocals remain soft and distant. The track exemplifies the 2010-era UK revival's commitment to classic texture and atmosphere.

**For Tracy Hyde — Underwater Girl (he(r)art, 2011)**
An elegant, melody-driven shoegaze track where bright hooks are softened by layered effects and distant vocals. It's influential for mapping a distinctly Japanese take that stays firmly shoegaze rather than drifting into pure dream-pop.

**Plastic Girl in Closet — In May (In May, 2011)**
A modern shoegaze classic built from glossy, chorus-drenched guitars and softly buried vocals. Its clean-but-hazy production became a reference point for many later East Asian 'gaze' acts.

**Tokyo Shoegazer — Bright (Crystallize, 2011)**
A cornerstone of the Japanese shoegaze boom: thick, shimmering guitar layers and vocals blended into the overall haze. It demonstrates how the classic early-90s palette was reinterpreted with modern precision and scale.

**The Joy Formidable — Whirring (The Big Roar, 2011)**
A stadium-sized shoegaze surge where repeating guitar figures and reverb create a continuous, enveloping field. The track is important for showing how shoegaze dynamics can scale up while keeping the haze central.

**Pia Fraus — In Solarium (In Solarium, 2011)**
Pia Fraus deliver classic shoegaze shimmer with a bright, airy wall of guitars and vocals blended into the glow. The track is emblematic of how shoegaze thrived in continental Europe as a continuous scene rather than a single historical moment.

**The Pains of Being Pure at Heart — Belong (Belong, 2011)**
On this track the band fully embraces shoegaze's wall-of-guitars—thick distortion and chorus swallow the vocal into the mix. It's important as an early-2010s moment when shoegaze sonics re-entered indie's mainstream conversation.

**Ringo Deathstarr — Imagine Hearts (Mauve, 2012)**
A modern standard: bright hooks submerged in thick fuzz and chorus, with vocals mixed as part of the texture. Ringo Deathstarr helped make shoegaze a living, touring genre again rather than a purely historical scene.

**Beliefs — Ghost Beach (Beliefs, 2013)**
Beliefs revive classic shoegaze shimmer with modern punch—guitars bloom into dense reverb while the vocal stays textural. It's a strong example of the 2010s North American wave that treated shoegaze as present tense.

**my dead girlfriend — Ixtab (hades (The Nine Stages of Change at the Deceased Remains), 2013)**
A modern shoegaze epic that leans into scale and emotional drift—guitars form a dense, luminous canopy and the voice sits deep within it. my dead girlfriend are central to the 2010s East Asian interpretation of shoegaze as immersive autobiography.

**No Joy — E (Wait to Pleasure, 2013)**
No Joy combine classic shoegaze guitar blur with sharper rhythmic attack, keeping the voice recessed and the sound immersive. The track is emblematic of the 2010s wave that reasserted shoegaze's intensity and volume.

**Kinoko Teikoku — Uzu ni Naru (Uzu ni Naru, 2013)**
Kinoko Teikoku's shoegaze hinges on luminous guitar fog and restrained, intimate vocals that never rise above the texture. The track is key for the 2010s Japanese scene's blend of immediacy and immersive sound.

**The Fauns — This Is Love (The Fauns, 2013)**
A polished, modern shoegaze single built on thick guitar layers and a chorus that arrives as a wash of sound rather than a clean hook. The Fauns were key in reintroducing shoegaze aesthetics to a wider indie audience in the 2010s.

**The Stargazer Lilies — Living with a Stranger (Aurelia, 2014)**
A lush, romantic revival track with classic chorus-drenched guitars and vocals tucked into the haze. It's a go-to example of how 2010s bands restored shoegaze's 'dreamy-but-loud' balance.

**Whirr — Mellow (Sway, 2014)**
A defining 'heavy shoegaze' cut: dense, low-end distortion supports a simple, yearning melodic core. It's influential for how it modernized the wall-of-sound aesthetic without sliding into metal or grunge dominance.

**Cheatahs — The Swan (Cheatahs, 2014)**
A concise example of UK revival shoegaze: bright, layered guitars and sighing vocals cohere into a perfectly blurred chorus. Cheatahs show how the form can be renewed with discipline rather than excess.

**Nothing — Bent Nail (Guilty of Everything, 2014)**
Nothing translate shoegaze's haze into a bruised, riff-forward language while preserving the genre's soft vocal placement and enveloping effects. The track is a key reference for the gritty, US-leaning 2010s branch of shoegaze.

**Gleemer — Gauze (Gauze, 2015)**
A lean, modern shoegaze track that marries emo-leaning melody with dense, reverbed guitar layers and recessed vocals. It's influential for the 2010s DIY wave that kept shoegaze loud and intimate at the same time.

**Pinkshinyultrablast — Kiddy Pool Dreams (Everything Else Matters, 2015)**
A high-energy, modern update of the UK template—sparkling guitars, buried vocals, and a propulsive beat. Pinkshinyultrablast became central to the global 'nu-gaze' circuit while staying authentically shoegaze in texture.

**Tamaryn — Love Fade (Cranekiss, 2015)**
A modern shoegaze-pop high point: guitars shimmer and smear into a dense halo while the vocal stays soft and embedded. Tamaryn's work helped connect classic shoegaze texture to contemporary, hook-forward songwriting.

**Westkust — Swirl (Westkust, 2015)**
Swedish shoegaze with sharp melodic drive: jangling figures are submerged in chorus and reverb, and the vocal blends into the haze. Westkust represent the mid-2010s European scene that modernized shoegaze without abandoning its core sonic blur.

**DIIV — Dopamine (Is the Is Are, 2016)**
DIIV reframe shoegaze as driving, contemporary indie rock while keeping guitars awash in reverb and vocals recessed. It's influential for bringing shoegaze sonics back into mainstream-adjacent alternative circles.

**Miserable — Uncontrollable (Uncontrollable, 2016)**
A modern shoegaze essential where intimate vocals are swallowed by thick, slow-turning guitar haze. Miserable's songwriting distills the genre's 'blurred confession' aesthetic into a contemporary, emotionally direct form.

**Narrow Head — Ashtray (Satisfaction, 2016)**
A prime example of riff-driven modern shoegaze: the guitars hit hard, but the mix remains foggy and immersive with vocals tucked beneath distortion. Narrow Head helped popularize the heavier US strain while keeping shoegaze central.

**Life on Venus — Away From the City (Away From the City, 2016)**
A modern Eastern European shoegaze highlight: wide, chorus-soaked guitars and a steady motorik pulse, with vocals submerged into the texture. It shows how the genre's core sound travels cleanly across scenes while staying recognizably shoegaze.

**Cloakroom — Big World (Time Well, 2017)**
A heavy, slow-moving shoegaze statement where distortion forms an enveloping blanket and the vocal sits deep inside the mix. Cloakroom exemplify the 2010s tendency toward thicker low-end while remaining clearly 'gaze' rather than doom-metal.

**Hundredth — Neurotic (RARE, 2017)**
A pivotal 'genre-shift' track: synthy shimmer and reverbed guitars create a sleek, modern shoegaze haze around a pop-structured core. It helped introduce shoegaze sonics to listeners coming from hardcore and alternative scenes.

**Seapool — Kiss the Element (Kiss the Element, 2017)**
A sleek Japanese shoegaze cut where layered guitars shimmer and blur into a single wide field, with vocals mixed as texture. Seapool represent the 2010s 'clean-shoegaze' approach that keeps the haze while sharpening the songwriting.

**Slow Crush — Aurora (Aurora, 2018)**
A contemporary shoegaze anthem built on massive, swirling guitar layers and a soaring, half-submerged vocal hook. Slow Crush represent the European revival's ability to sound huge while staying faithful to classic shoegaze grammar.

**Yuragi — nightlife (nightlife, 2018)**
Yuragi's shoegaze is built from gently cascading guitars and a soft vocal presence that never breaks the spell of reverb. The track captures the contemporary Japanese scene's emphasis on motion and glow within classic shoegaze boundaries.

**Deafcult — Auras (Auras, 2019)**
A muscular, melodic form of modern shoegaze—wide guitars and forward motion, with vocals integrated into the overall blur. Deafcult are key to the late-2010s 'anthemic' strain that privileges scale without losing haze.

## VI. 2020s global wave (2020–2021)

**Deserta — Black Aura My Sun (Black Aura My Sun, 2020)**
A modern shoegaze track that embraces scale: guitars stack into a luminous wall while the vocal remains a ghost within it. Deserta's approach typifies 2020s shoegaze—high-definition haze with classic genre DNA.

**bdrmm — Happy (Bedroom, 2020)**
A UK 2020s standout with bright, layered guitars and vocals set deep inside the mix—classic shoegaze traits in a modern, tight arrangement. It signals shoegaze's renewed presence in contemporary indie rock without becoming 'dream-pop' softness.

**Launder — Powder (Pink Cloud, 2020)**
A cleanly produced, melody-first shoegaze track whose guitars still dissolve into chorus and reverb at the edges. It's representative of the 2020s wave that favors clarity of song while keeping the genre's signature haze.

**Blankenberge — Radiogaze (Radiogaze, 2020)**
A modern European shoegaze track that leans hard into the genre's signature blur—thick guitars, softened edges, and vocals woven into the wash. Blankenberge represent the 2020s international scene where shoegaze thrives as a global, internet-era language.

**julie — flutter (flutter (Single), 2020)**
A defining track of the young US 'grungegaze' moment: fuzzy guitars and feedback dominate, with vocals treated as texture rather than lead. It shows shoegaze's vocabulary thriving in a new generation's loud, DIY context.

**Parannoul — Beautiful World (To See the Next Part of the Dream, 2021)**
A modern classic that reconnects shoegaze's wall-of-sound to diary-like songwriting and emotional immediacy. The track's layered guitars and washed vocals demonstrate the genre's ability to expand globally while staying sonically authentic.

**Kraus — Bum (View No Country, 2021)**
Kraus epitomize modern 'mega-shoegaze': thick, enveloping guitars form a continuous cloud around a sturdy song core. The track is notable for sustaining shoegaze's intensity in a contemporary, high-definition production style.
