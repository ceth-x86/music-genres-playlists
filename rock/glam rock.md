# Glam Rock

## Ignition: the first wave (1971–1973)

**Slade — Coz I Luv You (Non-album single, 1971)**
Slade's misspelled singalong aesthetic in full bloom: big chorus, thick drums, and working‑class bravado. A cornerstone of glam's chart‑dominant stomp.

**T. Rex — Get It On (Bang a Gong) (Electric Warrior, 1971)**
A definitive Bolan-era glam single: chunky boogie riff, handclaps, and sly sensuality. It set the template for UK glam's 'stomp + hook' economy.

**Sweet — Block Buster! (Non-album single, 1972)**
A perfect example of Chinn/Chapman-era glam: stomping beat, stacked vocals, and punchline riffs. It's pure 'glitter-pop' propulsion.

**Gary Glitter — Rock and Roll (Part 2) (Glitter, 1972)**
The archetypal 'glitter rock' thump: chanting, simple riff cells, and maximum arena-friendly impact. It distills glam to rhythm, hook, and spectacle.

**Elton John — Rocket Man (Honky Château, 1972)**
Elton's glam-adjacent classic: cinematic melancholy wrapped in pop grandeur and flamboyant persona. It shows how glam aesthetics could inhabit singer‑songwriter craft.

**Alice Cooper — School's Out (School's Out, 1972)**
Shock-glam's signature: cartoon menace, crowd-chant hooks, and stage-friendly drama. It shows glam's embrace of persona and spectacle as musical structure.

**Chicory Tip — Son of My Father (Non-album single, 1972)**
A bubblegum-sci‑fi glam hybrid: synthetic textures, simple melody, and showbiz sheen. It captures glam's taste for novelty and futurist sparkle.

**David Bowie — Starman (The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars, 1972)**
Textbook 'high glam': sci‑fi persona, bright chorus lift, and radio-ready guitar pop. It crystallized glam's mix of spectacle and pop craft.

**Roxy Music — Virginia Plain (Non-album single, 1972)**
Roxy's sleek, arty strain of glam: angular groove, glamorous pose, and modernist sonics. It shows how glam could be sophisticated without losing the glitter.

**Lou Reed — Walk on the Wild Side (Transformer, 1972)**
Glam's downtown mirror: cool narration, doo‑doo backing vocals, and a knowing, decadent gaze. It broadened glam's subject matter without abandoning pop directness.

**Mott the Hoople — All the Young Dudes (All the Young Dudes, 1972)**
The anthem that made glam feel communal and street-level, not just theatrical. Big chords and singalong swagger turned outsider identity into a rallying cry.

**Blackfoot Sue — Standing in the Road (Non-album single, 1972)**
A punchy, riff-led single that sits squarely in early UK glam's boot-stomp pocket. It's 'street glam'—tight, hooky, and built for TV performance.

**Vodka Collins — Automatic Pilot (Tokyo – New York, 1973)**
A Tokyo-bred glam rocker with tight boogie drive and futurist pose. It's key evidence that glam's classic toolkit (riff, hook, persona) globalized fast.

**Suzi Quatro — Can the Can (Non-album single, 1973)**
Quatro fuses leather‑clad attitude with a tight pop-rock chassis. The relentless riff and punchy vocal make it a definitive female-fronted glam statement.

**Donovan — Cosmic Wheels (Cosmic Wheels, 1973)**
A brief but telling glam flirtation: cosmic imagery, glossy production, and theatrical vocal turns. It shows how glam's aesthetics spilled into established 60s auteurs.

**Barry Blue — Dancing (on a Saturday Night) (Hot Shots, 1973)**
A glitter-pop classic: four-on-the-floor pulse, bright chorus, and cheeky erotic charge. It's glam's danceable pop heart in pure form.

**Alvin Stardust — My Coo Ca Choo (Non-album single, 1973)**
Rock'n'roll revival filtered through glam: swaggering vocal, simple changes, and irresistible wink. Stardust embodies glam's 'character' as the main instrument.

**New York Dolls — Personality Crisis (New York Dolls, 1973)**
American glam's scruffier twin: Stonesy riffs, frantic vocal, and trashy elegance. It's a blueprint for glam's proto‑punk edge.

**Wizzard — See My Baby Jive (Non-album single, 1973)**
Roy Wood turns glam into maximalist pop carnival—brass, bounce, and wall-to-wall exuberance. It captures glam's love of retro pastiche with modern punch.

**Secos & Molhados — Sangue Latino (Secos & Molhados, 1973)**
Brazilian glam's landmark: theatrical delivery and stylized arrangement fused to pop immediacy. It demonstrates glam's adaptability to local scenes and languages.

**The Sensational Alex Harvey Band — The Faith Healer (Next, 1973)**
A theatrical, persona-driven performance that turns a riff into a dramatic monologue. It captures glam's taste for character and cabaret-tinged intensity.

**Jobriath — I'm a Man (Jobriath, 1973)**
A key document of queer glam's theatrical ambition: oversized melody, camp drama, and rock heft. It's glam as self-mythology, performed at full volume.

**Silverhead — 16 and Savage (16 and Savage, 1973)**
A glam-hard rocker with swaggering riffs and an androgynous stage sensibility. It's a key example of glam's overlap with heavier rock while keeping the glitter pose.

**Brownsville Station — Smokin' in the Boys Room (School Punks, 1973)**
US teen-glam with a nasty wink: crunchy riff, chant chorus, and classroom rebellion. It channels glam's love of youthful theater into hard-edged pop.

**Space Waltz — Out on the Streets (Space Waltz, 1974)**
New Zealand glam with a punchy, streetwise hook and stomp-ready rhythm. It's a reminder that glam's core tools traveled fast across scenes.

**Kiss — Strutter (Kiss, 1974)**
Early Kiss carries glam's theatrical DNA—riff-centric, chantable, and built for stage persona. It marks glam's pathway into bigger, flashier rock spectacle.

## Peak charts & proliferation (1974–1976)

**Hollywood Brats — Dying to See You (Non-album track (recorded 1973), 1973)**
Cult 'proto-glam' with snarling guitars and streetwise attitude—cheap, fast, and stylish. It's a key missing link between glam's pose and punk's bite.

**The Glitter Band — Angel Face (Non-album single, 1974)**
A glitter-rock staple with a lean, chugging beat and chorus built for chanting back. It represents glam's hit-factory side beyond the marquee stars.

**Skyhooks — Horror Movie (Living in the 70's, 1974)**
A witty, riff-led glam-rock hit with big choruses and cheeky social commentary. It embodies Australia's brash, populist glam strain.

**Queen — Killer Queen (Sheer Heart Attack, 1974)**
A jewel-box glam single: vaudevillian wit, stacked harmonies, and sleek guitar lines. It connects glam's theatricality to virtuoso pop-rock writing.

**Pilot — Magic (From the Album of the Same Name, 1974)**
A sleek, melodic hit with glam-era brightness and harmonic lift. It's glam's pop-craft side—less grit, more shine and chorus.

**Be-Bop Deluxe — Maid in Heaven (Axe Victim, 1974)**
Glam with art-rock finesse: melodic guitar lines, smart changes, and dandy attitude. It's evidence that glam's surface could house real compositional ambition.

**The Rubettes — Sugar Baby Love (The Rubettes, 1974)**
Doo‑wop revival filtered through glam's polish—falsetto hooks and photogenic sweetness. It maps glam's overlap with 50s pastiche and teen-pop appeal.

**Bay City Rollers — Shang-a-Lang (Rollin', 1974)**
Tartan teen-glam at its catchiest: singalong chorus, handclaps, and simple, bright guitar pop. It demonstrates glam's capacity for mass youth culture.

**Sparks — This Town Ain't Big Enough for Both of Us (Kimono My House, 1974)**
Operatic melodrama meets glam crunch—hyper-articulate vocals, sudden dynamic shifts, and high camp. It proves glam could be weird and still hit hard.

**Mud — Tiger Feet (Mud Rock, 1974)**
A prime example of 'glam stomp' aimed at the dancefloor: tight groove, chantable hook, and crisp riffs. It shows glam's pop efficiency at peak commercial power.

**Bryan Ferry — The 'In' Crowd (Another Time, Another Place, 1974)**
A suave, camp cover that turns social pose into pure musical attitude. Ferry's phrasing and sheen embody glam's cultivated surface and cool.

**Kenny — The Bump (Non-album single, 1974)**
Pure dancefloor glam: novelty choreography, bright chorus, and a stomping backbeat. It shows how glam's spectacle translated into communal pop ritual.

**David Essex — Gonna Make You a Star (Non-album single, 1974)**
Essex channels glam's showbiz fantasy directly into the lyric, backed by stomping pop-rock. It's glam as aspiration: spotlight, stage, and instant hook.

**Paper Lace — The Night Chicago Died (Paper Lace, 1974)**
Narrative pop-rock dressed in glam-era gloss: dramatic storytelling, big chorus, and radio-ready production. It shows glam's wider chart ecosystem beyond 'glitter' tropes.

**Brian Eno — Baby's on Fire (Here Come the Warm Jets, 1974)**
Glam reimagined as avant-pop: ecstatic noise-guitar, absurdist imagery, and studio-as-stage. It's crucial for understanding glam's experimental possibilities.

**Mick Ronson — Slaughter on 10th Avenue (Slaughter on 10th Avenue, 1974)**
Ronson translates glam guitar heroics into a cinematic, orchestrated rock piece. It highlights the 'sideman as star' dimension of the glam moment.

**Heavy Metal Kids — Delirious (Heavy Metal Kids, 1974)**
Scrappy, riffy 'junkshop glam' with proto‑punk energy and shout-along attitude. It represents the grittier club-level side of the genre.

**Jet — My River (Non-album single, 1975)**
A glam-rock deep cut with a taut riff and stomping backbeat geared for TV and clubs. It represents the era's dense ecosystem of singles-built glam acts.

**Hello — New York Groove (Keeps Us Off the Streets, 1975)**
A glam-ready power-pop stomp with a big, bright chorus and flashy guitar hooks. Its clean swagger became a transatlantic glam standard.

**Ian Hunter — Once Bitten, Twice Shy (Ian Hunter, 1975)**
Post-Mott glam swagger: ringing guitars, streetwise lyrics, and a big, knowing chorus. It captures the genre's move from 'high glam' to barroom bravado.

**Arrows — I Love Rock 'n' Roll (Non-album single, 1975)**
Glam's chant-and-riff logic in three minutes: a sticky chorus built for mass participation. It also shows glam's direct line into later hard-edged pop-rock.

**Steve Harley & Cockney Rebel — Make Me Smile (Come Up and See Me) (The Best Years of Our Lives, 1975)**
Theatrical phrasing and sly lyrical bite over a crisp glam-pop arrangement. It's 'stagecraft in the grooves,' balancing camp and classic songwriting.

**Hush — Boney Maronie (Non-album single, 1975)**
A 50s cover turned into glam stomper: handclaps, punchy guitars, and high-energy delivery. It shows how glam reanimated rock'n'roll as spectacle.

**Angel — Tower (Angel, 1975)**
A flamboyant, arena-sized take on glam's grandiosity—stacked vocals and heroic riffing. It reflects US 'angelic' glam as a distinct, polished offshoot.

**Ney Matogrosso — Bandido Corazón (Bandido, 1976)**
A flamboyant solo statement that carries glam's gender-bending spectacle into MPB-inflected rock theatre. It's persona-first performance, with the voice and staging doing as much work as the riff.

**Doctors of Madness — Mainline (Late Night Movies, All Night Brainstorms, 1976)**
Art-glam with nervous energy: sharp hooks, theatrical vocals, and an urban edge. It foreshadows post-punk while remaining rooted in glam's stylized drama.

**The Tubes — White Punks on Dope (Young and Rich, 1976)**
Satirical glam-theatre: character work, big riffs, and showband excess. It captures glam's capacity for self-parody without losing power.

## Afterglow & new directions (1977–1982)

**Metro — Criminal World (Metro, 1977)**
A sleek, decadent slice of late glam with art-pop sharpness and nocturnal mood. It's glam turning towards the coming new wave while retaining style-first swagger.

**Magnus Uggla — Varning på stan (Varning på stan, 1977)**
Swedish glam-pop with sneering attitude and bright, hook-forward writing. It shows glam's late-70s persistence in continental pop-rock.

**Nick Gilder — Hot Child in the City (City Nights, 1978)**
Late-70s glam's radio peak: glossy guitars, stacked hooks, and voyeuristic teen-myth framing. It's glam sheen transitioning into power-pop and AOR.

**Japan — Adolescent Sex (Adolescent Sex, 1978)**
Early Japan lean into sleazy, stylized glam rock before their later new‑romantic turn. The groove-forward riffing and pose make it a late echo of classic glam.

**Girl — Hollywood Tease (Sheer Greed, 1980)**
Sleazy, hook-forward glam rock with glittered guitar bite and boulevard attitude. It connects late-70s glam directly to 80s rock without becoming glam metal caricature.

**Hanoi Rocks — Tragedy (Bangkok Shocks, Saigon Shakes, Hanoi Rocks, 1981)**
Punked-up glam swagger: ragged riffs, sneering melody, and street fashion attitude. A crucial bridge from classic glam into later sleaze and Scandinavian revival scenes.

**Zolar X — Timeless (Zolar X, 1982)**
Sci-fi glam taken literally: space-costume concept, crunchy riffs, and cult theatrics. It's an underground continuation of glam's outer-space imagination.

## Survival years (1983–1999)

**Specimen — Kiss Kiss Bang Bang (Batastrophe, 1983)**
Goth-leaning glam: theatrical vocals, stomping rhythm, and nightlife spectacle. It shows glam's aesthetics feeding the darker club cultures of the early 80s.

**Doctor and the Medics — Spirit in the Sky (Laughing at the Pieces, 1986)**
A glam-tinged cover that turns gospel-rock into neon spectacle. It shows how glam theatrics survived into the 80s via camp and singalong bombast.

**Mother Love Bone — Stardog Champion (Apple, 1990)**
A flamboyant, hooky rocker with glam phrasing and larger-than-life vocals. It's a crucial document of glam's survival inside early-90s alternative rock.

**Manic Street Preachers — You Love Us (Generation Terrorists, 1992)**
A glam-punk charge driven by image-conscious swagger and chantable hooks. It carries glam's theatrical provocation into early-90s alternative rock politics.

**Suede — Metal Mickey (Suede, 1993)**
Britpop's glam nerve: trashy guitar crunch, sleazy lyric pose, and sharp melody. A key 90s revival that kept glam's androgynous swagger alive.

**D Generation — No Lunch (D Generation, 1994)**
Glam punk's cleanest hit formula: crunchy riff, bratty vocal, and swaggering chorus. It carries Dolls-style attitude into 90s guitar rock.

**Spacehog — In the Meantime (Resident Alien, 1995)**
Brit-pop-meets-glam: strutting bassline, spacey sheen, and a chorus built for big rooms. It helped reintroduce glam attitude to 90s alternative radio.

## Neo-glam revival (2000s–2020s)

**The Ark — It Takes a Fool to Remain Sane (We Are the Ark, 2000)**
A glossy, camp revival that keeps glam's big-chorus showmanship front and center. It's neo-glam pop-rock built for theatrical performance.

**King Adora — Smoulder (Vibrate You, 2001)**
UK neo-glam with a sharp, sleazy hook and fast, stomping pulse. It's glam's teenage-excess themes updated for the post-Britpop era.

**Robin Black and the Intergalactic Rock Stars — Some Of You Boys (Planet:Fame, 2002)**
A barroom-glam rave-up built for sweat and singalongs—raucous hooks and high theatricality. It's glam as live-band adrenaline rather than studio perfection.

**Negative — Still Alive (War of Love, 2003)**
A polished, dramatic take on glam's melodrama—big chorus, glossy guitars, and stylized angst. It shows glam's theatricality thriving in 2000s Northern European rock.

**The Darkness — I Believe in a Thing Called Love (Permission to Land, 2003)**
A knowing, riffy pastiche that still works as a real anthem—falsetto fireworks, stomping groove, huge chorus. It's the 2000s' most visible straight-ahead glam-rock revival hit.

**White Hot Odyssey — Subway Killer (White Hot Odyssey, 2004)**
A modern glam-rock cut with crunchy riffs and a showbiz-ready chorus. It reflects early-2000s US attempts to re-center glam's theatrical hard-pop formula.

**Supergroupies — Bouncin' (Supergroupies, 2005)**
Bright, smiling neo-glam pop with a bouncing beat and glittery chorus. It's a consciously 'fun' continuation of 70s glam's party DNA.

**Sohodolls — Stripper (Ribbed Music for the Numb Generation, 2007)**
Cabaret-glam pop with a noir theatrical edge and sharp chorus writing. It's glam as burlesque narrative, tuned for the 2000s club circuit.

**Hot Leg — Chickenfoot (Red Light Fever, 2009)**
Modern glam-rock irreverence: big riff, cheeky vocal, and flamboyant excess played for fun. It demonstrates glam's enduring love of humor and spectacle.

**Foxy Shazam — Unstoppable (Foxy Shazam, 2010)**
Piano-driven, theatrical rock with maximal choruses and showman delivery—glam's spirit in modern form. It's built for spectacle and ecstatic crowd response.

**Giuda — Racey Roller (Racey Roller, 2010)**
A deliberate 'bovver-glam' throwback—chanting, marching drums, and boogie riffs. It revives the Mud/Sweet side of glam with loving precision.

**Smith Westerns — Weekend (Dye It Blonde, 2011)**
Jangle-and-glitter pop-rock with a dandy stance and big melodic payoff. It's glam in attitude and sheen, framed through indie production.

**Prima Donna — Doll Face Baby (Bless This Mess, 2012)**
Garagey glam-rock with a modern snarl, keeping the swagger and costume-party spirit intact. It's glam filtered through punky club energy rather than arena scale.

**The Medullary Paralysis — Fashion Slave (You Need Leaders, 2012)**
Industrial-tinged 'not-so-industrial' glam: synthy bite, latex-pageantry visuals, and chorus-forward writing. It demonstrates glam's adaptability to modern alternative textures.

**Diane Coffee — Spring Breathes (My Friend Fish, 2013)**
Soft-focus neo-glam: falsetto, bright synthy textures, and theatrical self-presentation. It shows glam sensibility migrating into indie-pop while keeping the pose.

**The Struts — Could Have Been Me (Everybody Wants, 2014)**
Neo-glam with arena ambition: stomping beat, big chorus, and classic-rock strut. It's modern glam rock that prioritizes performance and hook craft.

**Semi Precious Weapons — Magnetic Baby (We Love You, 2014)**
High-camp dance-rock with glam strut and pop immediacy. It's a club-friendly update to glam's 'look + hook' philosophy.

**Kyle Craft — Eye of a Hurricane (Dolls of Highland, 2016)**
Southern-gothic storytelling delivered with glam-era flamboyance and vocal drama. It demonstrates how glam's theatricality can thrive in contemporary singer-led rock.

**The Lemon Twigs — As Long As We're Together (Do Hollywood, 2016)**
Modern 'high glam' songwriting: theatrical changes, bright harmonies, and classic-pop melodrama. It's glam as craft and charm rather than pure stomp.

**Starbenders — Powder (Heavy Petting, 2016)**
A glitter-and-grit mix of pop hooks and punky bite that still reads glam in posture and chorus. It's contemporary glam rock with an elastic, nightlife-ready sound.

**Cait Brennan — Shake Away (Third, 2017)**
A modern 'glam craft' cut—bright melodic writing, theatrical lift, and pop-hall sensibility. It shows glam rock as a songwriting tradition, not just a 70s costume.

**Palaye Royale — Lonely (The Bastards, 2020)**
A stylized, dramatic anthem that foregrounds glam's fashion-meets-rock persona. Big hooks and emotional grandstanding keep it firmly in revival territory.

**HMLTD — To the Door (West of Eden, 2020)**
A modern, stylized rock track with exaggerated vocal persona and scene-kid theatrics. It's glam as performance-art energy in a contemporary post-punk frame.

**Måneskin — Zitti e buoni (Teatro d'ira: Vol. I, 2021)**
A high-energy glam revival moment: riff-driven propulsion and runway-ready attitude. It shows glam's visual-musical coupling still translating to mass audiences.

**Buzzard Buzzard Buzzard — Double Denim Hop (Backhand Deals, 2022)**
A contemporary stomp-glam pastiche that foregrounds riffs, gang vocals, and playful attitude. It's proof the 'TOTP' glam template still works in indie rock.
